Probability basics and axioms

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~32 min · Requires descriptive statistics only
📍 Scene — a university statistics classroom, first probability lesson
EM
Emma

We finished descriptive statistics, but probability feels like a different subject. Are we still learning statistics?

MR
Mr. R

Very much so. Descriptive statistics tells us what happened in the data we observed. Probability helps us reason about what could have happened under uncertainty.

OL
Oliver

What do you mean by “could have happened”? If I have my dataset, the numbers are already fixed.

MR
Mr. R

After you collect the dataset, yes, those numbers are fixed. But before collecting the sample, many possible samples could have been selected. Probability lets us describe that uncertainty.

Why probability enters statistics

Suppose you randomly select 50 patients and calculate their average blood pressure. That average describes your sample. But if you selected another 50 patients from the same population, the average would probably be slightly different.

That difference is not necessarily a mistake. It is sampling variation. Probability gives us a mathematical language for describing sampling variation.

JA
James

So probability is not just about dice, coins and cards?

MR
Mr. R

No. Dice and coins are useful teaching examples because their possible outcomes are clear. But the same logic applies to clinical trials, surveys, genetics, public health, machine learning, and risk prediction.

SO
Sophia

So probability is the theory behind uncertainty, and statistics uses it to make conclusions from data?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Probability moves from a known model to possible data. Statistics often moves from observed data back toward an unknown model or population.

Random experiments, outcomes, and sample spaces

The first idea is a random experiment. This is any process whose result is uncertain before it happens.

Emma

Then what is a sample space?

MR
Mr. R

The sample space is the set of all possible outcomes. For a die roll, it is the set containing 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6.

OL
Oliver

And an event is something like “getting an even number”?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. The event “even number” is the set containing 2, 4 and 6. In probability, an event is a subset of the sample space.

The three axioms

Probability looks intuitive at first, but it needs strict rules. These rules are called axioms. They make sure probability behaves consistently.

JA
James

Why do we need axioms? Can’t we just say probability is chance?

MR
Mr. R

“Chance” is a word. Axioms turn that word into a mathematical system. They tell us what probability values are allowed to do.

The three basic axioms are:

  • Non-negativity: probabilities cannot be negative.
  • Total probability: the probability of the whole sample space is 1.
  • Additivity: probabilities of non-overlapping events can be added.

Why overlap matters

Imagine a class where some students study statistics, some study economics, and some study both. If you add the probability of studying statistics and the probability of studying economics, students who study both are counted twice.

SO
Sophia

So that is why we subtract the overlap?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. For any two events A and B, the probability of A or B is the probability of A plus the probability of B minus the probability of both.

\[ P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B) \]

Probability is not certainty

If an event has probability 0.8, it does not mean it must happen in the next trial. It means that under the probability model, the event is expected to occur frequently in the long run.

EM
Emma

If a treatment has a 70% response probability, can one patient still fail to respond?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. Probability describes uncertainty before the outcome. It does not guarantee what happens to one individual.

OL
Oliver

So probability is about patterns across repeated situations, not perfect prediction of one case?

MR
Mr. R

That is a very important distinction. Statistics uses those repeated-pattern ideas to quantify uncertainty in real studies.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Probability is the mathematical language of uncertainty.
  • A random experiment has uncertain outcomes before it occurs.
  • The sample space contains all possible outcomes.
  • An event is a subset of the sample space.
  • The three probability axioms are non-negativity, total probability, and additivity.
  • The addition rule subtracts overlap because overlapping outcomes are counted twice.
  • Probability prepares the mathematical foundation for standard error, confidence intervals, and hypothesis testing.
Detailed theoretical notes

Probability basics and axioms

1. Why probability is needed in statistics

Statistics is concerned with learning from data under uncertainty. The uncertainty may come from sampling, random assignment, biological variability, measurement error, incomplete observation, or future outcomes that have not yet occurred.

Descriptive statistics summarises the data we have. Probability allows us to reason about the data we might have observed under a random process. This distinction is essential for inferential statistics.

Core idea

Probability describes uncertainty before observing the outcome. Statistical inference uses probability to quantify uncertainty after observing data.

2. Random experiment

A random experiment is a process whose outcome is uncertain before the process is carried out. The word “experiment” does not necessarily mean a laboratory experiment. It means a repeatable situation with uncertain outcome.

ExampleRandom experimentPossible outcomes
Coin tossToss one coinHead, tail
Die rollRoll one die1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6
Clinical responseObserve whether a patient respondsResponse, no response
Survey samplingSelect one person from a populationAny eligible person

3. Outcome, sample space, and event

An outcome is a single possible result of a random experiment. The sample space, usually denoted by \(S\), is the set of all possible outcomes. An event is a subset of the sample space.

\[ S=\{\text{all possible outcomes}\} \]
\[ A\subseteq S \]

The notation \(A\subseteq S\) means that every outcome in event \(A\) is also an outcome in the sample space \(S\).

Example: one die roll

\[ S=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\} \]

If \(A\) is the event “an even number appears”, then:

\[ A=\{2,4,6\} \]

If \(B\) is the event “a number greater than 4 appears”, then:

\[ B=\{5,6\} \]

4. Probability scale

A probability is a number between 0 and 1. A probability of 0 means the event is impossible under the model. A probability of 1 means the event is certain under the model.

\[ 0\leq P(A)\leq 1 \]

Probabilities can also be written as percentages. For example, \(P(A)=0.25\) is the same as saying the event has probability 25%.

5. The three probability axioms

The probability axioms are the mathematical rules that probability must obey. They are simple, but many important results follow from them.

Axiom 1: non-negativity

For any event \(A\), its probability cannot be negative.

\[ P(A)\geq 0 \]

This is necessary because probability represents uncertainty or long-run relative frequency. A negative probability has no meaningful interpretation.

Axiom 2: total probability of the sample space

The sample space \(S\) contains all possible outcomes. Therefore, the probability that some outcome in \(S\) occurs is 1.

\[ P(S)=1 \]

Axiom 3: additivity for disjoint events

If two events \(A\) and \(B\) cannot occur together, they are called disjoint or mutually exclusive. In that case:

\[ A\cap B=\varnothing \]

and:

\[ P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B) \]

The symbol \(A\cap B\) means “A and B”. The symbol \(A\cup B\) means “A or B”. The symbol \(\varnothing\) means the empty set.

6. Derivation of the complement rule

The complement of \(A\), written \(A^c\), is the event that \(A\) does not occur.

The event \(A\) and its complement \(A^c\) are disjoint:

\[ A\cap A^c=\varnothing \]

Together, they cover the whole sample space:

\[ A\cup A^c=S \]

Using additivity:

\[ P(A\cup A^c)=P(A)+P(A^c) \]

Since \(A\cup A^c=S\) and \(P(S)=1\):

\[ 1=P(A)+P(A^c) \]

Rearranging gives the complement rule:

\[ P(A^c)=1-P(A) \]
Complement rule

The probability that an event does not occur is one minus the probability that it does occur.

7. Derivation of the general addition rule

For two events \(A\) and \(B\), the event \(A\cup B\) means that \(A\) occurs, or \(B\) occurs, or both occur.

If we add \(P(A)\) and \(P(B)\), the overlap \(A\cap B\) is counted twice. Therefore, we subtract it once.

\[ P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B) \]

Why this formula is correct

Break \(A\cup B\) into three disjoint parts:

  • outcomes in \(A\) but not in \(B\),
  • outcomes in \(B\) but not in \(A\),
  • outcomes in both \(A\) and \(B\).

The formula \(P(A)+P(B)\) counts the third part twice. Subtracting \(P(A\cap B)\) corrects this double-counting.

8. Special case: mutually exclusive events

If \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive, then \(A\cap B=\varnothing\). Therefore:

\[ P(A\cap B)=0 \]

Substituting this into the general addition rule gives:

\[ P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B) \]

This is the simpler addition rule for disjoint events.

9. Equally likely outcomes

If all outcomes in the sample space are equally likely, the probability of event \(A\) is:

\[ P(A)=\frac{\text{number of outcomes in }A}{\text{number of outcomes in }S} \]

Using set notation, this can be written as:

\[ P(A)=\frac{|A|}{|S|} \]

For a fair die and \(A=\{2,4,6\}\):

\[ P(A)=\frac{|A|}{|S|}=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2} \]
Important caution

The formula \(P(A)=|A|/|S|\) is valid only when all outcomes are equally likely. It is not valid for biased samples, unequal-risk populations, or loaded dice.

10. Probability as long-run relative frequency

One interpretation of probability is long-run relative frequency. If an event has probability \(0.30\), then in a very long sequence of repeated trials under the same conditions, the event should occur about 30% of the time.

This does not mean the event must occur exactly 30 times in 100 trials. Small samples can fluctuate.

11. Probability model versus real world

A probability model is a mathematical representation of uncertainty. For example, saying that a die is fair means:

\[ P(1)=P(2)=P(3)=P(4)=P(5)=P(6)=\frac{1}{6} \]

If the die is loaded, this model is wrong. The mathematics may still be internally correct, but the model would not describe reality well.

12. Common mistakes

  • Not defining the sample space: probability calculations become unclear if \(S\) is not defined.
  • Assuming equal likelihood: not all outcomes are equally likely in real data.
  • Forgetting overlap: when events overlap, use \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\).
  • Confusing “or” with “exclusive or”: in probability, \(A\cup B\) usually includes the possibility that both occur.
  • Expecting exact short-run behaviour: probability describes long-run tendencies, not guaranteed small-sample outcomes.
Notes summary
  • A probability model begins with a sample space.
  • An event is a subset of the sample space.
  • Probabilities obey non-negativity, total probability, and additivity.
  • The complement rule follows from the axioms.
  • The general addition rule corrects double-counting of overlap.
  • Equally likely counting works only when outcomes are genuinely equally likely.

Practice exercises

Probability basics and axioms

Try each exercise before revealing the answer. The focus is on clean definitions, correct formulas, and careful interpretation.

Exercise 1
Sample space and event
Easy+

A fair coin is tossed three times. Write the sample space and the event \(A\) that exactly two heads occur.

Answer

The sample space is \(S=\{HHH,HHT,HTH,THH,HTT,THT,TTH,TTT\}\). The event exactly two heads is \(A=\{HHT,HTH,THH\}\).

Exercise 2
Complement rule
Easy+

If \(P(A)=0.76\), calculate \(P(A^c)\). Interpret the result.

Answer

Using \(P(A^c)=1-P(A)\), we get \(P(A^c)=1-0.76=0.24\). The probability that \(A\) does not occur is 0.24.

Exercise 3
General addition rule
Medium+

In a university cohort, \(P(S)=0.55\) for studying statistics, \(P(E)=0.40\) for studying economics, and \(P(S\cap E)=0.18\). Find \(P(S\cup E)\).

Answer

Use the addition rule: \(P(S\cup E)=P(S)+P(E)-P(S\cap E)=0.55+0.40-0.18=0.77\). The probability that a randomly selected student studies statistics or economics, or both, is 0.77.

Exercise 4
Mutually exclusive or not?
Medium+

For one die roll, let \(A=\{2,4,6\}\) and \(B=\{5,6\}\). Are \(A\) and \(B\) mutually exclusive? Explain.

Answer

No. \(A\cap B=\{6\}\), so the events overlap. Since they can occur together when the outcome is 6, they are not mutually exclusive.

Exercise 5
Counting probability
Medium+

One card is drawn from a standard 52-card deck. Find the probability of drawing a heart or a king.

Answer

There are 13 hearts and 4 kings. The king of hearts is counted in both groups, so subtract it once. The number of favourable outcomes is \(13+4-1=16\). Therefore, the probability is \(16/52=4/13\).

Exercise 6
Deriving the addition rule idea
Hard+

Explain in words why \(P(A)+P(B)\) overcounts \(P(A\cup B)\) when \(A\) and \(B\) overlap.

Answer

Outcomes in \(A\cap B\) belong to both \(A\) and \(B\). When we add \(P(A)\) and \(P(B)\), those overlapping outcomes are counted once inside \(P(A)\) and again inside \(P(B)\). Therefore, the overlap has been counted twice, so we subtract \(P(A\cap B)\) once.

Exercise 7
Model assumption
Hard+

A delivery company says each of four routes is equally likely for a random parcel. Later, data show that route 1 is used 70% of the time. Explain why the equally likely probability model is inappropriate.

Answer

The equally likely model would assign probability \(1/4\) to each route. If route 1 is actually used about 70% of the time, the real process is not consistent with equal probabilities. The counting formula \(P(A)=|A|/|S|\) would therefore be misleading.

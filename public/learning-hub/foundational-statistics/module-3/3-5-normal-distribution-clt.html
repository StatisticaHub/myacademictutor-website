Normal distribution and the Central Limit Theorem

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~45 min · Final probability foundation lesson
📍 Scene — probability classroom, bridge to inference
EM
Emma

We have studied probability, conditional probability, random variables, and discrete distributions. Now everyone says the normal distribution is the most important one. Why?

MR
Mr. R

The normal distribution is important for two reasons. First, many measured variables are roughly bell-shaped. Second, and more importantly, many sample averages become approximately normal even when the original data are not normal.

OL
Oliver

That second part sounds like the Central Limit Theorem?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. The Central Limit Theorem is the reason confidence intervals and many hypothesis tests work so widely.

The shape of the normal distribution

The normal distribution is a continuous probability distribution with a symmetric bell shape. It is centred at its mean \( \mu \), and its spread is controlled by its standard deviation \( \sigma \).

\[ X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2) \]

This notation means \(X\) follows a normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\).

JA
James

Why do we write \(\sigma^2\) instead of \(\sigma\)?

MR
Mr. R

Because the second parameter of the normal distribution is conventionally the variance. The standard deviation is \(\sigma\), but the variance is \(\sigma^2\).

Continuous probability means area

For continuous variables, probabilities are not assigned to single exact points. Instead, probabilities are areas under the curve.

\[ P(a\leq X\leq b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \]
SO
Sophia

So \(P(X=170)\) for height is not meaningful in the same way as \(P(X=2)\) for a die?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. For a continuous distribution, the probability of one exact point is zero. We calculate probabilities over intervals.

The standard normal distribution

The standard normal distribution has mean 0 and standard deviation 1.

\[ Z\sim N(0,1) \]

Any normal random variable can be standardised into a \(Z\)-score:

\[ Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \]
EM
Emma

So the z-score tells us how many standard deviations a value is from the mean?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. That connects this lesson back to Module 2.

The empirical rule

For a normal distribution, approximately 68% of values lie within one standard deviation of the mean, 95% lie within two standard deviations, and 99.7% lie within three standard deviations.

\[ P(\mu-\sigma\leq X\leq \mu+\sigma)\approx 0.68 \]
\[ P(\mu-2\sigma\leq X\leq \mu+2\sigma)\approx 0.95 \]
\[ P(\mu-3\sigma\leq X\leq \mu+3\sigma)\approx 0.997 \]

The Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem says that under suitable conditions, the sampling distribution of the sample mean becomes approximately normal as sample size increases.

\[ \bar{X}\approx N\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \]

Equivalently:

\[ \frac{\bar{X}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\approx N(0,1) \]
OL
Oliver

So the original data do not have to be normal?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. The theorem is about the sample mean, not necessarily about the raw observations.

JA
James

And the spread of sample means is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\)?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. That quantity is the standard error of the mean. It becomes central in Module 4.

Why this matters for inference

Confidence intervals and hypothesis tests often use normal or approximately normal sampling distributions. The Central Limit Theorem explains why sample means can be handled with normal-based methods even when individual observations are not perfectly normal.

SO
Sophia

So this lesson is the bridge from probability to statistical inference?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Module 4 begins with standard errors, confidence intervals, and hypothesis tests. The Central Limit Theorem is the probability engine underneath them.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • The normal distribution is continuous, symmetric and bell-shaped.
  • Normal probabilities are areas under the density curve.
  • The standard normal distribution has mean 0 and variance 1.
  • Z-scores convert normal variables to the standard normal scale.
  • The Central Limit Theorem concerns the sample mean, not the raw data.
  • The standard error of \(\bar{X}\) is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\).
  • The Central Limit Theorem prepares the foundation for inference.
Detailed theoretical notes

Normal distribution and the Central Limit Theorem

1. Continuous random variables

A continuous random variable can take values on an interval. Unlike a discrete random variable, it does not assign positive probability to single exact values. Probabilities are calculated over intervals.

\[ P(a\leq X\leq b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \]

The function \(f(x)\) is called a probability density function. It must satisfy:

\[ f(x)\geq 0 \]
\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx=1 \]

2. The normal distribution

A random variable \(X\) follows a normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) if its density is:

\[ f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2\right], \qquad -\infty

We write:

\[ X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2) \]

The mean \(\mu\) controls the centre. The standard deviation \(\sigma\) controls the spread. The variance is \(\sigma^2\).

3. Properties of the normal distribution

  • It is symmetric around \(\mu\).
  • Mean, median and mode are equal.
  • The total area under the curve is 1.
  • The tails extend indefinitely but approach zero.
  • The distribution is fully determined by \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\).

4. Standard normal distribution

The standard normal distribution is the normal distribution with mean 0 and variance 1.

\[ Z\sim N(0,1) \]

5. Standardisation

If \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\), then the standardised variable

\[ Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \]

has the standard normal distribution:

\[ Z\sim N(0,1) \]

Derivation of mean and variance after standardisation

Using linearity of expectation:

\[ E(Z)=E\left(\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right) =\frac{E(X)-\mu}{\sigma} =\frac{\mu-\mu}{\sigma}=0 \]

Using the variance rule \(\operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)\):

\[ \operatorname{Var}(Z)=\operatorname{Var}\left(\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right) =\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\operatorname{Var}(X) =\frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2}=1 \]

6. Normal interval probabilities

Because the normal distribution is continuous, probability is area. For example:

\[ P(X\leq x)=P\left(Z\leq \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right) \]

This allows all normal probability calculations to be converted to standard normal calculations.

7. Empirical rule

For normal distributions, approximate probability areas are:

IntervalApproximate probability
\(\mu\pm\sigma\)0.68
\(\mu\pm2\sigma\)0.95
\(\mu\pm3\sigma\)0.997

8. Sampling distribution of the sample mean

Let \(X_1,X_2,\ldots,X_n\) be independent observations from a population with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). The sample mean is:

\[ \bar{X}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i \]

Expectation of the sample mean

\[ E(\bar{X})= E\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}E(X_i) = \frac{1}{n}(n\mu)=\mu \]

Variance of the sample mean

If the observations are independent:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})= \operatorname{Var}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\operatorname{Var}(X_i) \]
\[ \operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})= \frac{1}{n^2}(n\sigma^2)=\frac{\sigma^2}{n} \]

Standard error of the sample mean

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})} = \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}{n}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

9. Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem states that if \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\) are independent and identically distributed with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2<\infty\), then for large \(n\):

\[ \bar{X}\approx N\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \]

Equivalently:

\[ \frac{\bar{X}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\approx N(0,1) \]

10. What the CLT does and does not say

Correct statementCommon wrong version
The sampling distribution of \(\bar{X}\) becomes approximately normal.The raw data become normal.
Approximation improves as \(n\) increases.Any sample size always works.
Finite variance is required in the classical version.No conditions are needed.
Independence matters.Dependence never matters.

11. Why the CLT matters

The CLT justifies normal approximations for sample means. This is the mathematical basis for many confidence intervals and hypothesis tests.

Bridge to Module 4

Module 4 uses the standard error and approximate normality of estimators to build confidence intervals and hypothesis tests.

12. Common mistakes

  • Thinking the CLT says data become normal: it says the sampling distribution of the mean becomes approximately normal.
  • Forgetting standard error: the spread of sample means is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), not \(\sigma\).
  • Ignoring dependence: strong dependence can invalidate simple CLT reasoning.
  • Using normal approximation blindly: severe skewness or heavy tails may require larger sample sizes.
  • Confusing probability distribution and sampling distribution: raw observations and sample means are different objects.
Notes summary
  • The normal distribution is a continuous bell-shaped distribution.
  • Probabilities for continuous variables are areas under the density.
  • Standardisation converts \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\) to \(Z\sim N(0,1)\).
  • The sample mean has expectation \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2/n\).
  • The standard error of the mean is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\).
  • The CLT explains why sample means are often approximately normal.

Practice exercises

Normal distribution and Central Limit Theorem

Exercise 1
Identify parameters
Easy+

If \(X\sim N(50,16)\), identify the mean, variance and standard deviation.

Answer

The mean is \(\mu=50\). The variance is \(\sigma^2=16\). The standard deviation is \(\sigma=4\).

Exercise 2
Z-score
Easy+

A variable has mean 100 and standard deviation 15. Calculate the z-score for \(x=130\).

Answer

\(z=(130-100)/15=30/15=2\). The value is 2 standard deviations above the mean.

Exercise 3
Empirical rule
Medium+

If \(X\sim N(70,10^2)\), approximately what percentage of observations lie between 50 and 90?

Answer

The interval 50 to 90 is \(70\pm20\), which is \(\mu\pm2\sigma\). By the empirical rule, about 95% of observations lie in this interval.

Exercise 4
Expectation of sample mean
Medium+

If a population has mean \(\mu=40\), what is \(E(\bar{X})\) for samples of size \(n=25\)?

Answer

\(E(\bar{X})=\mu=40\). The sample mean is centred at the population mean.

Exercise 5
Standard error
Medium+

If \(\sigma=12\) and \(n=36\), calculate \(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})\).

Answer

\(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\sigma/\sqrt{n}=12/\sqrt{36}=12/6=2\).

Exercise 6
Sampling distribution
Hard+

If \(X_i\) have mean 80 and variance 100, and \(n=25\), give the approximate distribution of \(\bar{X}\) using the CLT.

Answer

The mean of \(\bar{X}\) is 80. The variance is \(100/25=4\). Therefore, \(\bar{X}\approx N(80,4)\), assuming CLT conditions are reasonable.

Exercise 7
CLT misconception
Hard+

A student says, “The CLT says the original data become normal when \(n\) is large.” Explain the error.

Answer

The CLT concerns the sampling distribution of the sample mean, not the distribution of the original raw observations. The raw data can remain skewed while sample means become approximately normal.

Exercise 8
Sample size and precision
Hard+

If sample size increases from 25 to 100, what happens to the standard error of the mean?

Answer

The sample size is multiplied by 4. Since \(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), the standard error is divided by \(\sqrt{4}=2\). It is halved.

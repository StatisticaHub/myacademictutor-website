Conditional probability and Bayes' theorem

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~38 min · After probability axioms
📍 Scene — probability classroom, second lecture
EM
Emma

Last time probability felt manageable: sample spaces, events, complements and overlaps. But now I see this notation \(P(A|B)\). Why does the vertical line make everything harder?

MR
Mr. R

The vertical line simply means “given”. \(P(A|B)\) is the probability of \(A\), given that \(B\) has already occurred.

OL
Oliver

So it is still probability, but with extra information?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Conditional probability is probability after updating the information available to us.

The idea of restricting the sample space

Suppose a class has 100 students. Some study statistics, some receive a distinction, and some do both. If I ask, “What is the probability that a randomly selected student received a distinction?”, I look at all 100 students.

But if I ask, “What is the probability that a student received a distinction given that the student studies statistics?”, I no longer look at all 100 students. I restrict attention only to the students who study statistics.

JA
James

So the denominator changes?

MR
Mr. R

That is the heart of conditional probability. The condition becomes the new reference group.

SO
Sophia

So \(P(A|B)\) asks: among the outcomes where \(B\) happened, how many also have \(A\)?

MR
Mr. R

Perfect. That sentence is more important than the formula at first.

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)},\qquad P(B)>0 \]

Why \(P(A|B)\) is not the same as \(P(B|A)\)

This is one of the most common mistakes in probability. \(P(A|B)\) and \(P(B|A)\) usually answer different questions because they use different denominators.

EM
Emma

Can you give a clear example?

MR
Mr. R

Suppose 40 students study statistics, 25 receive a distinction, and 20 both study statistics and receive a distinction.

OL
Oliver

Then \(P(\text{distinction}|\text{statistics})\) uses 40 as the denominator?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. It is \(20/40=0.50\). But \(P(\text{statistics}|\text{distinction})\) uses 25 as the denominator, so it is \(20/25=0.80\).

JA
James

Same overlap, different denominator.

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Most conditional probability confusion is denominator confusion.

Multiplication rule

Conditional probability can be rearranged to give the multiplication rule. This rule helps calculate the probability that two events occur together.

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A|B)P(B) \]

Equally:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(B|A)P(A) \]
SO
Sophia

So the same intersection can be reached from either direction?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. That symmetry is what leads directly to Bayes' theorem.

Independence

Two events are independent if knowing that one has occurred does not change the probability of the other.

\[ P(A|B)=P(A) \]

If this is true, then:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B) \]
EM
Emma

Is independence the same as mutually exclusive?

MR
Mr. R

No. This is a serious misconception. Mutually exclusive means the events cannot occur together. Independent means one event does not change the probability of the other.

OL
Oliver

If two events are mutually exclusive and one happens, the other becomes impossible. So its probability changes.

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Mutually exclusive events with positive probabilities are not independent.

Bayes' theorem

Bayes' theorem is a way to reverse conditional probabilities. It is especially important when we know \(P(B|A)\) but want \(P(A|B)\).

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} \]

If \(A\) and \(A^c\) form two possible cases, then \(P(B)\) can be expanded as:

\[ P(B)=P(B|A)P(A)+P(B|A^c)P(A^c) \]

Medical testing example

Let \(D\) mean a person has a disease, and let \(+\) mean the test is positive. A test may have high sensitivity, but that does not mean a positive result guarantees disease.

JA
James

If a test is 95% sensitive, does a positive result mean a 95% chance of disease?

MR
Mr. R

No. Sensitivity is \(P(+|D)\). The patient usually wants \(P(D|+)\). Those are different probabilities.

SO
Sophia

So we need Bayes' theorem to move from test accuracy to probability of disease after a result.

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. And the disease prevalence matters a lot.

Suppose:

  • disease prevalence is 1%,
  • sensitivity is 95%,
  • specificity is 90%.

Then the false positive rate is 10%. Bayes' theorem gives:

\[ P(D|+)=\frac{P(+|D)P(D)}{P(+|D)P(D)+P(+|D^c)P(D^c)} \]

The calculation is:

\[ P(D|+)=\frac{0.95(0.01)}{0.95(0.01)+0.10(0.99)} \]
\[ P(D|+)=\frac{0.0095}{0.0095+0.099}=0.0876 \]

So the probability of disease after a positive result is about 8.8%, not 95%.

EM
Emma

That feels surprising. The test sounds good, but the positive result is not as strong as expected.

MR
Mr. R

Because the disease is rare. There are many more non-diseased people than diseased people. Even a small false positive rate can produce many false positives.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Conditional probability means probability after information is given.
  • The condition becomes the new reference group or denominator.
  • \(P(A|B)\) and \(P(B|A)\) usually answer different questions.
  • The multiplication rule follows directly from conditional probability.
  • Independence means knowing one event does not change the probability of the other.
  • Bayes' theorem reverses conditional probabilities.
  • In diagnostic testing, sensitivity is not the same as positive predictive value.
  • Prevalence strongly affects the interpretation of test results.
Detailed theoretical notes

Conditional probability and Bayes' theorem

1. Why conditional probability is needed

In real statistical reasoning, probabilities are rarely asked without context. We often want probabilities after receiving information: a test result, a symptom, a risk factor, a previous event, or a group membership.

Conditional probability formalises this idea. It tells us how to update the probability of an event when another event is known to have occurred.

Definition

Conditional probability is the probability of an event calculated under the condition that another event has already occurred.

2. Definition of conditional probability

Let \(A\) and \(B\) be events, with \(P(B)>0\). The conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\) is:

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \]

The denominator is \(P(B)\) because once we know \(B\) has occurred, the relevant sample space is reduced to \(B\).

3. Conditional probability using counts

If all outcomes are equally weighted or if we are working with counts in a finite dataset, then:

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{\text{number of outcomes in both }A\text{ and }B}{\text{number of outcomes in }B} \]

In set notation:

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{|A\cap B|}{|B|} \]

This count form helps students remember the meaning: among the \(B\) cases, what proportion are also \(A\)?

4. \(P(A|B)\) versus \(P(B|A)\)

The expressions \(P(A|B)\) and \(P(B|A)\) usually have different denominators:

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \]
\[ P(B|A)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)} \]

They share the same numerator but usually have different denominators. Therefore, they are not generally equal.

Common mistake

The probability of disease given a positive test is not the same as the probability of a positive test given disease.

5. Worked classroom example

Suppose a class has 100 students:

  • 40 study statistics,
  • 25 receive a distinction,
  • 20 both study statistics and receive a distinction.

Let \(S\) be the event “studies statistics” and \(D\) be the event “receives distinction”.

\[ P(D|S)=\frac{P(D\cap S)}{P(S)}=\frac{20/100}{40/100}=\frac{20}{40}=0.50 \]
\[ P(S|D)=\frac{P(S\cap D)}{P(D)}=\frac{20/100}{25/100}=\frac{20}{25}=0.80 \]

These probabilities are different because the reference groups are different.

6. Derivation of the multiplication rule

Start with the definition:

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \]

Multiply both sides by \(P(B)\):

\[ P(A|B)P(B)=P(A\cap B) \]

Therefore:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A|B)P(B) \]

Similarly:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(B|A)P(A) \]
Multiplication rule

The probability that both \(A\) and \(B\) occur can be calculated as the probability of one event multiplied by the conditional probability of the other.

7. Independence

Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if knowing that \(B\) occurred does not change the probability of \(A\):

\[ P(A|B)=P(A) \]

Using the multiplication rule:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A|B)P(B) \]

If \(A\) and \(B\) are independent, then \(P(A|B)=P(A)\), so:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B) \]

8. Mutually exclusive versus independent

Mutually exclusive events cannot occur together. Independent events can occur together, but one does not change the probability of the other.

ConceptMeaningFormula
Mutually exclusiveThe events cannot happen together\(P(A\cap B)=0\)
IndependentKnowing one event does not change the other probability\(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\)

If \(P(A)>0\) and \(P(B)>0\), mutually exclusive events are not independent. If \(B\) occurs, \(A\) becomes impossible, so the probability of \(A\) changes.

9. Derivation of Bayes' theorem

From the multiplication rule:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(A|B)P(B) \]

Also:

\[ P(A\cap B)=P(B|A)P(A) \]

Since both expressions equal \(P(A\cap B)\), set them equal:

\[ P(A|B)P(B)=P(B|A)P(A) \]

Divide both sides by \(P(B)\), assuming \(P(B)>0\):

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} \]

This is Bayes' theorem.

10. Law of total probability

Often \(P(B)\) is not given directly. If \(A\) and \(A^c\) split the sample space into two non-overlapping parts, then \(B\) can happen through \(A\) or through \(A^c\).

\[ P(B)=P(B\cap A)+P(B\cap A^c) \]

Using the multiplication rule:

\[ P(B)=P(B|A)P(A)+P(B|A^c)P(A^c) \]

Substituting this into Bayes' theorem gives:

\[ P(A|B)=\frac{P(B|A)P(A)} {P(B|A)P(A)+P(B|A^c)P(A^c)} \]

11. Medical testing terminology

Let \(D\) be the event that a person has disease and \(+\) be the event that the test is positive.

TermProbabilityMeaning
Prevalence\(P(D)\)Probability of disease before testing
Sensitivity\(P(+|D)\)Probability of a positive test among diseased people
Specificity\(P(-|D^c)\)Probability of a negative test among non-diseased people
False positive rate\(P(+|D^c)\)Probability of a positive test among non-diseased people
Positive predictive value\(P(D|+)\)Probability of disease after a positive test
Negative predictive value\(P(D^c|-)\)Probability of no disease after a negative test

12. Full diagnostic test derivation

We want \(P(D|+)\). By Bayes' theorem:

\[ P(D|+)=\frac{P(+|D)P(D)}{P(+)} \]

Using the law of total probability:

\[ P(+)=P(+|D)P(D)+P(+|D^c)P(D^c) \]

Therefore:

\[ P(D|+)= \frac{P(+|D)P(D)} {P(+|D)P(D)+P(+|D^c)P(D^c)} \]

13. Worked diagnostic test example

Suppose:

  • \(P(D)=0.01\),
  • \(P(+|D)=0.95\),
  • \(P(-|D^c)=0.90\), so \(P(+|D^c)=0.10\),
  • \(P(D^c)=0.99\).
\[ P(D|+)= \frac{0.95(0.01)} {0.95(0.01)+0.10(0.99)} \]
\[ P(D|+)= \frac{0.0095} {0.0095+0.099} = \frac{0.0095}{0.1085} \approx 0.0876 \]

The positive predictive value is approximately 8.76%.

14. Natural frequency explanation

Imagine 10,000 people:

  • 1% have disease, so 100 people have disease.
  • 99% do not have disease, so 9,900 people do not have disease.
  • 95% sensitivity gives 95 true positives.
  • 10% false positive rate gives 990 false positives.

Therefore, among all positive tests:

\[ P(D|+)=\frac{95}{95+990}=\frac{95}{1085}\approx 0.0876 \]

The natural frequency method often makes Bayes' theorem easier to understand than percentages alone.

15. Base-rate fallacy

The base-rate fallacy occurs when the prior probability or prevalence is ignored. In diagnostic testing, even a good test can have a low positive predictive value when the disease is rare.

Important warning

High sensitivity does not automatically imply high probability of disease after a positive test. Prevalence and false positive rate matter.

16. Common mistakes

  • Confusing \(P(A|B)\) and \(P(B|A)\): these usually have different denominators.
  • Ignoring the condition: always identify the reference group after “given”.
  • Assuming independence: independence must be justified, not assumed for convenience.
  • Confusing mutually exclusive with independent: these are different concepts.
  • Ignoring prevalence: diagnostic test interpretation depends heavily on baseline probability.
  • Interpreting sensitivity as positive predictive value: \(P(+|D)\neq P(D|+)\).
Notes summary
  • Conditional probability restricts the sample space to the condition.
  • The denominator of \(P(A|B)\) is \(P(B)\).
  • The multiplication rule is derived directly from conditional probability.
  • Independence means conditioning does not change the probability.
  • Bayes' theorem is derived by equating two expressions for \(P(A\cap B)\).
  • The law of total probability often supplies the denominator in Bayes' theorem.
  • Medical test interpretation requires prevalence, sensitivity and specificity.

Practice exercises

Conditional probability and Bayes' theorem

Try each exercise before revealing the answer. The focus is on correct denominators, derivations and interpretation.

Exercise 1
Basic conditional probability
Easy+

If \(P(A\cap B)=0.18\) and \(P(B)=0.30\), calculate \(P(A|B)\).

Answer

\(P(A|B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)=0.18/0.30=0.60\).

Exercise 2
Reverse conditioning
Easy+

Suppose \(P(A)=0.50\), \(P(B)=0.25\), and \(P(A\cap B)=0.20\). Calculate \(P(A|B)\) and \(P(B|A)\).

Answer

\(P(A|B)=0.20/0.25=0.80\). Also, \(P(B|A)=0.20/0.50=0.40\). They differ because the denominators differ.

Exercise 3
Multiplication rule
Medium+

If \(P(B)=0.40\) and \(P(A|B)=0.75\), find \(P(A\cap B)\).

Answer

By the multiplication rule, \(P(A\cap B)=P(A|B)P(B)=0.75\times0.40=0.30\).

Exercise 4
Independence check
Medium+

Let \(P(A)=0.60\), \(P(B)=0.50\), and \(P(A\cap B)=0.30\). Are \(A\) and \(B\) independent?

Answer

Check whether \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\). Since \(0.60\times0.50=0.30\), the condition is satisfied. The events are independent.

Exercise 5
Bayes theorem calculation
Medium+

A disease has prevalence 2%, sensitivity 90%, and false positive rate 5%. Calculate \(P(D|+)\).

Answer

\(P(D|+)=\frac{0.90(0.02)}{0.90(0.02)+0.05(0.98)}=\frac{0.018}{0.018+0.049}=\frac{0.018}{0.067}\approx0.269\). The probability of disease after a positive test is about 26.9%.

Exercise 6
Natural frequencies
Hard+

Using the disease example from Exercise 5, explain the result using a hypothetical population of 10,000 people.

Answer

Out of 10,000 people, 2% have disease, so 200 have disease. Sensitivity 90% gives 180 true positives. The remaining 9,800 do not have disease. False positive rate 5% gives 490 false positives. Total positives are 180+490=670. Therefore \(P(D|+)=180/670\approx0.269\).

Exercise 7
Base-rate fallacy
Hard+

A student says, “The test is 99% sensitive, so if I test positive, there is a 99% chance I have the disease.” Explain the mistake.

Answer

The student confuses \(P(+|D)\) with \(P(D|+)\). Sensitivity is the probability of a positive test among diseased people. The probability of disease after a positive test also depends on disease prevalence and the false positive rate.

Exercise 8
Deriving Bayes' theorem
Hard+

Derive Bayes' theorem from the multiplication rule.

Answer

From the multiplication rule, \(P(A\cap B)=P(A|B)P(B)\) and \(P(A\cap B)=P(B|A)P(A)\). Equating them gives \(P(A|B)P(B)=P(B|A)P(A)\). Dividing by \(P(B)\) gives \(P(A|B)=\frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}\).

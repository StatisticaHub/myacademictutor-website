What is statistics?

Mr. R · Emma, Oliver, James, Sophia · ~25 min · Introduction to statistics
📍 Scene — the first statistics class begins
EM
Emma

I hear the word statistics everywhere: health reports, sports, elections, research papers. But what exactly is statistics?

MR
Mr. R

Statistics is the discipline of learning from data. It helps us collect data carefully, summarise what we observe, and make reasoned conclusions under uncertainty.

OL
Oliver

So statistics is not just calculating averages?

MR
Mr. R

No. Averages are only one small part. Statistics is about turning data into evidence, while being honest about uncertainty, variability and limitations.

Big idea

Statistics is the science of collecting, organising, analysing, interpreting and communicating data in order to answer questions under uncertainty.

Why statistics exists

In real life, we rarely know everything. A researcher may study a sample of patients rather than every patient in the world. A university may survey some students rather than all students. A public health team may estimate infection levels from partial data. Statistics exists because decisions must often be made using incomplete but informative data.

JA
James

Is statistics mainly about uncertainty?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. Data vary from person to person, sample to sample and study to study. Statistics gives us language and methods for understanding that variation.

SO
Sophia

Then statistics is also about judgement?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Good statistics is not mechanical. It requires careful questions, good data, correct methods and responsible interpretation.

The statistical thinking cycle

A statistical investigation usually follows a cycle. We begin with a question, collect or obtain data, organise the data, analyse patterns, interpret results and communicate conclusions.

StageMain questionExample
QuestionWhat do we want to know?Do students sleep enough before exams?
Data collectionHow will we obtain reliable data?Survey a representative group of students
DescriptionWhat do the data show?Summarise sleep hours using centre and spread
InferenceWhat can we conclude beyond the sample?Estimate average sleep for the wider student population
CommunicationHow should we report the evidence?Explain findings, uncertainty and limitations clearly

Descriptive and inferential statistics

Statistics has two broad branches. Descriptive statistics summarises the data we have. Inferential statistics uses sample data to make conclusions about a wider population.

EM
Emma

If I calculate the mean height of students in this classroom, is that descriptive statistics?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. You are describing the students you measured. If you use that class to estimate the mean height of all university students, that becomes inference.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Statistics is about learning from data under uncertainty.
  • Data do not speak for themselves; they require context and interpretation.
  • Descriptive statistics summarises observed data.
  • Inferential statistics uses samples to reason about populations.
  • Good statistical thinking begins with a clear question.
Detailed notes

What is statistics?

1. Definition of statistics

Statistics is a discipline concerned with data. More formally, it studies methods for collecting, organising, summarising, analysing, interpreting and presenting data.

Definition — statistics

Statistics is the science of learning from data and making reasoned conclusions in the presence of uncertainty and variability.

This definition has three important parts:

  • Data: observations, measurements or recorded information.
  • Uncertainty: we usually do not know everything about the population or process.
  • Reasoned conclusion: statistical conclusions should follow from evidence, assumptions and appropriate methods.

2. Data, information and evidence

Data are raw recorded values. Information is created when data are organised and given meaning. Evidence is created when information is linked to a question using careful reasoning.

LevelMeaningExample
DataRaw observations7, 6, 8, 5, 9 hours of sleep
InformationOrganised summaryMean sleep is 7 hours
EvidenceInterpretation for a questionStudents may be sleeping less than recommended before exams

3. Population and sample

A population is the full group or process we want to understand. A sample is the subset of observations we actually observe.

Population = all individuals or units of interest
Sample = observed subset of the population

Statistics is often needed because populations are too large, costly or impossible to observe completely.

4. Descriptive statistics

Descriptive statistics summarises the data we have. It does not, by itself, claim that the summary applies to a wider population. It describes observed data using tables, graphs and numerical summaries.

Examples

Mean, median, standard deviation, interquartile range, percentages, frequency tables, histograms and boxplots are descriptive statistical tools.

5. Inferential statistics

Inferential statistics uses sample data to draw conclusions about a wider population or underlying process. Because samples vary, inference must include uncertainty.

Sample evidence + assumptions → conclusion about population

Inferential tools include confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, regression models and prediction intervals.

6. Parameters and statistics

A parameter is a numerical feature of a population. A statistic is a numerical feature calculated from a sample.

ConceptPopulationSample
MeanParameter: μStatistic: x̄
ProportionParameter: pStatistic: p̂
Standard deviationParameter: σStatistic: s

7. Variability

Variability means that observations differ. People have different heights, patients have different recovery times, and repeated samples give different results. Without variability, statistics would be much simpler. With variability, we need careful methods.

Important idea

Statistics does not remove uncertainty. It helps us quantify, understand and communicate uncertainty.

8. Statistical questions

A statistical question expects variation in the answer. For example, “How many hours do students sleep before an exam?” is statistical because different students will give different answers. “How many hours are in a day?” is not a statistical question because the answer is fixed.

9. The role of assumptions

Statistical conclusions depend on assumptions. These may involve sampling, measurement quality, independence, model form or study design. A method can be mathematically correct but still misleading if the assumptions do not match the data situation.

10. Common mistakes

  • Thinking statistics is only calculation.
  • Reporting numbers without context.
  • Confusing a sample summary with a population truth.
  • Ignoring how the data were collected.
  • Believing that a graph or average automatically proves a conclusion.
  • Forgetting that uncertainty is part of statistical reasoning.

Practice exercises

What is statistics?

Exercise 1
Statistical or non-statistical question?
Easy+

Is “How many hours do students sleep before an exam?” a statistical question? Explain.

Answer

Yes. Different students will give different answers, so the question expects variability.

Exercise 2
Descriptive or inferential?
Easy+

A class survey finds that the average sleep time of 30 students is 6.8 hours. Is this descriptive or inferential?

Answer

If it only describes those 30 students, it is descriptive statistics. If it is used to estimate sleep time for all students, it becomes inferential statistics.

Exercise 3
Population and sample
Medium+

A researcher studies 200 patients from one hospital to understand recovery time for all patients with the same condition. Identify the sample and population.

Answer

The sample is the 200 patients studied. The population is all patients with the same condition that the researcher wants to understand.

Exercise 4
Parameter or statistic?
Medium+

The mean height calculated from a sample of 80 students is 171 cm. Is 171 cm a parameter or a statistic?

Answer

It is a statistic because it is calculated from a sample.

Exercise 5
Why uncertainty matters
Hard+

Explain why two different samples from the same population may give different means.

Answer

Samples contain different individuals or observations. Because observations vary, the sample mean also varies from sample to sample. This is sampling variation.

Module 1 · Topic 2

Populations, samples and variables

Mr. R · Emma, Oliver, James, Sophia · ~28 min · Introduction to statistics
📍 Scene — the class prepares its first real statistical study
EM
Emma

Last time we said statistics helps us learn from data. But whose data are we learning from?

MR
Mr. R

Excellent starting point. In any statistical study, we must identify the population, the sample, the individuals or units, and the variables measured.

OL
Oliver

Population means everyone in the country?

MR
Mr. R

Not always. Population means the complete group we want to understand. It could be all UK adults, all students in one university, all blood samples in a laboratory, or all plants in a field trial.

Big idea

A statistical conclusion is only meaningful when we know who or what the data represent, what was measured, and how the observed sample relates to the target population.

Population

The population is the entire collection of individuals, units, cases or objects that the study is about. It is defined by the research question. If the question changes, the population may also change.

JA
James

So the population is not always directly observed?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. Often the population is too large or impossible to observe completely. That is why we use a sample.

Sample

A sample is the subset of the population that is actually observed or measured. The sample provides evidence, but it may not perfectly represent the population.

Population = complete group of interest
Sample = observed subset of that group

Individuals or observational units

The individuals, cases or observational units are the entities on which measurements are taken. These do not have to be people. They can be patients, schools, countries, cells, genes, leaves, hospitals, days, cities or trials.

SO
Sophia

If we record temperature every day for a month, what are the observational units?

MR
Mr. R

The days are the observational units, because each day receives a temperature measurement.

Variables

A variable is a characteristic that can vary from one observational unit to another. For students, variables may include age, height, programme of study, exam score, sleep duration and commute time.

Study situationPopulationSampleVariable
University sleep studyAll students in the university120 students surveyedHours of sleep
Hospital waiting time studyAll patients attending the clinic300 recorded visitsWaiting time in minutes
Plant experimentAll plants under a condition of interest60 measured plantsLeaf length

Why representativeness matters

A sample should resemble the population in the features that matter for the research question. If some groups are over-represented or under-represented, the sample may give misleading conclusions.

EM
Emma

If we only survey students in the library at midnight, can we generalise to all students?

MR
Mr. R

We should be very cautious. That sample may over-represent students who study late or live near campus.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • The population is the full group we want to understand.
  • The sample is the subset we actually observe.
  • The observational unit is the entity being measured.
  • A variable is a characteristic recorded on each unit.
  • The research question determines the correct population and variables.
  • A biased or unrepresentative sample can lead to misleading conclusions.
Detailed notes

Populations, samples and variables

1. Population

A population is the complete set of individuals, objects, events or measurements about which we want to draw conclusions. It is not necessarily large, national or human. It is defined by the research question.

Definition — population

The population is the full collection of units that the study aims to describe or understand.

Examples:

  • All first-year undergraduate students at a university.
  • All patients diagnosed with a particular condition in a country.
  • All Arabidopsis plants grown under a certain experimental condition.
  • All days in a year for which rainfall could be recorded.

2. Target population and accessible population

The target population is the group we truly want to generalise to. The accessible population is the group we can realistically reach or sample from.

ConceptMeaningExample
Target populationThe ideal group of interestAll adults in the UK
Accessible populationThe group we can actually accessAdults registered with selected GP practices
SampleThe units actually observed2,000 adults who completed the study
Important warning

If the accessible population is very different from the target population, generalising results becomes risky.

3. Sample

A sample is a subset of the population selected for observation. The purpose of a sample is to provide evidence about the population while avoiding the cost, time or impossibility of measuring every unit.

Sample ⊂ Population

The quality of a sample depends on how it was selected, not only on its size. A large biased sample may be less useful than a smaller well-designed sample.

4. Census

A census attempts to measure every unit in the population. In principle, a census removes sampling variation. In practice, it can still have measurement error, non-response, missing data and practical limitations.

5. Observational units

The observational unit is the unit on which data are collected. Identifying the unit prevents confusion between rows, variables and populations.

Data table row representsObservational unitPossible variables
One studentStudentAge, exam score, sleep hours
One hospitalHospitalNumber of beds, waiting time, region
One patient visitVisitWaiting time, diagnosis, appointment type
One plantPlantHeight, genotype, leaf count

6. Variables

A variable is any characteristic that may take different values for different units. In a data table, variables usually appear as columns.

Definition — variable

A variable is a measured or recorded characteristic of an observational unit.

Variables can be numerical or categorical. More detailed types will be studied in Lesson 1.3.

7. Parameters and statistics

A parameter is a numerical summary of the population. A statistic is a numerical summary of the sample.

QuantityPopulation parameterSample statistic
Meanμx̄
Proportionpp̂
Standard deviationσs

Statistics are often used to estimate parameters. This is the foundation of statistical inference.

8. Sampling variation

If we repeatedly take samples from the same population, the sample statistics will usually differ from sample to sample. This is called sampling variation.

Different samples → different sample statistics

Sampling variation is not a mistake. It is a natural consequence of observing only part of the population.

9. Bias and representativeness

A sample is biased if the method of selection systematically over-represents or under-represents certain parts of the population in a way that affects the study question.

Bias warning

Increasing the sample size does not automatically fix bias. If the sampling method is flawed, a very large sample can still be misleading.

10. Common mistakes

  • Calling the sample the population.
  • Defining the population vaguely.
  • Forgetting that observational units do not have to be people.
  • Confusing variables with values of variables.
  • Assuming large samples are always representative.
  • Generalising beyond the population actually represented by the sample.

Practice exercises

Populations, samples and variables

Exercise 1
Identify population and sample
Easy+

A university surveys 300 students to estimate average sleep time among all students at the university. Identify the population and sample.

Answer

The population is all students at the university. The sample is the 300 students surveyed.

Exercise 2
Observational unit
Easy+

A dataset records one row per hospital, including number of beds and average waiting time. What is the observational unit?

Answer

The observational unit is the hospital, because each row represents one hospital.

Exercise 3
Identify a variable
Medium+

In a study of students, the recorded columns are age, degree programme and exam score. Name the variables.

Answer

The variables are age, degree programme and exam score.

Exercise 4
Parameter or statistic
Medium+

The true average height of all students in a university is unknown. Is this unknown average a parameter or a statistic?

Answer

It is a parameter because it describes the entire population of students at the university.

Exercise 5
Sampling bias
Hard+

A survey about student exercise habits is conducted only among members of the university gym. Explain the possible bias.

Answer

Gym members are likely to exercise more than the average student, so the sample may overestimate exercise levels for the whole student population.

Types of data

Mr. R · Emma, Oliver, James, Sophia · ~30 min · Introduction to statistics
📍 Scene — the class opens a dataset and sees many different columns
EM
Emma

Our dataset has age, gender, exam score, blood group, satisfaction rating and height. Are all of these treated the same way?

MR
Mr. R

No. Different variables contain different kinds of information. The type of data decides what summaries, graphs and statistical methods are appropriate.

OL
Oliver

So before analysing data, we first classify the variables?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Good analysis begins by understanding what each variable actually represents.

Big idea

The type of data determines what mathematical operations, graphs, summaries and interpretations are meaningful.

The two broad families

Most variables can first be divided into two broad families: categorical variables and numerical variables.

FamilyMeaningExamplesCommon summaries
CategoricalValues are labels or groupsBlood group, eye colour, degree programmeCounts, percentages, mode
NumericalValues represent measurable quantitiesAge, height, income, exam scoreMean, median, spread
JA
James

What if categories are coded using numbers, like 1 for male and 2 for female?

MR
Mr. R

Then they are still categorical. Numerical codes do not automatically make a variable numerical.

Categorical data

Categorical data place units into groups. Some categorical variables have no natural order, while others do.

TypeMeaningExamples
NominalCategories have no natural orderBlood group, country, eye colour
OrdinalCategories have a meaningful orderLow/medium/high, satisfaction rating, disease stage

Numerical data

Numerical data represent quantities. They may be discrete or continuous.

TypeMeaningExamples
DiscreteCountable values, often whole numbersNumber of siblings, number of hospital visits
ContinuousMeasured values on a continuumHeight, weight, time, blood pressure
SO
Sophia

Exam score looks numerical, but it is often a whole number. Is it discrete?

MR
Mr. R

Usually yes, because scores are counted in fixed units. But in practice, many discrete numerical variables with many possible values are analysed similarly to continuous variables.

Measurement scales

Another way to classify data is by scale: nominal, ordinal, interval and ratio. These scales tell us what comparisons and calculations are meaningful.

ScaleOrder?Equal intervals?True zero?Example
NominalNoNoNoBlood group
OrdinalYesNot guaranteedNoSatisfaction rating
IntervalYesYesNo true zeroTemperature in Celsius
RatioYesYesYesHeight, weight, time

Why this matters

If we treat a nominal variable as numerical, we may calculate meaningless averages. If we ignore the order of an ordinal variable, we may lose important information. If we use the wrong graph, the data may be misrepresented.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Categorical variables describe groups or labels.
  • Numerical variables describe quantities.
  • Nominal categories have no natural order.
  • Ordinal categories have a meaningful order.
  • Discrete numerical variables are countable.
  • Continuous numerical variables are measured on a continuum.
  • Data type controls which summaries and graphs are meaningful.
Detailed notes

Types of data

1. Why data type matters

A variable's type tells us what its values mean. This matters because statistical summaries are not just calculations; they must be meaningful. For example, the mean of blood group codes is not meaningful, even if blood groups are coded as numbers.

Definition — data type

A data type describes the kind of information recorded by a variable and determines which mathematical and statistical operations are appropriate.

2. Categorical variables

A categorical variable places each observational unit into one of several groups. The values are labels rather than measurable quantities.

Nominal categorical data

Nominal data have categories with no natural order. Examples include blood group, nationality, hair colour, treatment group and degree programme.

Appropriate summaries

Counts, percentages, proportions, bar charts and pie charts are commonly used for nominal variables. The mode can be meaningful, but the mean is not.

Ordinal categorical data

Ordinal data have categories that can be ordered, but the distance between categories may not be equal. Examples include pain severity, satisfaction rating, disease stage and education level.

Low < Medium < High

The ordering is meaningful, but the difference between low and medium may not be the same as the difference between medium and high.

3. Numerical variables

A numerical variable records a quantity. Arithmetic operations such as addition and subtraction are usually meaningful for numerical variables.

Discrete numerical data

Discrete variables take separated, countable values. They often arise from counting.

  • Number of siblings
  • Number of goals scored
  • Number of clinic visits
  • Number of correct answers

Continuous numerical data

Continuous variables are measured on a continuum and can, in principle, take infinitely many values within an interval. They often arise from measurement.

  • Height
  • Weight
  • Time
  • Temperature
  • Blood pressure

4. Discrete versus continuous in practice

The boundary between discrete and continuous is sometimes practical rather than absolute. Age may be recorded in whole years, but conceptually it is continuous. Exam marks may be discrete, but if there are many possible scores, they may be summarised like continuous data.

5. Measurement scales

The four common measurement scales are nominal, ordinal, interval and ratio.

ScaleMain featureMeaningful operationsExample
NominalNames onlySame/different, countsBlood group
OrdinalOrdered categoriesRank comparisonsLikert satisfaction scale
IntervalEqual intervals, no true zeroDifferencesCelsius temperature
RatioEqual intervals and true zeroDifferences and ratiosHeight, mass, duration

6. Interval scale

Interval data have meaningful differences, but zero does not mean absence of the quantity. Celsius temperature is the standard example: 0°C does not mean “no temperature”. Therefore, 20°C is not “twice as hot” as 10°C in an absolute sense.

7. Ratio scale

Ratio data have meaningful differences and a meaningful zero. Height, mass, time duration and income are ratio variables. A value of zero represents absence of the measured quantity, and ratios are meaningful.

Example

A 10 kg object has twice the mass of a 5 kg object because mass has a true zero. But 20°C is not twice as hot as 10°C because Celsius does not have a true zero.

8. Binary variables

A binary variable has only two possible categories, often coded as 0 and 1. Examples include yes/no, dead/alive, disease present/absent and pass/fail.

Y = 1 if event occurs, and Y = 0 if event does not occur

Binary variables are categorical, even when coded numerically. However, their numerical coding becomes useful later in probability and logistic regression.

9. Data type and graph choice

Variable typeUseful graphsUseful summaries
Nominal categoricalBar chartCounts, percentages, mode
Ordinal categoricalOrdered bar chartCounts, percentages, median category
Discrete numericalDot plot, bar chart, histogramMean, median, frequency
Continuous numericalHistogram, boxplot, density curveMean, median, standard deviation, quartiles

10. Common mistakes

  • Assuming that numerical codes always mean numerical data.
  • Calculating means for nominal categories.
  • Ignoring the order in ordinal data.
  • Treating ordinal ratings as if all category distances are exactly equal without justification.
  • Confusing discrete counts with continuous measurements.
  • Choosing graphs without considering variable type.

Types of data

Exercise 1
Nominal or ordinal?
Easy+

Classify blood group as nominal or ordinal.

Answer

Blood group is nominal because the categories have no natural order.

Exercise 2
Discrete or continuous?
Easy+

Classify number of hospital visits in a year as discrete or continuous.

Answer

It is discrete because it is a count and takes separated whole-number values.

Exercise 3
Continuous measurement
Medium+

Classify height as discrete or continuous, and explain why.

Answer

Height is continuous because it is measured on a continuum and can in principle take many decimal values.

Exercise 4
Ordinal scale
Medium+

A satisfaction response has categories very dissatisfied, dissatisfied, neutral, satisfied, very satisfied. What type of data is this?

Answer

It is ordinal categorical data because the categories have a meaningful order, but equal distances between categories are not guaranteed.

Exercise 5
Numerical codes
Hard+

A dataset codes blood group as 1=A, 2=B, 3=AB, 4=O. Why should we not calculate the mean blood group code?

Answer

The numbers are only labels. They do not represent quantities or ordered distances, so their mean has no meaningful interpretation.

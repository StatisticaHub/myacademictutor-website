Sampling methods

Mr. R · Emma, Oliver, James, Sophia · ~32 min · Introduction to study design
📍 Scene — the class must decide how to choose people for a survey
EM
Emma

We know what a population and sample are. But how do we actually choose the sample?

MR
Mr. R

That is the central question of sampling. A sample should give useful evidence about the population, and the method used to select it matters enormously.

OL
Oliver

Can we just ask the first people we meet?

MR
Mr. R

That is convenient, but it may be biased. The easiest people to reach may not represent the population well.

Big idea

Sampling is not only about sample size. A smaller well-designed sample can be more informative than a large biased sample.

Why sampling matters

We usually sample because it is impossible, expensive or unnecessary to measure every unit in a population. But the sample must be selected carefully. Otherwise, the data may systematically misrepresent the population.

JA
James

If the sample is large enough, will that automatically solve the problem?

MR
Mr. R

No. A large biased sample is still biased. Increasing sample size reduces random sampling variation, but it does not automatically remove systematic selection bias.

Probability sampling

In probability sampling, units are selected using a random mechanism, and the chance of selection is known or at least controlled by design. This supports formal statistical inference.

MethodMain ideaExample
Simple random samplingEvery unit has equal chance of selectionRandomly select 100 student IDs from a register
Systematic samplingSelect every kth unit after a random startChoose every 10th patient record
Stratified samplingSample separately within important subgroupsSample students from each year group
Cluster samplingRandomly choose groups, then observe units inside themSelect several schools, then survey students inside them

Non-probability sampling

In non-probability sampling, selection is not based on a fully random mechanism. These methods may be practical, but they often make generalisation more difficult.

MethodMain ideaRisk
Convenience samplingChoose units that are easiest to accessSample may be unrepresentative

SO
Sophia

Is non-probability sampling always useless?

MR
Mr. R

No. It can be useful in exploratory research, hard-to-reach populations or early-stage studies. But we must be careful about how far we generalise.

Sampling bias and non-response

Sampling bias occurs when the sampling method systematically favours certain units. Non-response bias occurs when selected units do not participate, and non-responders differ from responders in important ways.

Warning

Random selection is helpful, but poor response rates, missing data and measurement problems can still damage representativeness.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Sampling methods determine how well a sample can represent a population.
  • Probability sampling uses random selection and supports stronger inference.
  • Simple random, systematic, stratified and cluster sampling are common probability methods.
  • Convenience and voluntary response samples can be seriously biased.
  • Larger samples reduce random variation but do not automatically remove bias.
  • Non-response can make even a carefully selected sample unrepresentative.
Detailed notes

Sampling methods

1. Purpose of sampling

Sampling is the process of selecting a subset of units from a population. The goal is to obtain data that can inform us about the population while saving time, cost and effort.

Definition — sampling

Sampling is the method by which units are selected from a population for observation or measurement.

2. Sampling frame

A sampling frame is the actual list or mechanism from which the sample is drawn. It should match the target population as closely as possible.

Target population → sampling frame → selected sample

If the sampling frame misses important members of the population, coverage bias can occur.

Coverage bias

Coverage bias occurs when some parts of the target population are absent, under-represented or over-represented in the sampling frame.

3. Simple random sampling

In simple random sampling, every unit has an equal chance of being selected, and every sample of the same size has an equal chance of selection.

P(unit selected) = n / N

where \(N\) is the population size and \(n\) is the sample size.

Strength

Simple random sampling is conceptually clean and supports many standard inference methods.

Limitation

It requires a complete sampling frame and may under-represent small subgroups by chance.

4. Systematic sampling

In systematic sampling, we order the sampling frame, choose a random starting point, and then select every \(k\)th unit.

k = N / n

For example, if there are 1,000 records and we want 100, we may choose every 10th record after a random start.

Caution

Systematic sampling can be biased if the list has a hidden pattern that aligns with the sampling interval.

5. Stratified sampling

In stratified sampling, the population is divided into subgroups called strata, and random samples are taken from each stratum.

Population = Stratum 1 ∪ Stratum 2 ∪ ⋯ ∪ Stratum K

Stratification is useful when important subgroups must be represented, such as year groups, regions, age bands or treatment centres.

Proportional allocation

In proportional stratified sampling, the sample size in each stratum is proportional to the stratum size in the population.

n_h = n × (N_h / N)

where \(N_h\) is the size of stratum \(h\), \(N\) is the population size and \(n\) is the total sample size.

6. Cluster sampling

In cluster sampling, the population is divided into naturally occurring groups called clusters. Some clusters are selected at random, and units within selected clusters are measured.

Cluster sampling can reduce cost when units are geographically or organisationally grouped, but individuals within the same cluster may be similar, which reduces statistical efficiency.

7. Convenience sampling

Convenience sampling selects units that are easiest to reach. It is quick but often biased.

Example

Surveying only people leaving a gym to estimate exercise habits in the whole city will likely overestimate exercise levels.

8. Voluntary response sampling

In voluntary response sampling, individuals choose whether to participate. People with strong opinions may be more likely to respond, creating bias.

9. Quota and snowball sampling

Quota sampling fills required numbers from subgroups, but selection within each subgroup may not be random. Snowball sampling uses participants to recruit others and is sometimes used for hard-to-reach populations.

10. Bias versus sampling variation

Sampling variation is random fluctuation from sample to sample. Bias is systematic error caused by the design or process.

ConceptMeaningReduced by
Sampling variationRandom differences between samplesLarger sample size and efficient design
BiasSystematic departure from the truthBetter design, better frame, improved response

11. Non-response bias

Non-response bias occurs when selected people do not respond and their outcomes differ from those who do respond.

Response rate = number responding / number selected

A high response rate is desirable, but response quality and differences between responders and non-responders also matter.

12. Common mistakes

  • Thinking that a large sample is automatically representative.
  • Ignoring how the sample was selected.
  • Confusing random sampling with random assignment.
  • Assuming voluntary response surveys represent everyone.
  • Ignoring non-response and missing data.
  • Generalising beyond the sampling frame.

Practice exercises

Sampling methods

Exercise 1
Simple random sampling
Easy+

A university randomly selects 200 student ID numbers from a complete student register. What sampling method is this?

Answer

This is simple random sampling because students are selected randomly from a complete list.

Exercise 2
Systematic sampling
Easy+

A researcher selects every 20th patient record after a random starting point. What sampling method is this?

Answer

This is systematic sampling.

Exercise 3
Stratified sampling
Medium+

A study randomly samples students separately from each year group. What sampling method is used, and why might it be useful?

Answer

This is stratified sampling. It is useful because each year group is guaranteed representation in the sample.

Exercise 4
Convenience bias
Medium+

A researcher surveys only students sitting in the library at 11 pm. Explain the likely bias.

Answer

The sample may over-represent students who study late, live near the library or have particular study habits. It may not represent all students.

Exercise 5
Bias versus sample size
Hard+

Why does increasing sample size not automatically remove bias?

Answer

Larger samples reduce random sampling variation, but if the selection process systematically excludes or over-represents certain groups, the estimate remains biased.

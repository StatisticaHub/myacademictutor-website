Module 5 · Lesson 5.2

Least squares derivation

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~60 min · Deriving the regression line
📍 Scene — regression classroom, the line is no longer magic
EM
Emma

In the last lesson, you said least squares chooses the regression line by minimising squared residuals. But how does it actually choose the intercept and slope?

MR
Mr. R

Today we derive that. The regression line is not guessed. It comes from minimising a mathematical function.

OL
Oliver

So we will use calculus?

MR
Mr. R

Yes, but carefully. The idea is simple: choose \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) so the squared vertical distances from the data points to the line are as small as possible.

The fitted line

For a sample of \(n\) observations \((x_i,y_i)\), the fitted simple linear regression line is:

\[ \hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_i \]

The residual for observation \(i\) is:

\[ e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i=y_i-b_0-b_1x_i \]

The least squares criterion

Least squares chooses \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) to minimise the sum of squared residuals:

\[ S(b_0,b_1)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)^2 \]
JA
James

Why squared residuals instead of absolute residuals?

MR
Mr. R

Squaring makes the function smooth and differentiable, gives a clean formula, and penalises large residuals strongly. Absolute deviations are useful too, but lead to a different method.

First derivative with respect to the intercept

To find the minimum, take partial derivatives and set them equal to zero. First differentiate with respect to \(b_0\):

\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial b_0} = -2\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i) \]

Setting this equal to zero gives:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)=0 \]

This says the residuals from the fitted least-squares line sum to zero.

SO
Sophia

So one consequence of least squares is that positive and negative residuals balance out?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly, provided the model includes an intercept.

The line passes through the sample means

Expanding the first normal equation:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}y_i-nb_0-b_1\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i=0 \]

Divide by \(n\):

\[ \bar{y}-b_0-b_1\bar{x}=0 \]

Therefore:

\[ b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x} \]

This means the fitted least-squares line always passes through \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\).

First derivative with respect to the slope

Now differentiate the sum of squared residuals with respect to \(b_1\):

\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial b_1} = -2\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i) \]

Setting this equal to zero gives:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)=0 \]

This is the second normal equation.

The slope formula

Substitute \(b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x}\) into the second normal equation. After simplification, the least-squares slope is:

\[ b_1= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})} {\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2} \]

The numerator measures how \(x\) and \(y\) vary together. The denominator measures how much \(x\) varies.

EM
Emma

So the slope is like covariance divided by variance?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Informally, slope is “co-movement of \(X\) and \(Y\)” divided by “movement in \(X\)”.

Using covariance and variance language

The formula can also be written conceptually as:

\[ b_1=\frac{\text{sample covariance of }X\text{ and }Y}{\text{sample variance of }X} \]

Because the same divisor cancels from numerator and denominator, the slope can be written using sums of deviations.

The intercept formula

Once \(b_1\) is found, the intercept follows immediately:

\[ b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x} \]

This formula forces the fitted line through the centre of the data cloud, \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\).

What the derivation teaches

The derivation is not just algebra. It tells us how least squares behaves: residuals sum to zero, the line passes through the sample means, the slope depends on co-variation, and extreme \(x\)-values can strongly influence the line.

OL
Oliver

Why do extreme \(x\)-values matter more?

MR
Mr. R

Because the slope formula uses \(x_i-\bar{x}\). Points far from the mean of \(X\) have more leverage over the fitted slope.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Least squares minimises \(S(b_0,b_1)=\sum(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)^2\).
  • The derivative with respect to \(b_0\) implies residuals sum to zero.
  • The fitted line passes through \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\).
  • The least-squares slope is \(b_1=\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})/\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2\).
  • The intercept is \(b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x}\).
  • The slope is covariance-like variation divided by variance-like variation in \(X\).
  • Extreme \(X\)-values can have high leverage.
Detailed theoretical notes

Least squares derivation

1. Starting point

Given paired data \((x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)\), the fitted line is:

\[ \hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_i \]

The residual is:

\[ e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i=y_i-b_0-b_1x_i \]

2. Least squares objective function

The least squares estimates minimise:

\[ S(b_0,b_1)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}e_i^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)^2 \]

The goal is to find the values of \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) where this function is as small as possible.

3. First normal equation

Differentiate \(S(b_0,b_1)\) with respect to \(b_0\):

\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial b_0} = \sum_{i=1}^{n}2(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)(-1) \]
\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial b_0} = -2\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i) \]

At the minimum, set the derivative equal to zero:

\[ -2\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)=0 \]

Therefore:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)=0 \]

This is equivalent to:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}e_i=0 \]

4. Intercept formula from the first normal equation

Expand the first normal equation:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}y_i-\sum_{i=1}^{n}b_0-b_1\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i=0 \]

Since \(b_0\) is constant:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}y_i-nb_0-b_1\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i=0 \]

Divide by \(n\):

\[ \bar{y}-b_0-b_1\bar{x}=0 \]

Thus:

\[ b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x} \]

5. Second normal equation

Differentiate \(S(b_0,b_1)\) with respect to \(b_1\):

\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial b_1} = \sum_{i=1}^{n}2(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)(-x_i) \]
\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial b_1} = -2\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i) \]

Set this equal to zero:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)=0 \]

This is equivalent to:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_ie_i=0 \]

So in least squares with an intercept, the residuals are also orthogonal to the predictor \(x\) in the sense that their weighted sum by \(x_i\) is zero.

6. Deriving the slope

Substitute \(b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x}\) into the second normal equation:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i \left[y_i-(\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x})-b_1x_i\right]=0 \]

Simplify inside the brackets:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i \left[(y_i-\bar{y})-b_1(x_i-\bar{x})\right]=0 \]

Expand the sum:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(y_i-\bar{y}) - b_1\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(x_i-\bar{x})=0 \]

Rearrange:

\[ b_1= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(y_i-\bar{y})} {\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(x_i-\bar{x})} \]

Using the identities:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(y_i-\bar{y}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y}) \]
\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i(x_i-\bar{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2 \]

we obtain the standard least-squares slope formula:

\[ b_1= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})} {\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2} \]

7. Intercept after the slope

Once \(b_1\) is calculated, the intercept is:

\[ b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x} \]

This ensures:

\[ \bar{y}=b_0+b_1\bar{x} \]

So the fitted line passes through the point \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\).

8. Alternative notation

Define:

\[ S_{xx}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2 \]
\[ S_{xy}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y}) \]

Then:

\[ b_1=\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \]
\[ b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x} \]

9. Relationship with correlation

The sample correlation is:

\[ r= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})} {\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2} \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\bar{y})^2}} \]

The regression slope can be written as:

\[ b_1=r\frac{s_y}{s_x} \]

This shows that slope is related to correlation, but it also depends on the units and spreads of \(X\) and \(Y\).

10. Why \(S_{xx}\) must be positive

The denominator \(S_{xx}\) must be greater than zero. If all \(x_i\) values are identical, then there is no variation in \(X\), and a slope cannot be estimated.

\[ S_{xx}=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{the regression slope is undefined} \]

11. Properties of least-squares residuals

For simple linear regression with an intercept, the least-squares residuals satisfy:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}e_i=0 \]
\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_ie_i=0 \]
\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}\hat{y}_ie_i=0 \]

These properties are consequences of the normal equations.

12. Common mistakes

  • Forgetting the denominator: the slope is not just the covariance-like numerator; it must be divided by variation in \(X\).
  • Thinking the fitted line passes through all points: it passes through \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\), not through every observation.
  • Ignoring the role of \(X\)-variation: if \(X\) barely varies, slope estimation becomes unstable.
  • Confusing residuals with true errors: residuals are observed deviations from the fitted line.
  • Assuming least squares is robust to outliers: squared residuals can make outliers highly influential.
Notes summary
  • Least squares minimises the sum of squared residuals.
  • Differentiating with respect to \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) gives the normal equations.
  • The slope is \(b_1=S_{xy}/S_{xx}\).
  • The intercept is \(b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x}\).
  • The fitted line passes through \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\).
  • Residuals sum to zero when the model includes an intercept.

Practice exercises

Least squares derivation

Exercise 1
Write the objective function
Easy+

Write the least-squares objective function for simple linear regression.

Answer

\(S(b_0,b_1)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)^2\).

Exercise 2
Residual sum property
Easy+

For a least-squares regression with an intercept, what is \(\sum e_i\)?

Answer

\(\sum_{i=1}^{n}e_i=0\). Positive and negative residuals balance exactly when the model includes an intercept.

Exercise 3
Slope from Sxx and Sxy
Medium+

If \(S_{xy}=40\) and \(S_{xx}=10\), calculate \(b_1\).

Answer

\(b_1=S_{xy}/S_{xx}=40/10=4\).

Exercise 4
Intercept calculation
Medium+

If \(\bar{x}=5\), \(\bar{y}=20\), and \(b_1=3\), calculate \(b_0\).

Answer

\(b_0=\bar{y}-b_1\bar{x}=20-3(5)=5\).

Exercise 5
Line through sample means
Medium+

Show that the line \(\hat{y}=5+3x\) passes through \((\bar{x},\bar{y})=(5,20)\).

Answer

At \(x=5\), \(\hat{y}=5+3(5)=20\). Therefore the fitted line passes through \((5,20)\).

Exercise 6
Compute Sxx
Hard+

For \(x=(1,2,3)\), compute \(S_{xx}\).

Answer

\(\bar{x}=2\). Therefore \(S_{xx}=(1-2)^2+(2-2)^2+(3-2)^2=1+0+1=2\).

Exercise 7
Undefined slope
Hard+

Why can the slope not be estimated if all \(x_i\) values are identical?

Answer

If all \(x_i\) values are identical, then \(S_{xx}=\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2=0\). The slope formula has \(S_{xx}\) in the denominator, so the slope is undefined.

Exercise 8
Outlier influence
Hard+

Explain why a point far from \(\bar{x}\) can strongly influence the least-squares slope.

Answer

The slope formula uses \((x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})\) and \((x_i-\bar{x})^2\). A large \(x_i-\bar{x}\) gives the point high leverage, so it can strongly affect the fitted slope.

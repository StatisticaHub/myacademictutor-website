Model assessment and diagnostics

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~60 min · Checking whether a regression model is trustworthy
📍 Scene — regression classroom, after fitting the model
EM
Emma

We can fit a regression model now. But how do we know whether the model is good?

MR
Mr. R

Good question. Fitting a model is only the beginning. We must assess fit, check assumptions, examine residuals, and look for influential observations.

OL
Oliver

So a small p-value for a slope does not mean the model is automatically appropriate?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A coefficient can be statistically significant inside a model that is badly specified.

What model assessment asks

Model assessment asks several different questions. Does the model explain variation in the outcome? Are the assumptions plausible? Are residuals patterned? Are some observations overly influential? Is the model useful for the intended purpose?

Core idea

A regression model should be judged by fit, assumptions, diagnostics, interpretability, and relevance to the research question.

Residuals as the main diagnostic tool

The residual for observation \(i\) is:

\[ e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i \]

Residuals show what the model failed to explain. If the model is reasonable, residuals should look like random scatter around zero rather than a systematic pattern.

JA
James

So residual plots are not just decorative?

MR
Mr. R

No. They are often the first place where model problems become visible.

Linearity

The linear regression model assumes that the conditional mean of \(Y\) is linear in the predictors. If the true relationship is curved, a straight-line model can be misleading.

\[ E(Y\mid X=x)=\beta_0+\beta_1x \]

If the residuals show a curved pattern against fitted values or a predictor, the linearity assumption may be questionable.

Constant variance

Constant variance means the residual spread is roughly similar across fitted values. If residuals fan out as fitted values increase, the model may have heteroscedasticity.

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_i\mid X_i)=\sigma^2 \]

When variance is not constant, standard errors, confidence intervals, and p-values may be affected.

Normality of errors

Normality of errors is mainly important for small-sample inference. It is usually checked using residual histograms or normal Q-Q plots.

SO
Sophia

Does the outcome \(Y\) itself have to be normally distributed?

MR
Mr. R

Not exactly. The usual regression assumption is about the errors or residuals around the modelled mean, not necessarily the marginal distribution of \(Y\).

Residual standard error

The residual standard error estimates the typical size of residuals. For simple regression, it is:

\[ s_e=\sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{SSE}}{n-2}} \]

For multiple regression with \(p\) predictors, the denominator becomes \(n-p-1\):

\[ s_e=\sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{SSE}}{n-p-1}} \]

\(R^2\)

\(R^2\) measures the proportion of total variation in \(Y\) explained by the model.

\[ R^2=1-\frac{\operatorname{SSE}}{\operatorname{SST}} \]

where:

\[ \operatorname{SST}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\bar{y})^2 \]

and:

\[ \operatorname{SSE}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2 \]
EM
Emma

If \(R^2\) is high, is the model always good?

MR
Mr. R

No. A high \(R^2\) can still come with bias, non-linearity, overfitting, poor causal interpretation, or influential observations.

Adjusted \(R^2\)

Adding predictors usually cannot decrease ordinary \(R^2\), even if the predictors are not useful. Adjusted \(R^2\) penalises unnecessary predictors.

\[ R^2_{\text{adj}} = 1- \left(\frac{\operatorname{SSE}/(n-p-1)} {\operatorname{SST}/(n-1)}\right) \]

Outliers, leverage and influence

An outlier has an unusual \(Y\)-value. A high-leverage point has an unusual \(X\)-value or unusual predictor combination. An influential point strongly changes the fitted model if removed.

TermMeaningWhy it matters
OutlierUnusual outcome valueMay signal error, rare case, or poor fit
LeverageUnusual predictor valueCan pull the regression line
InfluenceStrong effect on model estimatesCan change conclusions

Diagnostics are not automatic deletion rules

Finding an unusual observation does not mean deleting it. We first ask whether it is a data error, a meaningful rare case, a measurement issue, or evidence that the model is inadequate.

OL
Oliver

So we should not just remove points because they make the model look worse?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Removing observations requires a defensible reason. Diagnostics guide investigation; they are not automatic commands.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Model assessment examines fit, assumptions, residuals and influential observations.
  • Residuals should ideally show no systematic pattern.
  • Curved residual patterns suggest non-linearity.
  • Fan-shaped residual plots suggest non-constant variance.
  • \(R^2\) measures explained variation but does not prove model validity.
  • Adjusted \(R^2\) penalises unnecessary predictors.
  • Outliers, leverage and influence are related but different ideas.
  • Diagnostics require statistical and scientific judgement.
Detailed theoretical notes

Model assessment and diagnostics

1. Why diagnostics matter

A fitted regression model is a mathematical summary of data. It may be useful, misleading, over-simplified, unstable, or inappropriate for the research question. Diagnostics help us evaluate whether the model is behaving reasonably.

2. Residuals

The fitted value is:

\[ \hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_i \]

or, in multiple regression:

\[ \hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_{1i}+b_2x_{2i}+\cdots+b_px_{pi} \]

The residual is:

\[ e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i \]

Residual plots help detect model misspecification, non-linearity, unequal variance, outliers and influential observations.

3. Sum of squares

The total sum of squares measures total variation in the outcome:

\[ \operatorname{SST}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\bar{y})^2 \]

The residual sum of squares measures unexplained variation after fitting the model:

\[ \operatorname{SSE}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2 \]

The regression sum of squares measures explained variation:

\[ \operatorname{SSR}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\hat{y}_i-\bar{y})^2 \]

For ordinary least squares with an intercept:

\[ \operatorname{SST}=\operatorname{SSR}+\operatorname{SSE} \]

4. \(R^2\)

The coefficient of determination is:

\[ R^2=\frac{\operatorname{SSR}}{\operatorname{SST}} = 1-\frac{\operatorname{SSE}}{\operatorname{SST}} \]

It measures the proportion of variation in \(Y\) explained by the fitted model.

Interpretation warning

A high \(R^2\) does not prove causation, correct functional form, valid assumptions, or good out-of-sample prediction.

5. Adjusted \(R^2\)

Ordinary \(R^2\) tends to increase when predictors are added. Adjusted \(R^2\) accounts for the number of predictors:

\[ R^2_{\text{adj}} = 1- \left( \frac{\operatorname{SSE}/(n-p-1)} {\operatorname{SST}/(n-1)} \right) \]

Here, \(p\) is the number of predictors excluding the intercept.

6. Residual standard error

The residual standard error estimates the standard deviation of the errors:

\[ s_e=\sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{SSE}}{n-p-1}} \]

It is measured in the units of \(Y\), which makes it easier to interpret than \(R^2\) in many applied settings.

7. Linearity diagnostic

The linearity assumption says:

\[ E(Y\mid X_1,\ldots,X_p) = \beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\cdots+\beta_pX_p \]

If residuals plotted against fitted values or predictors show curvature, the linear mean structure may be inadequate. Possible responses include transformations, polynomial terms, splines, interaction terms, or a different modelling strategy.

8. Constant variance diagnostic

Homoscedasticity means:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_i\mid X_{1i},\ldots,X_{pi})=\sigma^2 \]

Heteroscedasticity means the error variance changes with predictors or fitted values. A fan-shaped residual plot is a common visual sign.

9. Normality diagnostic

For small-sample inference, we often assume:

\[ \varepsilon_i\sim N(0,\sigma^2) \]

This can be assessed using a residual histogram or a normal Q-Q plot. Mild non-normality is often less serious in large samples than strong non-linearity, dependence, or severe heteroscedasticity.

10. Outliers, leverage and influence

A residual outlier is unusual in the \(Y\)-direction. Leverage is unusualness in the predictor space. Influence combines leverage and residual size.

A commonly used leverage quantity is the diagonal element \(h_{ii}\) of the hat matrix:

\[ \mathbf{H}=\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^{T} \]
\[ \hat{\mathbf{Y}}=\mathbf{H}\mathbf{Y} \]

The value \(h_{ii}\) measures how much observation \(i\)'s fitted value depends on its own observed value.

11. Cook's distance

Cook's distance measures how much the fitted model changes when an observation is removed. A simplified conceptual description is:

\[ D_i = \text{overall change in fitted values when observation }i\text{ is removed} \]

Large Cook's distance suggests an observation may be influential. It should trigger investigation, not automatic deletion.

12. Model assessment checklist

  • Does the fitted model answer the research question?
  • Are the outcome and predictors coded correctly?
  • Are residuals centred around zero?
  • Is there evidence of non-linearity?
  • Is residual variance roughly constant?
  • Are there strong outliers, high-leverage points or influential observations?
  • Is \(R^2\) interpreted appropriately?
  • Are conclusions robust to reasonable modelling choices?

13. Common mistakes

  • Relying only on \(R^2\): fit is not the same as validity.
  • Ignoring residual plots: important model failures can be visual.
  • Assuming normality of \(Y\) is required: regression assumptions concern errors conditional on predictors.
  • Deleting outliers automatically: unusual observations require investigation.
  • Using diagnostics mechanically: judgement and subject-matter knowledge are essential.
  • Forgetting the modelling goal: prediction, explanation and causal interpretation require different assessment priorities.
Notes summary
  • Diagnostics check whether a model is reasonable for the intended purpose.
  • \(R^2=1-\operatorname{SSE}/\operatorname{SST}\).
  • Adjusted \(R^2\) accounts for the number of predictors.
  • Residual standard error estimates typical unexplained error in units of \(Y\).
  • Residual plots help assess linearity and constant variance.
  • Outliers, leverage and influence are different concepts.
  • Diagnostics guide investigation; they do not replace judgement.

Practice exercises

Model assessment and diagnostics

Exercise 1
Calculate a residual
Easy+

If \(y_i=28\) and \(\hat{y}_i=24\), calculate the residual.

Answer

\(e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i=28-24=4\). The observation is 4 units above the fitted value.

Exercise 2
Calculate \(R^2\)
Easy+

If \(\operatorname{SSE}=40\) and \(\operatorname{SST}=100\), calculate \(R^2\).

Answer

\(R^2=1-\operatorname{SSE}/\operatorname{SST}=1-40/100=0.60\). The model explains 60% of the total variation in \(Y\).

Exercise 3
Residual standard error
Medium+

A multiple regression model has \(\operatorname{SSE}=180\), \(n=30\), and \(p=2\) predictors. Calculate the residual standard error.

Answer

The degrees of freedom are \(n-p-1=30-2-1=27\). Therefore \(s_e=\sqrt{180/27}\approx\sqrt{6.667}\approx2.58\).

Exercise 4
Interpret residual pattern
Medium+

A residual plot shows a clear U-shaped pattern. What assumption might be questionable?

Answer

A U-shaped residual pattern suggests non-linearity. The model's linear mean structure may be inadequate.

Exercise 5
Heteroscedasticity
Medium+

A residual plot fans out as fitted values increase. What does this suggest?

Answer

It suggests non-constant variance, also called heteroscedasticity. Standard errors and inference may be affected.

Exercise 6
Adjusted \(R^2\)
Hard+

Why might adjusted \(R^2\) decrease after adding a new predictor, even though ordinary \(R^2\) does not?

Answer

Adjusted \(R^2\) penalises model complexity. If the new predictor does not improve fit enough to justify the extra parameter, adjusted \(R^2\) can decrease.

Exercise 7
Outlier versus leverage
Hard+

Explain the difference between an outlier and a high-leverage point.

Answer

An outlier is unusual in the outcome direction, often having a large residual. A high-leverage point is unusual in predictor space. A point can be one, both, or neither.

Exercise 8
Why not delete automatically?
Hard+

Why should influential observations not be deleted automatically?

Answer

They may be valid rare observations, important subgroups, or signs of model misspecification. Deletion requires a defensible reason, such as verified data error, not merely inconvenience.

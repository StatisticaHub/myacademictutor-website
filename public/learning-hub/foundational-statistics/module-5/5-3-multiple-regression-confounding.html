Multiple regression and confounding

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~60 min · Adjusted models and careful interpretation
📍 Scene — regression classroom, one predictor is no longer enough
EM
Emma

Simple regression used one predictor. But real outcomes usually depend on many things. Is that why we need multiple regression?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Multiple regression lets us model the mean of an outcome using more than one explanatory variable. It also helps us compare adjusted and unadjusted relationships.

OL
Oliver

Adjusted means “after accounting for other variables”, right?

MR
Mr. R

Yes, but that phrase must be used carefully. Adjustment changes the interpretation of a coefficient.

The multiple regression model

With \(p\) predictors, the population model is:

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_{1i}+\beta_2X_{2i}+\cdots+\beta_pX_{pi}+\varepsilon_i \]

The conditional mean model is:

\[ E(Y_i\mid X_{1i},X_{2i},\ldots,X_{pi}) = \beta_0+\beta_1X_{1i}+\beta_2X_{2i}+\cdots+\beta_pX_{pi} \]

Each coefficient describes an adjusted association: the expected change in the mean of \(Y\) for a one-unit increase in that predictor, while holding the other predictors fixed.

JA
James

What does “holding other predictors fixed” mean in practice?

MR
Mr. R

It means comparing observations that have the same values of the other variables in the model, at least conceptually. For example, comparing two people of the same age and sex but different exposure values.

Unadjusted versus adjusted coefficients

An unadjusted coefficient comes from a model with only the predictor of interest. An adjusted coefficient comes from a model that also includes other variables.

\[ \text{Unadjusted model:}\qquad Y_i=\alpha_0+\alpha_1X_i+u_i \]
\[ \text{Adjusted model:}\qquad Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\beta_2Z_i+\varepsilon_i \]

The coefficient \(\alpha_1\) and the coefficient \(\beta_1\) can differ because the adjusted model compares observations at the same value of \(Z\), whereas the unadjusted model does not.

Confounding

Confounding occurs when the association between an exposure \(X\) and an outcome \(Y\) is mixed with the effect of another variable \(Z\). A confounder is typically related to both the exposure and the outcome, and is not simply a consequence of the exposure.

Confounder

A confounder is a variable associated with the exposure and the outcome that can distort the exposure-outcome association if not appropriately controlled.

SO
Sophia

Can you give a simple example?

MR
Mr. R

Suppose coffee drinking is associated with heart disease, but smoking is more common among coffee drinkers and also affects heart disease risk. Smoking may confound the coffee-heart disease association.

Omitted-variable bias

If an important confounder is omitted from the model, the coefficient of the exposure may be biased. In a simple two-predictor situation, if the true model is:

\[ Y=\beta_0+\beta_1X+\beta_2Z+\varepsilon \]

but we fit a model omitting \(Z\), the estimated coefficient of \(X\) can absorb some of the effect of \(Z\), especially when \(X\) and \(Z\) are associated.

\[ \text{Omitted-variable bias depends on the association between }X\text{ and }Z \text{ and on the effect of }Z\text{ on }Y. \]

Adjustment is not automatic truth

Adding variables to a regression model does not automatically make it causal or correct. Adjustment helps only when the variables are appropriate to control for. Adjusting for mediators, colliders, or badly measured variables can create problems.

EM
Emma

So more variables is not always better?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A model with more variables can be less interpretable, less stable, or even more biased if the wrong variables are adjusted for.

Multicollinearity

Multicollinearity occurs when predictors are strongly correlated with each other. It does not necessarily make predictions impossible, but it can make individual coefficients unstable and difficult to interpret.

\[ X_1\approx a+bX_2 \]

If two predictors contain almost the same information, the model struggles to separate their individual contributions.

OL
Oliver

So the model may still predict well but the coefficient estimates become unreliable?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. Prediction and coefficient interpretation are different goals.

Categorical predictors

Multiple regression can include categorical predictors by using indicator variables. For a binary group variable, define:

\[ G_i= \begin{cases} 1,& \text{if observation }i\text{ is in the group}\\ 0,& \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

The coefficient of \(G_i\) represents the adjusted mean difference between the group coded 1 and the reference group coded 0, holding other predictors fixed.

Model building is scientific, not mechanical

Multiple regression is not just about adding every available variable. Variable choice should be guided by the research question, study design, causal reasoning, prior knowledge, measurement quality and sample size.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Multiple regression models the mean of \(Y\) using several predictors.
  • An adjusted coefficient is interpreted while holding other model predictors fixed.
  • Confounding can distort an exposure-outcome association.
  • Omitting an important confounder can bias the coefficient of interest.
  • Adjustment is useful only when the adjusted variables are appropriate.
  • Multicollinearity makes individual coefficients unstable.
  • Categorical predictors can be represented using indicator variables.
  • Regression modelling requires scientific judgement, not only algebra.
Detailed theoretical notes

Multiple regression and confounding

1. From simple to multiple regression

Simple linear regression uses one predictor. Multiple regression extends the same idea to several predictors. The goal is still to model the conditional mean of the outcome, but now the conditioning set includes more variables.

\[ E(Y\mid X_1,X_2,\ldots,X_p) = \beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\beta_2X_2+\cdots+\beta_pX_p \]

2. Population model

For observation \(i\), the multiple linear regression model is:

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_{1i}+\beta_2X_{2i}+\cdots+\beta_pX_{pi}+\varepsilon_i \]

The fitted version is:

\[ \hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_{1i}+b_2x_{2i}+\cdots+b_px_{pi} \]

The residual is:

\[ e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i \]

3. Interpreting an adjusted coefficient

The coefficient \(\beta_j\) is interpreted as the expected change in the mean of \(Y\) for a one-unit increase in \(X_j\), holding the other predictors in the model fixed.

\[ \beta_j = \text{change in }E(Y\mid X_1,\ldots,X_p) \text{ for a one-unit increase in }X_j, \text{ with other predictors fixed} \]

This “holding fixed” interpretation is mathematical. Whether it corresponds to a meaningful real-world comparison depends on the data and design.

4. Two-predictor example

Suppose:

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\beta_2Z_i+\varepsilon_i \]

Then \(\beta_1\) compares observations with different values of \(X\) but the same value of \(Z\). Similarly, \(\beta_2\) compares observations with different values of \(Z\) but the same value of \(X\).

5. Confounding concept

A variable \(Z\) may confound the relationship between exposure \(X\) and outcome \(Y\) when:

  • \(Z\) is associated with \(X\),
  • \(Z\) is associated with \(Y\),
  • \(Z\) is not merely a consequence of \(X\) on the causal pathway from \(X\) to \(Y\).
Confounding

Confounding is mixing of effects. The observed association between \(X\) and \(Y\) partly reflects the influence of \(Z\).

6. Omitted-variable bias formula intuition

Suppose the true model is:

\[ Y=\beta_0+\beta_1X+\beta_2Z+\varepsilon \]

If we omit \(Z\) and fit:

\[ Y=\alpha_0+\alpha_1X+u \]

then the simple regression slope \(\alpha_1\) can differ from the true adjusted effect \(\beta_1\). In a linear setting, the bias has the structure:

\[ \alpha_1-\beta_1 = \beta_2 \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Z)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)} \]

This expression shows two requirements for omitted-variable bias: \(Z\) must affect \(Y\), and \(Z\) must be associated with \(X\).

\[ \beta_2=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{no omitted-variable bias from }Z \]
\[ \operatorname{Cov}(X,Z)=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{no omitted-variable bias from }Z \]

7. Adjusted versus unadjusted interpretation

ModelCoefficient of \(X\)Interpretation
\(Y=\alpha_0+\alpha_1X+u\)\(\alpha_1\)Unadjusted association between \(X\) and \(Y\)
\(Y=\beta_0+\beta_1X+\beta_2Z+\varepsilon\)\(\beta_1\)Association between \(X\) and \(Y\) adjusted for \(Z\)

8. Overadjustment and bad adjustment

Not every variable should be adjusted for. Problems can arise when we adjust for:

  • Mediators: variables on the causal pathway from exposure to outcome.
  • Colliders: variables influenced by two other variables, which can create artificial associations when conditioned on.
  • Post-outcome variables: variables measured after the outcome or affected by the outcome.
  • Poorly measured variables: noisy adjustment variables can leave residual confounding.
Important warning

Adjustment is not a magic cure. Regression coefficients are only as meaningful as the design, measurements and modelling assumptions behind them.

9. Multicollinearity

Multicollinearity means predictors are strongly linearly related. For example:

\[ X_2\approx a+bX_1 \]

When predictors are highly correlated, the model has difficulty separating their individual associations with \(Y\). This can lead to large standard errors and unstable coefficients.

A common diagnostic concept is the variance inflation factor:

\[ \operatorname{VIF}_j=\frac{1}{1-R_j^2} \]

Here, \(R_j^2\) is obtained by regressing \(X_j\) on the other predictors. If \(R_j^2\) is close to 1, then \(X_j\) is highly predictable from the other predictors, and the VIF becomes large.

10. Categorical predictors and indicator variables

A binary categorical predictor can be represented as an indicator variable:

\[ G_i= \begin{cases} 1,& \text{group A}\\ 0,& \text{reference group} \end{cases} \]

In the model:

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1G_i+\varepsilon_i \]

\(\beta_0\) is the mean for the reference group, and \(\beta_1\) is the mean difference between group A and the reference group.

With additional covariates:

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1G_i+\beta_2X_i+\varepsilon_i \]

\(\beta_1\) becomes the adjusted mean difference between groups, holding \(X\) fixed.

11. Matrix form

Multiple regression is often written compactly in matrix form:

\[ \mathbf{Y}=\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}+\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \]

The least-squares estimator is:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{Y} \]

This formula requires \(\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{X}\) to be invertible. Severe multicollinearity can make this matrix nearly singular, causing unstable estimates.

12. Common mistakes

  • Interpreting adjusted coefficients as unadjusted effects: each coefficient is conditional on the other variables in the model.
  • Thinking more variables always improve causal interpretation: wrong adjustment can increase bias.
  • Ignoring confounding: omitted confounders can distort the coefficient of interest.
  • Ignoring multicollinearity: strongly correlated predictors make individual coefficients unstable.
  • Assuming regression proves causation: causal interpretation requires design and assumptions.
  • Forgetting the reference category: categorical predictor coefficients depend on coding.
Notes summary
  • Multiple regression models \(E(Y\mid X_1,\ldots,X_p)\).
  • An adjusted coefficient is interpreted holding other predictors fixed.
  • Confounding occurs when another variable distorts an exposure-outcome association.
  • Omitted-variable bias depends on the effect of the omitted variable and its association with the included predictor.
  • Multicollinearity inflates uncertainty and destabilises coefficients.
  • Categorical variables enter regression through indicator variables.
  • Good modelling requires subject-matter reasoning.

Practice exercises

Multiple regression and confounding

Exercise 1
Write a multiple regression model
Easy+

Write a multiple regression model with outcome \(Y\) and predictors \(X_1\), \(X_2\), and \(X_3\).

Answer

\(Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_{1i}+\beta_2X_{2i}+\beta_3X_{3i}+\varepsilon_i\).

Exercise 2
Interpret an adjusted slope
Easy+

In a model predicting blood pressure from age and BMI, the coefficient for BMI is 1.8. Interpret it.

Answer

Holding age fixed, a one-unit increase in BMI is associated with a 1.8-unit higher mean blood pressure, according to the model.

Exercise 3
Identify confounding
Medium+

A variable is associated with both exposure and outcome, and is not caused by the exposure. What role might it play?

Answer

It may be a confounder. If omitted, it can distort the estimated exposure-outcome association.

Exercise 4
Omitted-variable bias calculation
Medium+

If \(\beta_2=4\), \(\operatorname{Cov}(X,Z)=6\), and \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=12\), calculate the omitted-variable bias.

Answer

Bias \(=\beta_2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Z)/\operatorname{Var}(X)=4(6)/12=2\).

Exercise 5
When omitted-variable bias is zero
Medium+

Give two situations where omitting \(Z\) does not bias the coefficient of \(X\) in the simple omitted-variable formula.

Answer

The bias is zero if \(\beta_2=0\), meaning \(Z\) has no effect on \(Y\), or if \(\operatorname{Cov}(X,Z)=0\), meaning \(Z\) is not associated with \(X\).

Exercise 6
Interpret VIF
Hard+

If \(R_j^2=0.80\), calculate \(\operatorname{VIF}_j\).

Answer

\(\operatorname{VIF}_j=1/(1-R_j^2)=1/(1-0.80)=1/0.20=5\).

Exercise 7
Indicator variable
Hard+

In \(Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1G_i+\varepsilon_i\), where \(G_i=1\) for treatment and \(0\) for control, what does \(\beta_1\) represent?

Answer

\(\beta_1\) is the mean difference between the treatment group and the control group. The control group is the reference category.

Exercise 8
Why more variables is not always better
Hard+

Explain why simply adding every available variable to a regression model can be problematic.

Answer

Adding inappropriate variables can cause overadjustment, collider bias, instability, multicollinearity and loss of interpretability. Variable choice should be guided by the research question and study design.

Module 5 · Lesson 5.1

Simple linear regression

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~55 min · First regression lesson
📍 Scene — statistics classroom, moving from comparison to modelling
EM
Emma

So far we compared means, proportions and categories. Regression feels different. Is regression just drawing a line through points?

MR
Mr. R

Drawing a line is the visual part. Regression is deeper: it models how the mean of an outcome changes with an explanatory variable.

OL
Oliver

Mean of an outcome? Not every individual value?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Simple linear regression models the conditional mean of \(Y\) given \(X\). It does not claim every person follows the line exactly.

The basic regression question

Simple linear regression studies the relationship between one quantitative outcome \(Y\) and one explanatory variable \(X\). The word “simple” means there is only one explanatory variable. The word “linear” means the model assumes a straight-line relationship in the mean.

\[ E(Y\mid X=x)=\beta_0+\beta_1x \]

This equation says: among all observations with \(X=x\), the average value of \(Y\) is modelled as \(\beta_0+\beta_1x\).

JA
James

Why write \(E(Y\mid X=x)\) instead of just \(Y\)?

MR
Mr. R

Because actual observations vary around the line. The line represents the average outcome at a given value of \(X\), not every individual outcome.

The statistical model

The observed value \(Y_i\) is written as a fitted mean plus random error:

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\varepsilon_i \]

The error term \(\varepsilon_i\) represents everything that makes observation \(i\) differ from the conditional mean: measurement noise, individual variation, omitted factors and random fluctuation.

SO
Sophia

So \(\varepsilon_i\) is not necessarily a mistake?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. In regression, “error” usually means unexplained deviation from the modelled mean, not a data entry error.

Intercept and slope

The intercept \(\beta_0\) is the modelled mean of \(Y\) when \(X=0\). The slope \(\beta_1\) is the expected change in the mean of \(Y\) for a one-unit increase in \(X\).

\[ \beta_1=\text{change in }E(Y\mid X)\text{ for one-unit increase in }X \]
EM
Emma

If \(X\) is age and \(Y\) is blood pressure, a slope of 0.7 means blood pressure increases by 0.7 for each extra year of age?

MR
Mr. R

More carefully: the modelled mean blood pressure is 0.7 units higher for each one-year increase in age, on average, within the scope of the data.

Fitted values and residuals

After fitting the model, we get estimated coefficients \(b_0\) and \(b_1\). The fitted value for observation \(i\) is:

\[ \hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_i \]

The residual is the difference between the observed outcome and the fitted value:

\[ e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i \]
OL
Oliver

If the observed value is above the line, the residual is positive?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. A positive residual means the observed value is higher than the fitted value. A negative residual means it is lower.

Least squares idea

The usual regression line is chosen by least squares. It chooses \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) to minimise the sum of squared residuals.

\[ \operatorname{SSE}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2 \]

Squaring prevents positive and negative residuals from cancelling and gives larger penalties to larger errors.

JA
James

Will we derive the least squares formulas?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. Lesson 5.2 is devoted to that derivation. Today we build the meaning and interpretation first.

Association is not causation

A regression slope describes an association in the fitted model. It does not automatically prove that changing \(X\) would cause \(Y\) to change. Causal interpretation requires study design and assumptions, not just a regression line.

SO
Sophia

So if ice-cream sales and drowning incidents are positively associated, regression alone does not mean ice cream causes drowning?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A third factor, such as hot weather, may explain both. Regression is powerful, but interpretation must be careful.

Prediction and explanation

Simple linear regression can be used for prediction, explanation, or description. The same fitted line can serve different goals, but the interpretation changes. Predictive use asks how accurately \(Y\) can be predicted. Explanatory use asks how \(Y\) changes with \(X\), often requiring deeper assumptions.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Simple linear regression models the mean of \(Y\) conditional on one explanatory variable \(X\).
  • The population regression model is \(Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\varepsilon_i\).
  • The intercept is the modelled mean when \(X=0\), if that value is meaningful.
  • The slope is the expected change in mean \(Y\) for a one-unit increase in \(X\).
  • Fitted values are predicted means from the fitted line.
  • Residuals are observed minus fitted values.
  • Least squares chooses the line that minimises squared residuals.
  • A regression slope is not automatically causal.
Detailed theoretical notes

Simple linear regression

1. Regression as conditional mean modelling

Simple linear regression models how the mean of a quantitative outcome changes with one explanatory variable. The central object is not the value of \(Y\) for every individual, but the conditional expectation of \(Y\) given \(X=x\).

\[ E(Y\mid X=x)=\beta_0+\beta_1x \]

This is the population regression line. It is usually unknown and must be estimated from sample data.

2. Population model

The simple linear regression model is:

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\varepsilon_i,\qquad i=1,\ldots,n \]

where:

  • \(Y_i\) is the outcome for observation \(i\),
  • \(X_i\) is the explanatory variable for observation \(i\),
  • \(\beta_0\) is the intercept,
  • \(\beta_1\) is the slope,
  • \(\varepsilon_i\) is the random error term.

The usual mean-zero error assumption is:

\[ E(\varepsilon_i\mid X_i)=0 \]

This implies:

\[ E(Y_i\mid X_i)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i \]

3. Interpreting the intercept

The intercept is:

\[ \beta_0=E(Y\mid X=0) \]

This interpretation is meaningful only when \(X=0\) is within the relevant range and makes scientific sense. If \(X=0\) is impossible or far outside the data range, the intercept may be mathematically necessary but not substantively meaningful.

Interpretation warning

Do not over-interpret the intercept when \(X=0\) is outside the observed data range or has no real meaning.

4. Interpreting the slope

The slope is the change in the conditional mean of \(Y\) for a one-unit increase in \(X\):

\[ \beta_1= E(Y\mid X=x+1)-E(Y\mid X=x) \]

Using the model:

\[ E(Y\mid X=x+1)=\beta_0+\beta_1(x+1) \]
\[ E(Y\mid X=x)=\beta_0+\beta_1x \]

Subtracting gives:

\[ E(Y\mid X=x+1)-E(Y\mid X=x)=\beta_1 \]

5. Fitted regression line

Because \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) are unknown, we estimate them from data. Let the estimates be \(b_0\) and \(b_1\). The fitted regression line is:

\[ \hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_i \]

The fitted value \(\hat{y}_i\) is the model's estimated mean outcome at \(x_i\).

6. Residuals

The residual for observation \(i\) is:

\[ e_i=y_i-\hat{y}_i \]

Residuals show how far observed values are from the fitted line. They are sample quantities, while the model errors \(\varepsilon_i\) are population-level theoretical quantities.

QuantityMeaningObserved?
\(\varepsilon_i\)True error around population regression lineNo
\(e_i\)Residual around fitted regression lineYes

7. Least squares criterion

Least squares chooses the fitted line that minimises the sum of squared residuals:

\[ \operatorname{SSE}(b_0,b_1) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-b_0-b_1x_i)^2 \]

The least squares estimates are the values of \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) that minimise this function.

\[ (b_0,b_1)= \underset{a,c}{\operatorname{arg\,min}} \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-a-cx_i)^2 \]

Lesson 5.2 derives the closed-form solutions. For now, the conceptual idea is that the fitted line makes squared vertical deviations as small as possible.

8. Regression and correlation

Correlation and simple linear regression are related but not identical.

CorrelationSimple linear regression
Measures strength and direction of linear associationModels mean outcome as a function of \(X\)
Symmetric: correlation of \(X,Y\) equals correlation of \(Y,X\)Asymmetric: \(Y\) is outcome, \(X\) is predictor
Unit-freeSlope has units of \(Y\) per unit of \(X\)
No fitted prediction equation by itselfProduces fitted values and residuals

9. Assumptions of simple linear regression

Common assumptions include:

  • Linearity: the conditional mean of \(Y\) changes linearly with \(X\).
  • Independence: observations are independent unless the design says otherwise.
  • Mean-zero errors: \(E(\varepsilon_i\mid X_i)=0\).
  • Constant variance: the variability of errors is constant across values of \(X\).
  • Approximate normality of errors: mainly needed for small-sample inference, not for defining the line.

10. Prediction versus causal interpretation

A regression model can be used for prediction without causal interpretation. A causal interpretation of \(\beta_1\) requires stronger assumptions, such as no uncontrolled confounding and an appropriate study design.

Causality warning

A statistically significant slope does not automatically mean that \(X\) causes \(Y\). Association, prediction and causation are different goals.

11. Extrapolation

Regression lines should not be trusted far outside the range of observed \(X\)-values. A relationship that is roughly linear inside the observed range may not remain linear outside it.

\[ \text{Prediction outside the observed range of }X \text{ is extrapolation.} \]

12. Common mistakes

  • Thinking regression predicts every individual exactly: it models the conditional mean.
  • Calling residuals “errors” without context: residuals are observed deviations from the fitted line.
  • Interpreting the intercept when \(X=0\) is meaningless: check the data range.
  • Assuming significance implies causation: causal interpretation needs design and assumptions.
  • Extrapolating far outside the observed \(X\)-range: the fitted line may not remain valid.
  • Ignoring residual patterns: patterns can reveal non-linearity, unequal variance or outliers.

  • The fitted line is \(\hat{y}_i=b_0+b_1x_i\).

Practice exercises

Simple linear regression

Exercise 1
Interpret a slope
Easy+

A fitted regression line is \(\hat{y}=12+3x\). Interpret the slope.

Answer

For each one-unit increase in \(x\), the fitted mean of \(Y\) increases by 3 units, on average, within the scope of the data.

Exercise 2
Calculate a fitted value
Easy+

Using \(\hat{y}=12+3x\), calculate \(\hat{y}\) when \(x=5\).

Answer

\(\hat{y}=12+3(5)=12+15=27\).

Exercise 3
Calculate a residual
Medium+

If the observed value is \(y=31\) and the fitted value is \(\hat{y}=27\), calculate the residual.

Answer

The residual is \(e=y-\hat{y}=31-27=4\). The observed value is 4 units above the fitted line.

Exercise 4
Interpret intercept carefully
Medium+

A model predicts blood pressure from age and has an intercept of 62. Explain why the intercept may not be scientifically meaningful.

Answer

The intercept is the fitted mean blood pressure when age is 0. If the study includes adults, age 0 is outside the relevant data range, so the intercept is mathematically part of the line but not substantively meaningful.

Exercise 5
SSE calculation
Medium+

Residuals are \(2,-1,3\). Calculate the sum of squared residuals.

Answer

\(\operatorname{SSE}=2^2+(-1)^2+3^2=4+1+9=14\).

Exercise 6
Conditional mean interpretation
Hard+

Explain why \(E(Y\mid X=x)=\beta_0+\beta_1x\) is not the same as saying every observation with \(X=x\) equals \(\beta_0+\beta_1x\).

Answer

The equation describes the conditional mean of \(Y\) at \(X=x\). Individual observations vary around this mean because of the error term \(\varepsilon_i\).

Exercise 7
Correlation versus regression
Hard+

Give one difference between correlation and simple linear regression.

Answer

Correlation is symmetric and unit-free. Simple linear regression treats one variable as the outcome and one as the predictor, and the slope has units of \(Y\) per unit of \(X\).

Exercise 8
Association versus causation
Hard+

A regression slope between exercise hours and health score is positive. Why should we avoid immediately claiming exercise caused the higher health score?

Answer

The association may be affected by confounding, selection bias or reverse causation. Causal interpretation requires appropriate design and assumptions, not just a positive regression slope.

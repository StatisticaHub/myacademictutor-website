Shape, skewness and outliers

Mr. R · ~22 min · Builds on centre and spread
📍 Scene — the class is comparing two histograms
EM
Emma

We have already learned the mean, median, range, IQR and standard deviation. Is that enough to describe a dataset properly?

MR
Mr. R

Not always. Centre tells us where the data is located. Spread tells us how variable it is. But shape tells us how the values are arranged across the distribution.

OL
Oliver

What do you mean by arranged? Like whether the histogram is balanced or stretched?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A distribution might be symmetric, skewed to the right, skewed to the left, have one peak, have two peaks, or contain extreme values. These features change how we interpret centre and spread.

Why shape matters

Suppose two classes both have a mean exam score of 60. In one class, most students score around 60. In another class, half score near 30 and half score near 90. The same mean hides very different stories.

Core idea

A good descriptive summary usually answers four questions: where is the data centred, how spread out is it, what shape does it have, and are there unusual observations?

JA
James

So a histogram is not just a picture. It helps us decide whether the mean and standard deviation are appropriate?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. If the distribution is roughly symmetric, mean and standard deviation can be very informative. If the data is strongly skewed or has outliers, the median and IQR may describe the typical value more honestly.

Symmetry and skewness

A distribution is roughly symmetric when the left and right sides look like mirror images around the centre. It is right-skewed when a long tail extends toward larger values. It is left-skewed when a long tail extends toward smaller values.

ShapeTail directionTypical relationshipExample
SymmetricBalanced tailsMean ≈ MedianAdult height in a narrow age group
Right-skewedLong right tailMean > MedianIncome, hospital length of stay
Left-skewedLong left tailMean < MedianEasy test scores with many high marks
SO
Sophia

Why does the mean move toward the tail?

MR
Mr. R

Because the mean uses the actual numerical size of every value. Very large values pull the mean upward. Very small values pull it downward. The median is based only on position, so it is more resistant.

Outliers are not automatically mistakes

An outlier is an observation that lies far away from the main body of the data. It may be a data-entry error, but it may also be a genuine and important case.

Warning

Never delete an outlier just because it looks unusual. First ask whether it is impossible, wrongly recorded, outside the target population, or a genuine extreme observation.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Shape describes the overall pattern of a distribution, not just its centre or spread.
  • Right-skewed data usually has mean greater than median; left-skewed data usually has mean less than median.
  • Outliers strongly affect the mean, range and standard deviation, but affect the median and IQR much less.
  • Graphs and numerical summaries should be interpreted together.
Detailed notes

Shape, skewness and outliers

1. What do we mean by the shape of a distribution?

The shape of a distribution refers to the overall visual and numerical pattern formed by the data values. After describing centre and spread, shape is the next major descriptive feature. Shape tells us whether values are concentrated around one main centre, whether there are long tails, whether the distribution is balanced, and whether some observations are unusually far from the rest.

Definition — distribution shape

The distribution shape describes how data values are arranged across their possible range, including symmetry, skewness, peaks, tails and outlying observations.

In descriptive statistics, shape is usually studied using:

  • histograms, for numerical data;
  • boxplots, for spread, median and possible outliers;
  • density curves, for smooth distributional patterns;
  • summary statistics, especially mean, median, quartiles, IQR and standard deviation.

2. Symmetric distributions

A distribution is approximately symmetric if its left and right sides are similar in shape and length. In a perfectly symmetric distribution, the centre divides the distribution into two mirror-image halves.

Symmetry

A distribution is symmetric when values below the centre and values above the centre occur in a broadly balanced way.

For symmetric numerical data:

  • the mean and median are usually close;
  • the mean is often a useful description of centre;
  • the standard deviation is often useful as a description of spread;
  • histograms tend to look balanced around the middle.
Symmetric distribution: mean ≈ median

Examples that are often approximately symmetric include adult height within a specific sex and age group, repeated laboratory measurement errors under stable conditions, and many exam-score distributions where the paper has moderate difficulty.

3. Skewed distributions

A distribution is skewed when one tail is longer or more stretched than the other. The tail direction determines the name of the skew.

TypeDescriptionMean–median patternTypical examples
Right-skewedMost values are low or moderate, with a few very high valuesMean > MedianIncome, waiting times, hospital stay length, house prices
Left-skewedMost values are high, with a few unusually low valuesMean < MedianEasy exam scores, age at retirement in some groups
Approximately symmetricBalanced tails on both sidesMean ≈ MedianHeight, many measurement errors

Right-skewness

Right-skewness means the long tail points to the right, toward larger numerical values. Most observations may be relatively small, but a few unusually large observations stretch the distribution to the right.

Right-skewed data

In right-skewed data, the mean is often larger than the median because the large values in the right tail pull the mean upward.

For example, consider hospital length of stay. Many patients may stay 1 to 4 days, but a few patients may stay 30, 50 or 100 days. The median stay may remain small, but the mean becomes larger because it is affected by the long right tail.

Left-skewness

Left-skewness means the long tail points to the left, toward smaller numerical values. Most observations may be high, with a few unusually low observations pulling the mean downward.

Left-skewed data

In left-skewed data, the mean is often smaller than the median because the small values in the left tail pull the mean downward.

An example is an easy exam where most students score between 75 and 100, but a few students score very low. The median may remain high, while the mean is pulled lower by the few low scores.

4. Why the mean follows the tail

The mean is calculated using the actual value of every observation:

x̄ = (x₁ + x₂ + ⋯ + xₙ) / n

Because every numerical value contributes to the total sum, extreme observations have a direct effect on the mean. If one value is extremely large, it increases the total and therefore increases the mean. If one value is extremely small, it decreases the total and therefore decreases the mean.

The median, in contrast, is based on the ordered position of the values. It depends on which observation lies in the middle, not on how extreme the largest or smallest values are.

Step idea: why one extreme value changes the mean more than the median
1
Data: 4, 5, 6, 7, 8
Mean = 6 and median = 6. The data are balanced around the centre.
2
Replace 8 by 80: 4, 5, 6, 7, 80
The largest value becomes extreme. The middle value is still 6, so the median stays 6.
3
New mean = (4 + 5 + 6 + 7 + 80)/5 = 20.4
The mean is pulled strongly upward because the extreme value enters the total sum.
4
Conclusion: median is resistant; mean is non-resistant
This is why median and IQR are often preferred for strongly skewed data.

5. Outliers

An outlier is a value that lies unusually far away from the rest of the data. Outliers matter because they can influence descriptive summaries, distort graphs, and sometimes reveal important scientific or practical information.

Definition — outlier

An outlier is an observation that is unusually distant from the main pattern of the data. It may be an error, a rare case, or a valid but extreme observation.

Possible causes of outliers

  • Data-entry error: a height of 1700 cm instead of 170 cm.
  • Measurement error: a faulty machine gives an impossible reading.
  • Different population: an adult included accidentally in a children’s dataset.
  • Genuine rare observation: an unusually long hospital stay due to severe complications.
  • Natural variability: some observations are simply far from the average.

6. The 1.5 × IQR rule for detecting possible outliers

A common descriptive rule uses the first quartile \(Q_1\), third quartile \(Q_3\), and interquartile range:

IQR = Q₃ − Q₁

The lower and upper fences are:

Lower fence = Q₁ − 1.5 × IQR
Upper fence = Q₃ + 1.5 × IQR

Values below the lower fence or above the upper fence are often flagged as possible outliers. The phrase “possible outliers” is important: the rule identifies observations worth checking, not observations that must automatically be removed.

Interpretation rule

The 1.5 × IQR rule is a screening tool. It tells us which observations deserve attention; it does not decide whether those observations are errors.

7. Shape and choice of descriptive summary

The shape of a distribution affects which numerical summaries are most useful.

Distribution shapeBetter centreBetter spreadWhy?
Roughly symmetric, no strong outliersMeanStandard deviationAll values contribute meaningfully and extremes are not dominating.
Strongly skewedMedianIQRMedian and IQR are resistant to long tails.
Contains serious outliersMedianIQRMean and SD may be distorted by extreme values.
Bimodal or multimodalMay need subgroup summariesSeparate summaries may be neededOne centre may hide two or more groups.

8. Unimodal, bimodal and multimodal distributions

A mode is a peak or most common region in a distribution. A distribution can be:

  • unimodal: one clear peak;
  • bimodal: two clear peaks;
  • multimodal: more than two peaks.

Bimodality is important because it may suggest that the dataset contains two different subgroups. For example, a distribution of adult heights may become bimodal if men and women are pooled together. A distribution of test scores may become bimodal if one group was taught the material and another was not.

Descriptive caution

If a distribution has two clear peaks, reporting one overall mean may be misleading. It may be better to describe the subgroups separately.

9. Kurtosis: tail weight and peakedness

Kurtosis is a more advanced shape measure related to tail behaviour. In introductory descriptive statistics, it is enough to understand that some distributions have heavier tails than others. Heavy-tailed distributions produce extreme values more often than light-tailed distributions.

Excess kurtosis = 0 for a normal distribution

A distribution with heavier tails than a normal distribution has more extreme observations than we would expect under normality. This is common in finance, environmental measurements, hospital stays and biological variables with rare but large values.

10. Careful descriptive writing

When writing a descriptive interpretation, avoid simply listing statistics. Explain what the shape tells the reader.

Weak wordingStronger descriptive wording
The mean is 48 and median is 32.The mean is larger than the median, suggesting a right-skewed distribution with some high values.
The range is large.The large range appears to be driven by one unusually high observation, so the IQR gives a more stable summary of the main body of the data.
There is an outlier.One observation lies beyond the upper 1.5 × IQR fence and should be checked before deciding whether it is an error or a genuine extreme value.
Summary
  • Shape completes the descriptive picture after centre and spread.
  • Skewness describes tail direction; the mean is usually pulled toward the tail.
  • Outliers should be investigated, not automatically removed.
  • Median and IQR are usually better for skewed or outlier-prone data.
  • Bimodal data may need subgroup analysis rather than one overall summary.

Practice exercises

Shape, skewness and outliers

Five detailed exercises. Try each one before revealing the solution.

Exercise 1
Identify the shape from mean and median
Easy+

For each dataset summary, decide whether the distribution is likely symmetric, right-skewed or left-skewed:

  1. Mean = 72, median = 71
  2. Mean = £58,000, median = £34,000
  3. Mean = 46, median = 61
  4. Mean = 12.1, median = 12.0
Answer
  1. Approximately symmetric because mean and median are close.
  2. Right-skewed because the mean is much larger than the median, suggesting high values in the right tail.
  3. Left-skewed because the mean is smaller than the median, suggesting low values in the left tail.
  4. Approximately symmetric because the mean and median are nearly identical.
Exercise 2
Outlier fences using the IQR rule
Medium+

A dataset has Q₁ = 18 and Q₃ = 34.

  1. Calculate the IQR.
  2. Calculate the lower and upper outlier fences using the 1.5 × IQR rule.
  3. Would the values 2, 7, 38 and 61 be flagged as possible outliers?
Answer
  1. IQR = Q₃ − Q₁ = 34 − 18 = 16.
  2. Lower fence = 18 − 1.5(16) = 18 − 24 = −6. Upper fence = 34 + 24 = 58.
  3. 2, 7 and 38 are not beyond the fences, so they are not flagged. 61 is above 58, so 61 is flagged as a possible outlier.
Exercise 3
Choosing centre and spread
Medium+

A hospital reports patient length of stay. Most patients stay 1–5 days, but a few patients stay more than 60 days. Which measures of centre and spread should be reported, and why?

Answer

This distribution is likely strongly right-skewed. The median should be reported as the measure of centre and the IQR as the measure of spread. The mean and standard deviation may still be useful as supplementary information, but they can be strongly influenced by the few very long stays. A good report might say: “Length of stay was right-skewed, so the median and IQR are used to describe the typical stay and variability.”

Exercise 4
Mean, median and an extreme value
Medium+

Consider the dataset: 10, 12, 13, 15, 15, 16, 17. Then an additional value of 80 is added.

  1. Find the original mean and median.
  2. Find the new mean and median after adding 80.
  3. Explain what this shows about resistance.
Answer
  1. Original mean = 98/7 = 14.0. Original median = 15.
  2. New mean = 178/8 = 22.25. New median = average of 15 and 15 = 15.
  3. The mean changed substantially, but the median did not change. This shows that the median is resistant to extreme values, while the mean is not.
Exercise 5
Writing a descriptive interpretation
Hard+

A dataset of annual household income has mean £51,800, median £32,400, Q₁ £22,100, Q₃ £45,900, minimum £8,000 and maximum £410,000. Write a careful descriptive interpretation.

Answer

The income distribution appears strongly right-skewed. The mean (£51,800) is much larger than the median (£32,400), suggesting that a small number of high-income households pull the mean upward. The middle 50% of households lie between £22,100 and £45,900, giving an IQR of £23,800. Because the maximum (£410,000) is far from the upper quartile, there may be high-income outliers or a long right tail. For describing a typical household, the median and IQR are more appropriate than the mean and range.

Standardisation and z-scores

Mr. R · ~24 min · Builds on mean and standard deviation
📍 Scene — comparing two exam results from different classes
EM
Emma

I scored 78 in statistics and 82 in biology. So biology was definitely my better performance, right?

MR
Mr. R

Not necessarily. A raw score only tells us the value itself. To judge performance, we also need to know the average and spread in each exam.

OL
Oliver

So 78 could be excellent if everyone else scored around 55, but average if everyone scored around 80?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. That is the idea behind standardisation. We transform a raw value into a z-score, which tells us how many standard deviations the value is from the mean.

The z-score formula

A z-score measures a value’s position relative to the mean, using the standard deviation as the unit of measurement.

z = (x − mean) / standard deviation
Definition — z-score

A z-score tells us how many standard deviations a value lies above or below the mean. Positive z-scores are above the mean; negative z-scores are below the mean.

JA
James

If my z-score is 2, does that mean I am exactly two marks above the mean?

MR
Mr. R

No. It means you are two standard deviations above the mean. If the standard deviation is 5 marks, that is 10 marks above the mean. If the standard deviation is 12 marks, that is 24 marks above the mean.

Why standardisation is useful

Standardisation allows us to compare values that were originally measured on different scales. This is especially useful when variables have different units, different averages, or different variability.

QuestionRaw value problemZ-score solution
Which exam performance was stronger?Exams may have different difficulty and spread.Compare each score relative to its own exam distribution.
Is a blood pressure value unusually high?Raw value lacks context.Compare it with the population mean and SD.
Which patient measurement is more unusual?Units differ across variables.Put measurements on the same standard scale.

Interpretation

A z-score of 0 means the value is exactly at the mean. A z-score of +1 means one standard deviation above the mean. A z-score of −1 means one standard deviation below the mean.

SO
Sophia

Can z-scores help detect outliers?

MR
Mr. R

They can flag unusual values. In many roughly symmetric datasets, values with z-scores beyond about ±2 are unusual, and beyond about ±3 are very unusual. But for skewed data, z-scores must be interpreted carefully.

Important caution

Z-scores rely on the mean and standard deviation. If the data are strongly skewed or contain severe outliers, the mean and SD may themselves be distorted.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • A z-score converts a raw value into a standardised value.
  • It tells us how many standard deviations a value lies from the mean.
  • Z-scores allow comparison across different units and scales.
  • Large absolute z-scores may indicate unusual values, but context and shape matter.
Detailed notes

Standardisation and z-scores

1. Why raw values are not always enough

A raw data value tells us what was observed, but it does not tell us whether that value is typical, high, low, or unusual. To interpret a value properly, we need to compare it with the distribution from which it came.

For example, a test score of 75 may be excellent in a very difficult exam with mean 50 and standard deviation 10. But it may be ordinary in an easy exam with mean 73 and standard deviation 8. The raw score alone is incomplete.

Core idea

Standardisation gives a raw value context by measuring its distance from the mean in standard deviation units.

2. The z-score

The most common standardised score is the z-score. For a population mean \( \mu \) and population standard deviation \( \sigma \), the population z-score is:

z = (x − μ) / σ

For sample data, where we usually use the sample mean \( \bar{x} \) and sample standard deviation \( s \), the sample z-score is:

z = (x − x̄) / s
QuantityMeaning
xThe raw value being standardised
μ or x̄The mean of the population or sample
σ or sThe standard deviation of the population or sample
zThe number of standard deviations from the mean

3. Interpretation of z-scores

The sign of the z-score tells us direction. The magnitude tells us distance.

Z-scoreInterpretation
z = 0The value is exactly equal to the mean.
z = +1The value is one standard deviation above the mean.
z = −1The value is one standard deviation below the mean.
z = +2.5The value is 2.5 standard deviations above the mean.
z = −3The value is three standard deviations below the mean.
Descriptive wording

A z-score should be described in words: “This value is 1.8 standard deviations above the mean,” not simply “z = 1.8.”

4. Step-by-step calculation

Suppose the mean systolic blood pressure in a group is 120 mmHg and the standard deviation is 15 mmHg. A patient has systolic blood pressure 150 mmHg.

z = (150 − 120) / 15 = 30 / 15 = 2

The patient’s value is two standard deviations above the mean. In a roughly symmetric distribution, this would usually be considered relatively high.

5. Derivation: why standardised values have mean 0 and standard deviation 1

Standardisation transforms values using:

Z = (X − μ) / σ

This transformation creates a new variable whose centre is 0 and whose spread is 1.

Proof idea: E[Z] = 0 and SD(Z) = 1
1
Z = (X − μ) / σ
Start with a variable X with mean μ and standard deviation σ.
2
E[Z] = E[(X − μ)/σ] = (E[X] − μ)/σ
Use linearity of expectation and take constants outside.
3
E[Z] = (μ − μ)/σ = 0
The standardised variable has mean 0.
4
Var(Z) = Var((X − μ)/σ) = Var(X)/σ²
Subtracting a constant does not change variance; dividing by σ divides variance by σ².
5
Var(Z) = σ²/σ² = 1, so SD(Z) = 1
The standardised variable has standard deviation 1.

6. Comparing values from different distributions

One of the most useful applications of z-scores is comparison across distributions. Raw values may be measured in different units or have different spreads. Z-scores place them on a common scale.

VariableRaw valueMeanSDZ-score
Statistics exam78609(78 − 60)/9 = 2.00
Biology exam827612(82 − 76)/12 = 0.50

Although 82 is a higher raw score than 78, the statistics score is stronger relative to its distribution. A z-score of 2.00 means the score is two standard deviations above the statistics mean, while 0.50 means only half a standard deviation above the biology mean.

7. Z-scores and unusual observations

Z-scores can be used as a descriptive rule for identifying unusual values, especially when the distribution is approximately symmetric and mound-shaped.

|z| valueDescriptive interpretation
|z| < 1Fairly close to the mean
1 ≤ |z| < 2Somewhat away from the mean, but not very unusual
2 ≤ |z| < 3Unusual or relatively extreme
|z| ≥ 3Very unusual; should usually be checked carefully
Caution

The ±2 and ±3 rules are descriptive guides, not universal laws. They work best for roughly symmetric distributions. For skewed distributions, large z-scores may be common on the long-tail side.

8. Z-scores and the normal distribution

When the original variable is normally distributed, z-scores connect directly to the standard normal distribution. A standard normal variable has mean 0 and standard deviation 1.

If X ~ Normal(μ, σ²), then Z = (X − μ)/σ ~ Normal(0, 1)

This idea becomes very important in probability and inference. For now, the descriptive interpretation is enough: z-scores tell us location on a common standard scale.

Normal-rule intervalApproximate percentage
Between z = −1 and z = +1About 68%
Between z = −2 and z = +2About 95%
Between z = −3 and z = +3About 99.7%

9. Standardisation vs normalisation

Students often confuse standardisation with normalisation. In statistics, standardisation usually means transforming values into z-scores. The transformed variable has mean 0 and standard deviation 1.

Normalisation is a broader term and can mean different transformations, such as scaling values between 0 and 1. Normalisation does not necessarily produce mean 0 or standard deviation 1.

TransformationFormulaResult
Standardisationz = (x − mean)/SDMean 0, SD 1
Min-max scaling(x − min)/(max − min)Values between 0 and 1

10. Common mistakes

  • Interpreting z as raw units: z = 2 does not mean 2 units above the mean; it means 2 standard deviations above the mean.
  • Ignoring the sign: z = −1.5 means below the mean, not just “1.5 away.”
  • Using z-scores blindly for skewed data: mean and SD can be distorted by skewness and outliers.
  • Comparing raw values across different scales: raw marks, weights or biological measurements may not be comparable unless standardised.
  • Assuming z-score means normality: calculating z-scores does not make the data normally distributed; it only changes the scale.
Summary
  • A z-score is a standardised value.
  • It measures distance from the mean in standard deviation units.
  • Standardised values have mean 0 and standard deviation 1.
  • Z-scores help compare values from different distributions.
  • Z-scores are most straightforward when the original distribution is roughly symmetric.

Practice exercises

Standardisation and z-scores

Five detailed exercises. Try each before revealing the answer.

Exercise 1
Basic z-score calculation
Easy+

A class has mean exam score 64 and standard deviation 8. A student scores 80.

  1. Calculate the z-score.
  2. Interpret it in words.
Answer
  1. z = (80 − 64)/8 = 16/8 = 2.
  2. The student scored 2 standard deviations above the class mean. This is a relatively high score compared with the class distribution.
Exercise 2
Negative z-score
Easy+

The mean resting heart rate in a sample is 72 bpm with standard deviation 10 bpm. A participant has resting heart rate 57 bpm.

  1. Calculate the z-score.
  2. What does the negative sign mean?
Answer
  1. z = (57 − 72)/10 = −15/10 = −1.5.
  2. The negative sign means the participant’s heart rate is below the sample mean. Specifically, it is 1.5 standard deviations below the mean.
Exercise 3
Comparing two performances
Medium+

A student scores 70 in Course A and 76 in Course B. Course A has mean 58 and SD 6. Course B has mean 70 and SD 4.

  1. Calculate the z-score for each course.
  2. In which course was the student’s relative performance stronger?
  3. Why might the raw scores be misleading?
Answer
  1. Course A: z = (70 − 58)/6 = 2. Course B: z = (76 − 70)/4 = 1.5.
  2. The relative performance was stronger in Course A because z = 2 is larger than z = 1.5.
  3. The raw score is higher in Course B, but Course B also has a higher mean. Z-scores compare each score relative to its own distribution.
Exercise 4
Reverse z-score calculation
Medium+

A measurement has mean μ = 100 and standard deviation σ = 12. An observation has z-score 1.75. Find the raw value x.

Answer

Start with z = (x − μ)/σ. Rearranging gives x = μ + zσ. Therefore x = 100 + 1.75(12) = 100 + 21 = 121. The raw value is 121.

Exercise 5
Proof and interpretation
Hard+

Let X have mean μ and variance σ². Define Z = (X − μ)/σ.

  1. Show that E[Z] = 0.
  2. Show that Var(Z) = 1.
  3. Does this transformation make any distribution normal? Explain.
Answer
  1. E[Z] = E[(X − μ)/σ] = (E[X] − μ)/σ = (μ − μ)/σ = 0.
  2. Var(Z) = Var((X − μ)/σ) = Var(X)/σ² = σ²/σ² = 1.
  3. No. Standardisation changes the centre and scale, but it does not change the fundamental shape into normality. A skewed distribution remains skewed after standardisation, although its mean becomes 0 and SD becomes 1.

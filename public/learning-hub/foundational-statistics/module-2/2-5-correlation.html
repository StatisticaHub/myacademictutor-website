Correlation and association

Mr. R · ~26 min · Module 2 final topic
📍 Scene — students are looking at a scatterplot
EM
Emma

I can see that students who study more hours often get higher marks. Is that what correlation means?

MR
Mr. R

That is the basic idea. Correlation measures the direction and strength of a relationship between two numerical variables.

OL
Oliver

Direction means whether both variables go up together, or one goes down when the other goes up?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A positive association means larger values of one variable tend to occur with larger values of the other. A negative association means larger values of one tend to occur with smaller values of the other.

Scatterplots first, correlation second

Before calculating a correlation coefficient, we should look at a scatterplot. A scatterplot shows each observation as a point with an x-value and a y-value. It reveals direction, strength, shape, clusters, and outliers.

Definition — association

Two variables are associated when the values of one variable tend to vary systematically with the values of another variable.

JA
James

So if the scatterplot slopes upward, that is positive association?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. Upward pattern: positive. Downward pattern: negative. No clear pattern: weak or no association. But the shape also matters — Pearson correlation is mainly designed for linear relationships.

Pearson correlation coefficient

The Pearson correlation coefficient is usually written as \(r\). It ranges from −1 to +1.

Value of rDirectionStrengthInterpretation
r close to +1PositiveStrongPoints lie close to an upward-sloping line
r close to −1NegativeStrongPoints lie close to a downward-sloping line

SO
Sophia

If r = 0, does that mean the variables are completely unrelated?

MR
Mr. R

Not necessarily. It means there is little or no linear association. There could still be a curved relationship. That is why the scatterplot matters.

Correlation is not causation

Correlation describes how two variables move together. It does not by itself prove that one variable causes the other.

Important warning

A correlation coefficient alone cannot establish causality. Association may arise from causation, reverse causation, confounding, selection effects, measurement patterns, or coincidence.

EM
Emma

If study hours and marks are positively correlated, can I say studying causes higher marks?

MR
Mr. R

You can say they are associated. To make a causal claim, you would need stronger study design and careful control of other factors, such as prior ability, teaching quality, sleep, motivation and assessment type.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Association means two variables vary together in a systematic way.
  • Scatterplots should be examined before calculating or interpreting correlation.
  • Pearson’s r measures the direction and strength of a linear relationship between two numerical variables.
  • Outliers and non-linear patterns can make correlation misleading.
  • Correlation alone does not prove causation.
Detailed notes

Correlation and association

1. Association between two variables

In descriptive statistics, we often want to understand whether two variables are related. For example, we may ask whether height and weight are associated, whether hours studied and exam score are associated, or whether age and blood pressure are associated.

Definition — association

Two variables are associated if the distribution of one variable changes systematically as the other variable changes.

Association is a broad idea. It can apply to numerical variables, categorical variables, or a mixture of variable types. In this lesson, we focus mainly on association between two numerical variables, where scatterplots and correlation are most commonly used.

2. Scatterplots

A scatterplot displays paired numerical observations. Each individual, patient, object or unit contributes one point. The horizontal axis represents one variable and the vertical axis represents the other variable.

Definition — scatterplot

A scatterplot is a graph for paired numerical data in which each point represents one observation with coordinates \((x_i, y_i)\).

A good scatterplot should be examined for:

  • direction: positive, negative, or no clear direction;
  • form: linear, curved, clustered, or irregular;
  • strength: how tightly points follow the pattern;
  • outliers: points far from the main cloud;
  • clusters: separate groups that may need separate interpretation.

3. Direction of association

The direction of an association describes whether the variables tend to move in the same direction or in opposite directions.

DirectionPatternExample
Positive associationAs x increases, y tends to increaseHeight and weight in children
Negative associationAs x increases, y tends to decreasePrice and demand in many markets
No clear associationNo systematic pattern between x and yShoe size and exam score

4. Strength of association

The strength of an association describes how closely the points follow a pattern. A strong linear association has points close to a straight line. A weak association has points widely scattered around the general trend.

Important point

Strength is not the same as slope. A steep but noisy relationship may have weaker correlation than a flatter but very tight relationship.

5. Pearson correlation coefficient

The Pearson correlation coefficient, written \(r\), is a numerical summary of the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two numerical variables.

−1 ≤ r ≤ +1

The sign tells the direction. The absolute value tells the strength of the linear association.

Correlation valueUsual interpretation
r = +1Perfect positive linear relationship
r = −1Perfect negative linear relationship
r = 0No linear relationship
r = +0.85Strong positive linear association
r = −0.70Strong negative linear association
r = +0.20Weak positive linear association

6. Formula for Pearson’s r

For paired data \((x_1,y_1), (x_2,y_2), \ldots, (x_n,y_n)\), Pearson’s correlation coefficient can be written as:

r = Σ[(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)] / √{Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²}

This formula compares how deviations in x and deviations in y move together. If large x-values tend to occur with large y-values, the products \((x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})\) are often positive. If large x-values tend to occur with small y-values, those products are often negative.

7. Correlation as standardised covariance

Correlation is closely related to covariance. Covariance measures whether two variables vary together, but it depends on the original units. Correlation standardises covariance so that the result always lies between −1 and +1.

r = covariance(x, y) / (sₓ sᵧ)
Interpretation of the formula
1
xᵢ − x̄ and yᵢ − ȳ
Each value is compared with its own mean. These are deviations from centre.
2
(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)
If both deviations have the same sign, the product is positive. If they have opposite signs, the product is negative.
3
Σ[(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ)]
Adding these products summarises whether the variables tend to move together or in opposite directions.
4
Divide by √{Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²}
This standardises the result so the correlation is unit-free and always between −1 and +1.

8. How to describe a correlation properly

A good descriptive sentence should mention the variables, direction, approximate strength, and whether the statement is descriptive rather than causal.

Weak wordingStronger wording
Correlation is 0.72.There is a strong positive linear association between hours studied and exam score (r = 0.72).
Exercise causes lower weight because r = −0.55.Exercise time and body weight show a moderate negative association (r = −0.55); this does not by itself establish causation.
There is no relationship because r = 0.There is little evidence of a linear association; a scatterplot should be checked for possible non-linear patterns.

9. Correlation is affected by outliers

Pearson’s correlation can be strongly affected by outliers. One unusual point may create a correlation, hide a correlation, or reverse the apparent direction.

Outlier caution

Always inspect the scatterplot before interpreting r. A single influential point can make a correlation coefficient misleading.

10. Correlation measures linear association

A correlation coefficient near zero does not necessarily mean there is no relationship. It may mean there is no linear relationship. A curved relationship can have a small Pearson correlation even when the variables are clearly related.

Non-linear relationship

If the scatterplot shows a curved pattern, Pearson’s r may not summarise the association well. The graph is more informative than the single number.

11. Correlation and units

Correlation is unit-free. If height is measured in centimetres or metres, the correlation with weight is unchanged. This happens because correlation standardises both variables before comparing them.

12. Correlation and causation

Correlation alone cannot show causation. If two variables are associated, several explanations are possible:

  • x may influence y;
  • y may influence x;
  • a third variable may influence both x and y;
  • the association may arise from selection bias or measurement patterns;
  • the pattern may be coincidental.
Confounding

A confounder is a third variable related to both the exposure and outcome that can create or distort an observed association.

For example, ice cream sales and drowning incidents may be positively correlated, but ice cream does not cause drowning. Warm weather is a plausible third variable that increases both swimming and ice cream sales.

13. Pearson vs Spearman correlation

Pearson correlation measures linear association using actual numerical values. Spearman correlation is based on ranks and is useful for monotonic relationships, ordinal data, or situations where extreme values may distort Pearson correlation.

MeasureBest forKey idea
Pearson correlationLinear relationship between numerical variablesUses actual values
Spearman correlationMonotonic relationship or ranked dataUses ranks

14. Common mistakes

  • Ignoring the scatterplot: r alone hides non-linearity, clusters and outliers.
  • Interpreting r as slope: r is not the increase in y for one unit increase in x.
  • Claiming causation: correlation does not prove cause and effect.
  • Using Pearson r for categorical data: Pearson r is for paired numerical data.
  • Assuming r = 0 means no relationship at all: it only means no linear relationship.
Summary
  • Correlation summarises linear association between two numerical variables.
  • The sign of r gives direction; the absolute value gives strength.
  • Pearson’s r is unit-free and lies between −1 and +1.
  • Scatterplots are essential before interpreting correlation.
  • Outliers, non-linearity and confounding can make correlation misleading.

Correlation and association

Five detailed exercises. Try each one before revealing the answer.

Exercise 1
Interpreting r
Easy+

For each correlation coefficient, describe the direction and approximate strength of the linear association:

  1. r = 0.82
  2. r = −0.64
  3. r = 0.08
  4. r = −0.95
Answer
  1. Strong positive linear association.
  2. Moderate to strong negative linear association.
  3. Very weak positive linear association; essentially little linear pattern.
  4. Very strong negative linear association.
Exercise 2
Writing a descriptive sentence
Medium+

A dataset of 120 students gives r = 0.71 between weekly study hours and final exam score. Write a careful descriptive interpretation.

Answer

There is a strong positive linear association between weekly study hours and final exam score in this sample of 120 students (r = 0.71). Students who studied more hours tended to have higher exam scores. This is a descriptive association and does not by itself prove that study hours caused higher scores.

Exercise 3
Correlation and non-linearity
Medium+

A scatterplot shows a clear U-shaped relationship between stress level and performance. The Pearson correlation is close to 0. Explain why this can happen.

Answer

Pearson correlation measures linear association. A U-shaped pattern is non-linear: performance may be high at low and high stress levels but low at moderate stress levels, or vice versa. Positive and negative parts of the curve can cancel out in the calculation, giving r close to 0 even though the variables are clearly related. The scatterplot is therefore essential.

Exercise 4
Correlation is not causation
Medium+

A city dataset shows a positive correlation between ice cream sales and drowning incidents. Give two possible explanations that do not require ice cream to cause drowning.

Answer

One explanation is confounding by temperature or season: warm weather increases both ice cream sales and swimming, which may increase drowning incidents. Another explanation is behavioural patterning: more people spend time outdoors near water during summer, increasing both exposure to water and purchase of cold foods. The correlation does not mean ice cream causes drowning.

Exercise 5
Formula interpretation
Hard+

Explain why the product \((x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})\) is positive when both variables are above their means or both below their means, and how this relates to positive correlation.

Answer

If both variables are above their means, then \(x_i-\bar{x}\) and \(y_i-\bar{y}\) are both positive, so their product is positive. If both variables are below their means, both deviations are negative, and the product of two negatives is also positive. Therefore, when large x-values tend to occur with large y-values and small x-values tend to occur with small y-values, many products are positive. Their sum becomes positive, producing a positive covariance and hence a positive correlation.

Measures of spread

Mr. R · Emma, Oliver, James, Sophia · ~22 min · Descriptive statistics
📍 Scene — the same classroom, after discussing averages
EM
Emma

Last lesson we learned mean, median and mode. If I know the average, do I really need anything else?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. An average tells you where the data are centred, but it does not tell you how tightly or widely the observations are spread around that centre.

OL
Oliver

So two datasets can have the same mean but look very different?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Consider exam scores. Class A has scores 48, 49, 50, 51, 52. Class B has 10, 30, 50, 70, 90. Both have mean 50, but Class B is much more variable.

Big idea

A measure of centre without a measure of spread is incomplete. The mean tells us a typical value; the spread tells us how consistent, variable, risky or unequal the observations are.

Why spread matters

Spread is not just a technical detail. In real data, spread often carries the practical meaning. A hospital with an average waiting time of 30 minutes may still be problematic if some patients wait 5 minutes and others wait 3 hours. A treatment with an average improvement of 10 units may be unreliable if patient responses vary wildly.

JA
James

So spread tells us about consistency?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. Low spread means observations are close together. High spread means observations are widely scattered. In medicine, high spread may indicate heterogeneous patients. In education, it may show unequal performance. In finance, it often means risk.

SO
Sophia

Is the range enough? It sounds simple: maximum minus minimum.

MR
Mr. R

The range is useful as a first look, but it uses only two observations. A single unusually large or small value can change it completely. That is why we also use the interquartile range, variance and standard deviation.

The main measures

The intuition behind standard deviation

The standard deviation answers a simple question: roughly how far are observations from the mean? To measure this, we first compute each deviation from the mean. Some deviations are positive and some are negative. If we simply add them, they cancel to zero. So we square the deviations, average them, and then take the square root to return to the original units.

EM
Emma

Why not just take absolute distances instead of squaring?

MR
Mr. R

That is possible, and it leads to mean absolute deviation. But squared deviations have strong mathematical properties, especially in probability, regression and inference. That is why variance and standard deviation dominate statistical theory.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Measures of spread describe variability, consistency, inequality or risk.
  • The range is simple but highly sensitive to extreme observations.
  • The IQR describes the spread of the middle 50% and is robust to outliers.
  • Variance and standard deviation use deviations from the mean.
  • The standard deviation is usually easier to interpret than the variance because it is in the original units.
Detailed notes

Measures of spread

1. Why descriptive statistics needs spread

Descriptive statistics does not only describe the centre of a dataset. It also describes how observations are distributed around that centre. Two datasets may have identical means or medians but very different levels of variation. Therefore, a complete descriptive summary usually includes both a measure of centre and a measure of spread.

Definition — spread

Spread, also called variability or dispersion, describes how far observations are from each other or from a central value such as the mean or median.

In descriptive reporting, spread helps answer questions such as:

  • Are most observations close to the centre, or are they widely scattered?
  • Are there extreme values?
  • Is the dataset consistent or heterogeneous?
  • Can the centre alone give a fair description?
  • Should we use mean and SD, or median and IQR?

2. Range

The range is the simplest measure of spread. It is defined as:

Range = maximum value − minimum value

For the dataset 12, 14, 16, 18, 20, the range is 20 − 12 = 8. This means the observations span 8 units from the smallest to the largest value.

Strength

The range is easy to compute and gives a quick first impression of the total span of the data.

Limitation

The range depends only on the minimum and maximum. It ignores all observations between them and can be completely distorted by one outlier.

3. Quartiles and the interquartile range

Quartiles divide ordered data into four parts. The first quartile, Q1, marks the value below which roughly 25% of the observations lie. The second quartile, Q2, is the median. The third quartile, Q3, marks the value below which roughly 75% of observations lie.

IQR = Q3 − Q1

The interquartile range measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data. Because it ignores the lowest 25% and highest 25%, it is much less affected by extreme values than the range or standard deviation.

Example

Suppose the ordered dataset is:

4, 5, 7, 8, 9, 11, 13, 15, 18

The median is 9. The lower half is 4, 5, 7, 8, so Q1 = (5 + 7)/2 = 6. The upper half is 11, 13, 15, 18, so Q3 = (13 + 15)/2 = 14. Therefore:

IQR = 14 − 6 = 8
Important

Different textbooks and software use slightly different quartile calculation rules, especially for small datasets. For large datasets, these differences are usually negligible. In exams, follow the rule taught in your course.

4. Five-number summary

A five-number summary gives a compact description of spread and location:

minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum

It is especially useful for skewed data and is the numerical basis of a boxplot. It allows us to see the full span, the central value, the middle 50%, and potential asymmetry.

5. Outliers using the 1.5 × IQR rule

A common descriptive rule flags possible outliers using the IQR:

Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5 × IQR
Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR

Values below the lower fence or above the upper fence are often treated as potential outliers. This does not automatically mean they are errors. It means they deserve investigation.

Do not delete automatically

An outlier may be a data entry error, a genuine rare observation, a clinically important case, or a sign that the population is heterogeneous. Descriptive statistics should identify outliers before deciding what to do with them.

6. Deviations from the mean

Variance and standard deviation begin with the idea of a deviation from the mean:

Deviation for observation i = xᵢ − x̄

If xᵢ is above the mean, the deviation is positive. If xᵢ is below the mean, the deviation is negative. A key property of the mean is that the deviations from it always sum to zero:

Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = 0
Proof: deviations from the mean sum to zero
1
Σ(xᵢ − x̄)
Start with the sum of deviations from the sample mean.
2
Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = Σxᵢ − Σx̄
Separate the sum into the sum of observations minus the sum of repeated means.
3
Σx̄ = n x̄
The sample mean appears n times, so its sum is n times the mean.
4
x̄ = Σxᵢ / n, so n x̄ = Σxᵢ
Use the definition of the sample mean.
5
Σxᵢ − n x̄ = Σxᵢ − Σxᵢ = 0
Therefore raw deviations cancel. This is why we square deviations before averaging them.

7. Variance

Variance measures the average squared distance from the mean. For a population, the variance is:

σ² = (1/N) Σ(xᵢ − μ)²

For a sample, the usual sample variance is:

s² = [1/(n − 1)] Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²

The reason for using n − 1 instead of n is that x̄ is estimated from the same data. Once the sample mean is fixed, only n − 1 deviations are free to vary independently. This is called Bessel's correction, and it makes s² an unbiased estimator of the population variance under standard random sampling assumptions.

8. Standard deviation

The standard deviation is the square root of the variance:

s = √s² = √{ [1/(n − 1)] Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² }

Standard deviation is usually easier to interpret than variance because it is measured in the same units as the original variable. If heights are measured in centimetres, the standard deviation is also in centimetres.

Descriptive interpretation

If the mean systolic blood pressure is 130 mmHg and the standard deviation is 12 mmHg, then a typical patient value is roughly about 12 mmHg away from the mean. This is a descriptive statement about variability in the observed data.

10. Mean absolute deviation

The mean absolute deviation uses absolute distances from the mean or median instead of squared distances:

MAD about mean = (1/n) Σ |xᵢ − x̄|

It is intuitive because it is the average absolute distance from the centre. However, it is less central in classical statistical theory than variance and standard deviation.

11. Coefficient of variation

The coefficient of variation measures relative spread:

CV = s / x̄

It is often reported as a percentage:

CV% = (s / x̄) × 100

CV is useful when comparing variability between variables measured on different scales. For example, an SD of 5 kg in body weight and an SD of 5 cm in height cannot be compared directly because the units differ. CV gives a relative comparison.

Caution

Do not use the coefficient of variation when the mean is zero, close to zero, or can be negative. In those cases the ratio becomes unstable or meaningless.

13. How to write descriptive summaries properly

A strong descriptive summary should combine centre, spread and context. Weak writing says only “the average was 42”. Better writing says what 42 refers to and how variable the data were.

Good reporting examples

“The mean age was 42.6 years with a standard deviation of 8.4 years, indicating moderate variation around the average.”

“Waiting times were skewed, so the median and IQR were used. The median waiting time was 31 minutes, with an IQR of 18 to 54 minutes.”

14. Common mistakes

  • Reporting the mean without any measure of spread.
  • Using standard deviation for strongly skewed data without checking the distribution.
  • Interpreting variance directly even though its units are squared.
  • Calling every extreme value an error.
  • Using range alone as the main measure of variability.
  • Comparing SDs across variables with very different means or units without considering CV.

Practice exercises

Measures of spread

Attempt each question before revealing the answer. These are designed to build both calculation skill and interpretation.

Exercise 1
Range and IQR
Easy+

For the ordered dataset 3, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, 15, find the range, median, Q1, Q3 and IQR.

Answer

Range = 15 − 3 = 12. Median = 8. Lower half is 3, 5, 6, so Q1 = 5. Upper half is 10, 12, 15, so Q3 = 12. Therefore IQR = 12 − 5 = 7.

Exercise 2
Sample variance and standard deviation
Medium+

Calculate the sample variance and standard deviation for the data: 4, 6, 8, 10, 12.

Answer

The mean is x̄ = 8. Deviations are −4, −2, 0, 2, 4. Squared deviations are 16, 4, 0, 4, 16. Sum of squared deviations = 40. Sample variance is s² = 40/(5−1) = 10. Standard deviation is s = √10 ≈ 3.16.

Exercise 3
Effect of an outlier
Medium+

A dataset contains waiting times: 12, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19. A data entry adds 90 minutes. Explain which spread measures will be most affected and which will be more stable.

Answer

The range will be strongly affected because the maximum changes from 19 to 90. The standard deviation will also increase substantially because it uses squared deviations from the mean, so extreme values have large influence. The IQR will be more stable because it focuses on the middle 50% of the data and is less affected by one extreme value.

Exercise 4
Choosing a descriptive summary
Medium+

A researcher summarises hospital length of stay. Most patients stay 1–4 days, but a few complex cases stay 30–60 days. Should the researcher report mean ± SD or median and IQR? Explain.

Answer

Median and IQR are more appropriate. Length of stay is usually right-skewed because a small number of patients have very long stays. The mean and SD would be pulled upward by these extreme values. Median and IQR give a more representative description of the typical stay and the spread of the middle 50%.

Exercise 5
Proof and interpretation
Hard+

Show why the sum of deviations from the sample mean is zero. Then explain why variance uses squared deviations.

Answer

Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = Σxᵢ − Σx̄ = Σxᵢ − nx̄. Since x̄ = Σxᵢ/n, we have nx̄ = Σxᵢ. Therefore Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = Σxᵢ − Σxᵢ = 0. Raw deviations cannot measure spread because positive and negative deviations cancel. Squaring makes all deviations non-negative and gives greater weight to observations far from the mean.

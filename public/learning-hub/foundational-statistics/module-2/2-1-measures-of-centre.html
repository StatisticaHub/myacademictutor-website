Measures of centre

Mr. R · Emma, Oliver, James, Sophia · ~22 min · Descriptive statistics
📍 Scene — the class returns after collecting their first dataset
EM
Emma

We have collected data now. I suppose the next step is to find the average?

MR
Mr. R

Yes, but let us be precise. In statistics, “average” can mean different things. Today we study the main measures of centre: mean, median and mode.

OL
Oliver

Are mean, median and mode all trying to answer the same question?

MR
Mr. R

They all describe a central or typical value, but each one defines “typical” differently.

Big idea

A measure of centre is a single value chosen to represent the middle, balance point, or most typical value in a dataset.

Why centre matters

Raw data can be long and difficult to interpret. A measure of centre helps us summarise a dataset with one meaningful value. For example, we may report the average age of students, the median waiting time in a clinic, or the most common blood group in a sample.

JA
James

So the centre is like the representative value?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. But the best representative value depends on the type and shape of the data.

SO
Sophia

When would the mean be misleading?

MR
Mr. R

When the data are skewed or contain extreme values. In those cases, the median may better describe the typical observation.

The three main measures

MeasureDefinitionBest useWeakness
MeanSum of values divided by number of valuesRoughly symmetric numerical dataSensitive to outliers
MedianMiddle value after ordering the dataSkewed data or data with outliersDoes not use all magnitudes fully
ModeMost frequently occurring value or categoryCategorical data and repeated valuesMay be absent or not unique

The mean as a balance point

The mean is not only a calculation. It is the balance point of the data. If the observations were weights placed on a number line, the mean would be the point where the line balances.

Mean = sum of all observations ÷ number of observations
EM
Emma

If one value is very large, does it pull the mean towards it?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. This is why the mean is powerful but also sensitive. Extreme observations pull the balance point.

The median as a middle position

The median is found by arranging the data from smallest to largest and choosing the middle value. If there are two middle values, we take their average.

The median is resistant to outliers because it depends on position, not the exact size of extreme values.

The mode as the most common value

The mode is the value or category that appears most often. It is especially important for categorical data, where mean and median may not make sense.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Measures of centre summarise a dataset using a typical or central value.
  • The mean is the arithmetic average and acts like a balance point.
  • The median is the middle ordered value and is robust to outliers.
  • The mode is the most frequent value or category.
  • The best measure of centre depends on data type, skewness and extreme values.
Detailed notes

Measures of centre

1. What is a measure of centre?

A measure of centre is a numerical or categorical summary that represents the typical location of a dataset. It helps reduce many observations into one interpretable value.

Definition — centre

A measure of centre describes where the observations in a dataset tend to cluster or balance.

Measures of centre are useful because they help answer questions such as:

  • What is a typical value?
  • Where is the dataset located on the measurement scale?
  • How can we compare two groups quickly?
  • Which summary is most representative for this data shape?

2. Arithmetic mean

For observations \(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n\), the sample mean is:

x̄ = (x₁ + x₂ + ⋯ + xₙ) / n

Using summation notation:

x̄ = (1/n) Σxᵢ

For a population of size \(N\), the population mean is:

μ = (1/N) Σxᵢ

Example

For the values 4, 6, 8, 10, 12:

x̄ = (4 + 6 + 8 + 10 + 12) / 5 = 40 / 5 = 8
Strength

The mean uses every numerical value and is mathematically central to later topics such as variance, standard deviation, regression and inference.

Limitation

The mean is sensitive to extreme values. One unusually large or small observation can pull it away from the typical majority.

3. The mean as a balance point

The mean has an important balancing property. The deviations from the mean always sum to zero:

Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = 0
Proof: why deviations from the mean sum to zero
1
Σ(xᵢ − x̄)
Start with the sum of deviations from the sample mean.
2
Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = Σxᵢ − Σx̄
Separate the summation.
3
Σx̄ = n x̄
The mean is repeated n times.
4
x̄ = Σxᵢ / n, so n x̄ = Σxᵢ
Use the definition of the mean.
5
Σxᵢ − n x̄ = Σxᵢ − Σxᵢ = 0
Positive and negative deviations balance exactly.

4. Median

The median is the middle value after arranging the observations in increasing order.

If \(n\) is odd, the median is the single middle value. If \(n\) is even, the median is the average of the two middle values.

CaseDatasetMedian
Odd number of values3, 5, 7, 9, 117
Even number of values3, 5, 7, 9(5 + 7) / 2 = 6
Strength

The median is resistant to extreme values and is often the preferred centre for skewed data such as income, waiting times and length of hospital stay.

5. Mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring value. For categorical data, the mode is often the only meaningful measure of centre.

A dataset may have no repeated value, one mode, two modes, or several modes.

DatasetDescriptionMode
2, 3, 3, 5, 7One value occurs most often3
1, 1, 2, 3, 3Two values tie1 and 3
4, 5, 6, 7No repeated valueNo clear mode

6. Effect of outliers

Consider two datasets:

A: 10, 12, 13, 14, 16
B: 10, 12, 13, 14, 100

The first four values are similar, but dataset B contains a large outlier. The mean changes dramatically, while the median changes much less. This is why the median is called a resistant measure.

7. Choosing the correct measure of centre

Data situationRecommended centreReason
Roughly symmetric numerical dataMeanUses all values and represents balance
Skewed numerical dataMedianLess affected by extreme values
Data with strong outliersMedianMore representative of the typical observation
Nominal categorical dataModeMean and median are not meaningful
Ordinal categorical dataMedian or modeOrdering exists but distances may not be numerical

8. Reporting centre properly

A good statistical summary should report a measure of centre with context and, usually, a measure of spread. For example:

Good reporting examples

“The mean exam score was 68.4 marks.”

“Because waiting times were right-skewed, the median waiting time was reported instead of the mean.”

“The most common blood group in the sample was O positive.”

9. Common mistakes

  • Using the word “average” without saying whether it means mean, median or mode.
  • Using the mean for strongly skewed data without checking outliers.
  • Calculating a mean for nominal categories such as blood group or eye colour.
  • Forgetting to order the data before finding the median.
  • Assuming the mode must be unique.
  • Reporting a centre without considering spread.

Practice exercises

Measures of centre

Attempt each question before revealing the answer.

Exercise 1
Mean, median and mode
Easy+

For the dataset 4, 5, 5, 7, 9, find the mean, median and mode.

Answer

Mean = (4 + 5 + 5 + 7 + 9) / 5 = 30 / 5 = 6. Median = 5. Mode = 5 because it occurs most often.

Exercise 2
Even number of observations
Easy+

Find the median of 3, 8, 10, 15.

Answer

There are four values, so the median is the average of the two middle values: (8 + 10) / 2 = 9.

Exercise 3
Outlier effect
Medium+

Compare the mean and median of 10, 12, 13, 14, 100. Which measure better represents the typical value?

Answer

Mean = 149 / 5 = 29.8. Median = 13. The median better represents the typical value because 100 is an extreme value that pulls the mean upward.

Exercise 4
Categorical data
Medium+

For blood groups A, B, O, O, AB, O, A, which measure of centre is appropriate and what is its value?

Answer

The mode is appropriate because blood group is categorical. The mode is O because it appears most often.

Exercise 5
Choosing a summary
Hard+

A dataset of monthly incomes is strongly right-skewed. Which centre should be reported and why?

Answer

The median should usually be reported because income data often contain a few very large values that pull the mean upward. The median is more resistant and better represents the typical income.

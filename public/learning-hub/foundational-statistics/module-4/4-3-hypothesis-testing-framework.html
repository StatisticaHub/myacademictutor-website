Module 4 · Lesson 4.3

Hypothesis testing framework

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~50 min · After confidence intervals
📍 Scene — inference classroom, testing a scientific claim
EM
Emma

Confidence intervals gave us plausible values. But papers often say “statistically significant”. Is that the same idea?

MR
Mr. R

Related, but not identical. Confidence intervals estimate a parameter with uncertainty. Hypothesis tests assess how compatible the observed data are with a specific null hypothesis.

OL
Oliver

So a hypothesis test starts with a claim we assume temporarily?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. We assume the null hypothesis and ask whether the observed statistic is unusually far from what the null would predict.

The two hypotheses

A hypothesis test begins with two competing statements. The null hypothesis, written \(H_0\), usually represents no effect, no difference, or a benchmark value. The alternative hypothesis, written \(H_1\) or \(H_A\), represents the direction or type of departure we want to detect.

\[ H_0:\theta=\theta_0 \]
\[ H_1:\theta\neq\theta_0 \]
JA
James

So if a new teaching method is being tested, \(H_0\) might say there is no improvement?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. And the alternative might say the mean score is higher, lower, or simply different, depending on the research question.

The test statistic

A test statistic measures how far the sample result is from the null value, in standard-error units. For many tests, the structure is:

\[ \text{test statistic} = \frac{\text{estimate}-\text{null value}}{\text{standard error}} \]

For a simple \(z\)-test for a mean with known \(\sigma\):

\[ z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \]
SO
Sophia

So the statistic is large when the estimate is far from the null compared with its uncertainty?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A difference of 5 units can be impressive or unimportant depending on the standard error.

The p-value

The p-value is the probability, assuming the null hypothesis is true, of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one obtained.

Definition

A p-value measures how surprising the observed data are under the null model. It is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true.

EM
Emma

If the p-value is 0.03, does that mean there is a 3% probability the null hypothesis is true?

MR
Mr. R

No. That is one of the most common errors. The p-value is calculated under the assumption that the null is true; it does not give the probability that the null itself is true.

Significance level and decision rule

The significance level \(\alpha\) is the pre-chosen threshold for deciding whether the evidence is strong enough to reject \(H_0\). Commonly, \(\alpha=0.05\).

\[ \text{Reject }H_0 \text{ if } p\leq \alpha. \]

If \(p>\alpha\), we do not reject \(H_0\). We do not say the null is proven true.

OL
Oliver

Why not say “accept the null”?

MR
Mr. R

Because failing to find enough evidence against the null is not the same as proving the null. The study may be underpowered, noisy, or poorly designed.

Type I error and Type II error

Hypothesis testing involves possible mistakes. A Type I error occurs when we reject a true null hypothesis. A Type II error occurs when we fail to reject a false null hypothesis.

RealityDecisionResult
\(H_0\) trueReject \(H_0\)Type I error
\(H_0\) trueDo not reject \(H_0\)Correct decision
\(H_0\) falseReject \(H_0\)Correct decision
\(H_0\) falseDo not reject \(H_0\)Type II error

Power

Power is the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when a specific alternative is true. It is the probability of detecting an effect when that effect genuinely exists.

\[ \text{Power}=1-\beta \]

where \(\beta\) is the probability of a Type II error.

JA
James

So a non-significant result can happen because there is no effect, or because the study had low power?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. That is why “not significant” should never automatically be interpreted as “no effect”.

Statistical significance versus practical importance

A small p-value does not prove that an effect is large or important. With a very large sample, even a tiny effect can be statistically significant. Conversely, a clinically meaningful effect may fail to reach significance if the study is too small.

SO
Sophia

So we should report effect sizes and confidence intervals, not just p-values?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A p-value is one piece of evidence. Good statistical reporting includes the estimate, uncertainty, assumptions, and context.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • A hypothesis test assesses compatibility of data with a null hypothesis.
  • The null hypothesis is assumed for the purpose of calculating the test statistic and p-value.
  • The test statistic often equals estimate minus null value divided by standard error.
  • A p-value is not the probability that \(H_0\) is true.
  • Reject \(H_0\) when \(p\leq\alpha\); otherwise, do not reject \(H_0\).
  • Type I error means rejecting a true null; Type II error means failing to reject a false null.
  • Power is the probability of detecting a real effect.
  • Statistical significance is not the same as practical importance.
Detailed theoretical notes

Hypothesis testing framework

1. Purpose of hypothesis testing

Hypothesis testing provides a structured way to compare observed data with what would be expected under a specified null model. It does not prove hypotheses in the mathematical sense. It quantifies evidence against a reference assumption.

Core idea

Assume the null hypothesis is true, calculate how unusual the observed result is under that assumption, and decide whether the evidence is strong enough to reject the null.

2. Null and alternative hypotheses

The null hypothesis \(H_0\) usually states no difference, no effect, or equality to a benchmark value:

\[ H_0:\theta=\theta_0 \]

The alternative hypothesis states the type of departure from the null:

\[ H_1:\theta\neq\theta_0 \]

For one-sided tests, the alternative may be:

\[ H_1:\theta>\theta_0 \]

or:

\[ H_1:\theta<\theta_0 \]

3. One-sided versus two-sided tests

AlternativeQuestionTail area
\(H_1:\theta\neq\theta_0\)Is the parameter different?Two tails
\(H_1:\theta>\theta_0\)Is the parameter larger?Right tail
\(H_1:\theta<\theta_0\)Is the parameter smaller?Left tail

The direction of the test should be chosen based on the scientific question before seeing the data.

4. Test statistic

A test statistic converts the difference between the estimate and the null value into a standardised quantity.

\[ \text{test statistic} = \frac{\text{estimate}-\text{null value}}{\text{standard error}} \]

For a mean with known population standard deviation:

\[ z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \]

When \(\sigma\) is unknown and estimated by \(s\), a \(t\)-statistic is often used:

\[ t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \]

5. Null distribution

The null distribution is the distribution of the test statistic assuming \(H_0\) is true. The p-value is calculated using this null distribution.

Null distribution

The null distribution describes the values the test statistic would take under repeated sampling if the null hypothesis were true.

6. P-value

The p-value is the probability, under \(H_0\), of observing a result at least as extreme as the one observed.

For a two-sided \(z\)-test:

\[ p=2P(Z\geq |z_{\text{obs}}|) \]

For a right-tailed test:

\[ p=P(Z\geq z_{\text{obs}}) \]

For a left-tailed test:

\[ p=P(Z\leq z_{\text{obs}}) \]
Important

The p-value is not \(P(H_0\mid \text{data})\). It is calculated as \(P(\text{data as or more extreme}\mid H_0)\).

7. Significance level

The significance level \(\alpha\) is chosen before the test. It controls the probability of a Type I error when the null is true.

\[ \alpha=P(\text{reject }H_0\mid H_0\text{ true}) \]

The decision rule is:

\[ p\leq \alpha \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{reject }H_0 \]
\[ p>\alpha \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{do not reject }H_0 \]

8. Critical-value approach

Instead of comparing the p-value to \(\alpha\), we can compare the test statistic to a critical value.

For a two-sided \(z\)-test at \(\alpha=0.05\):

\[ \text{Reject }H_0 \text{ if } |z_{\text{obs}}|>1.96 \]

This is equivalent to the p-value rule for the same test.

9. Type I and Type II errors

\(H_0\) true\(H_0\) false
Reject \(H_0\)Type I errorCorrect rejection
Do not reject \(H_0\)Correct non-rejectionType II error

The probability of a Type I error is \(\alpha\). The probability of a Type II error is usually denoted \(\beta\).

\[ \beta=P(\text{do not reject }H_0\mid H_0\text{ false}) \]

10. Power

Power is the probability of rejecting \(H_0\) when a specified alternative is true.

\[ \text{Power}=1-\beta \]

Power generally increases when:

  • the true effect size is larger,
  • the sample size is larger,
  • the variability is smaller,
  • the significance level \(\alpha\) is larger,
  • the test is well matched to the study design and data.

11. Relationship with confidence intervals

For many common two-sided tests at \(\alpha=0.05\), rejecting \(H_0:\theta=\theta_0\) corresponds to the 95% confidence interval excluding \(\theta_0\).

Connection

If a 95% confidence interval for a mean difference excludes 0, the corresponding two-sided test at the 5% level usually rejects the null of no difference.

12. Common mistakes

  • Wrong: The p-value is the probability that the null hypothesis is true.
  • Better: The p-value is calculated assuming the null hypothesis is true.
  • Wrong: Not significant means no effect.
  • Better: Not significant means insufficient evidence to reject the null.
  • Wrong: Statistically significant means practically important.
  • Better: Practical importance depends on effect size and context.
  • Wrong: \(\alpha=0.05\) is a universal law.
  • Better: \(\alpha\) is a convention and should be justified by context.
Notes summary
  • Hypothesis testing begins by stating \(H_0\) and \(H_1\).
  • The test statistic standardises the distance from the null value.
  • The p-value is calculated under the null hypothesis.
  • The significance level \(\alpha\) controls the Type I error rate.
  • Power is \(1-\beta\), the probability of detecting a specified effect.
  • Statistical significance must be interpreted with effect size, uncertainty and study design.

Practice exercises

Hypothesis testing framework

Exercise 1
State hypotheses
Easy+

A researcher wants to test whether a population mean differs from 50. State \(H_0\) and \(H_1\).

Answer

\(H_0:\mu=50\). Since the question asks whether it differs, the alternative is two-sided: \(H_1:\mu\neq50\).

Exercise 2
Calculate a z statistic
Easy+

If \(\bar{x}=105\), \(\mu_0=100\), and \(\operatorname{SE}=2.5\), calculate the test statistic.

Answer

\(z=(105-100)/2.5=5/2.5=2\).

Exercise 3
Decision from p-value
Medium+

A test gives \(p=0.032\). At \(\alpha=0.05\), what decision is made?

Answer

Since \(0.032\leq0.05\), reject \(H_0\). The result is statistically significant at the 5% level.

Exercise 4
Do not accept the null
Medium+

A test gives \(p=0.22\). Explain why we should not say “the null hypothesis is true”.

Answer

A large p-value means the data are not sufficiently inconsistent with \(H_0\). It does not prove \(H_0\). The study may have low power or high variability.

Exercise 5
Type I error
Medium+

Define a Type I error in words.

Answer

A Type I error occurs when we reject the null hypothesis even though it is actually true.

Exercise 6
Power
Hard+

If \(\beta=0.20\), what is the power of the test? Interpret it.

Answer

Power is \(1-\beta=1-0.20=0.80\). This means that for the specified alternative, the test has an 80% probability of rejecting \(H_0\).

Exercise 7
P-value misconception
Hard+

A student says, “\(p=0.04\) means there is a 4% probability the null hypothesis is true.” Correct this statement.

Answer

The p-value is the probability of obtaining data at least as extreme as observed, assuming \(H_0\) is true. It is not the probability that \(H_0\) is true.

Exercise 8
Significance versus importance
Hard+

Explain why a statistically significant effect may not be practically important.

Answer

With a very large sample, even a tiny effect can produce a small p-value. Practical importance depends on effect size, uncertainty, cost, clinical relevance, and context, not only statistical significance.

Module 4 · Lesson 4.4

z-tests, t-tests and tests for proportions

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~55 min · After hypothesis testing framework
📍 Scene — inference classroom, choosing the right test
EM
Emma

We know the general hypothesis testing framework now. But in examples I see z-tests, one-sample t-tests, paired t-tests, two-sample t-tests and proportion tests. How do I know which one to use?

MR
Mr. R

Start with the outcome and the study design. Is the outcome quantitative or binary? Is there one group, two independent groups, or paired measurements? The test follows from the estimand and the sampling distribution.

OL
Oliver

So tests are not just names to memorise. They are attached to designs?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A test is a mathematical expression of a research question plus assumptions.

One-sample z-test for a mean

A one-sample z-test for a mean is used when we compare a sample mean with a hypothesised population mean and the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known.

\[ H_0:\mu=\mu_0 \]
\[ z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \]

In practice, true \(\sigma\) is rarely known, so this test is more common as a theoretical foundation than as a real-data default.

JA
James

If we do not know \(\sigma\), we use \(s\), right?

MR
Mr. R

Yes, and once we replace \(\sigma\) with \(s\), the uncertainty increases. That leads us to the t-test.

One-sample t-test

A one-sample t-test compares a sample mean with a hypothesised mean when the population standard deviation is unknown and estimated from the sample.

\[ t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \]

The test statistic is compared with a \(t\)-distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.

\[ \nu=n-1 \]
SO
Sophia

Why does the t-distribution have degrees of freedom?

MR
Mr. R

Because the sample standard deviation is estimated from the same data. After estimating the sample mean, only \(n-1\) independent pieces of information remain for estimating variability.

Paired t-test

A paired t-test is used when observations come in natural pairs: before and after treatment, left and right eye, matched participants, or the same person measured twice. The key is that we analyse the within-pair differences.

\[ D_i=X_{i,\text{after}}-X_{i,\text{before}} \]

Then the paired t-test is just a one-sample t-test on the differences:

\[ t=\frac{\bar{d}-0}{s_d/\sqrt{n}} \]
EM
Emma

So paired does not mean two independent groups?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. Paired means the two measurements are linked. Ignoring the pairing throws away useful information and can give the wrong standard error.

Two-sample t-test

A two-sample t-test compares the means of two independent groups. For example, mean blood pressure in treatment and control groups.

\[ H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0 \]

A common version is Welch's t-test, which does not assume equal population variances:

\[ t=\frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} \]
OL
Oliver

Why use Welch's version?

MR
Mr. R

Because equal variances are often not guaranteed. Welch's test is usually safer as a default for independent groups.

One-sample test for a proportion

When the outcome is binary, the parameter is often a population proportion \(p\). To test whether \(p=p_0\), we use the null standard error based on \(p_0\):

\[ z=\frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}} \]

This is a large-sample normal approximation. It works better when the expected numbers of successes and failures under the null are not too small.

Two-sample test for proportions

To compare two independent proportions, we test:

\[ H_0:p_1-p_2=0 \]

Under the null, the two proportions are assumed equal, so we use a pooled estimate:

\[ \hat{p}_{\text{pool}}=\frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2} \]
\[ z=\frac{\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2} {\sqrt{\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}) \left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}} \]

Choosing the test

Assumptions matter

All tests rely on assumptions. These include independence, appropriate measurement scale, correct pairing or grouping, approximate normality of the relevant sampling distribution, and absence of major design bias.

JA
James

If the sample is huge, can we ignore all assumptions?

MR
Mr. R

No. Large samples help with sampling distribution approximations, but they do not fix dependence, confounding, selection bias, measurement error, or the wrong study design.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • A z-test for a mean uses known \(\sigma\); a t-test uses estimated \(s\).
  • A one-sample t-test compares one mean with a benchmark.
  • A paired t-test analyses within-pair differences.
  • A two-sample t-test compares independent group means.
  • Welch's t-test is often safer when variances may differ.
  • Proportion tests use normal approximations for binary outcomes.
  • The correct test depends on outcome type, design, estimand and assumptions.
Detailed theoretical notes

z-tests, t-tests and tests for proportions

1. The common structure of tests

Many tests in this lesson have the same underlying structure:

\[ \text{test statistic} = \frac{\text{estimate}-\text{null value}}{\text{standard error under the null}} \]

The difference between tests lies in the estimate, the standard error, the reference distribution and the study design.

2. One-sample z-test for a mean

Suppose \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\) are independent observations from a population with mean \(\mu\) and known standard deviation \(\sigma\). To test:

\[ H_0:\mu=\mu_0 \]

use:

\[ z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \]

Under \(H_0\), if assumptions are satisfied:

\[ Z\sim N(0,1) \]

3. One-sample t-test

When \(\sigma\) is unknown, we estimate it using the sample standard deviation \(s\). The test statistic becomes:

\[ t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \]

Under \(H_0\), and assuming the data are normal or the sample size is sufficiently large, this statistic follows approximately a t-distribution with:

\[ \nu=n-1 \]

Why \(t\), not \(z\)?

The \(t\)-distribution has heavier tails than the normal distribution because \(s\) is estimated from the data. The extra uncertainty is larger when \(n\) is small. As \(n\) grows, the \(t\)-distribution approaches the standard normal distribution.

4. Paired t-test

A paired test is appropriate when each observation in one condition is naturally linked to an observation in another condition. Define the differences:

\[ D_i=X_{i,2}-X_{i,1},\qquad i=1,\ldots,n \]

The null hypothesis is usually:

\[ H_0:\mu_D=0 \]

The test statistic is:

\[ t=\frac{\bar{d}}{s_D/\sqrt{n}} \]

with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom. The paired test is a one-sample t-test applied to the differences.

5. Independent two-sample t-test

For two independent groups, the parameter of interest is often the difference in population means:

\[ \Delta=\mu_1-\mu_2 \]

The null hypothesis is often:

\[ H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0 \]

Welch's t-test

Welch's t-test uses the standard error:

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2) = \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}} \]

The test statistic is:

\[ t=\frac{(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)-0} {\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} \]

Welch's test uses an approximate degrees-of-freedom formula and does not require equal variances.

Pooled two-sample t-test

If equal variances are assumed, a pooled variance may be used:

\[ s_p^2= \frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2} \]

The pooled test statistic is:

\[ t= \frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2} {s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}}} \]

Because equal variances are not always plausible, Welch's test is often preferred.

6. One-sample proportion z-test

For binary data, let \(X\) be the number of successes in \(n\) trials:

\[ X\sim \operatorname{Binomial}(n,p) \]

The sample proportion is:

\[ \hat{p}=\frac{X}{n} \]

To test \(H_0:p=p_0\), use:

\[ z= \frac{\hat{p}-p_0} {\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}} \]

The standard error uses \(p_0\) because the test is performed under the null hypothesis.

7. Two-sample proportion z-test

For two independent groups, let:

\[ \hat{p}_1=\frac{x_1}{n_1},\qquad \hat{p}_2=\frac{x_2}{n_2} \]

To test:

\[ H_0:p_1-p_2=0 \]

we pool the successes under the null:

\[ \hat{p}_{\text{pool}}=\frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2} \]

The test statistic is:

\[ z= \frac{\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2} {\sqrt{\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}(1-\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}) \left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}} \]

9. Conditions and assumptions

  • Independence: observations should be independent unless using a paired design.
  • Correct pairing: paired tests require meaningful one-to-one pairs.
  • Quantitative outcome: t-tests are for numeric outcomes.
  • Binary outcome: proportion tests are for success/failure outcomes.
  • Approximate normality: t-tests rely on normality of the mean or differences, especially in small samples.
  • Large-sample proportion approximation: expected success and failure counts should not be too small.
  • Study design validity: tests cannot fix bias, confounding or poor measurement.

10. Common mistakes

  • Using a paired test for independent groups: pairing must reflect linked observations.
  • Using an independent t-test for before-after data: this ignores within-person pairing.
  • Using a z-test for a mean when \(\sigma\) is unknown: a t-test is usually more appropriate.
  • Using a proportion test for a quantitative outcome: binary and quantitative outcomes require different tests.
  • Reporting only p-values: estimates and confidence intervals should also be reported.
Notes summary
  • The test statistic is estimate minus null value divided by standard error.
  • z-tests use a standard normal reference distribution.
  • t-tests account for estimating variability with \(s\).
  • Paired t-tests analyse differences within pairs.
  • Welch's t-test compares independent means without assuming equal variances.
  • Proportion tests use binomial-to-normal approximations.
  • Correct test choice depends on outcome type and study design.

Practice exercises

z-tests, t-tests and tests for proportions

Exercise 1
One-sample z-test
Easy+

A sample has \(\bar{x}=52\), \(n=64\), known \(\sigma=16\). Test against \(\mu_0=50\). Calculate the z statistic.

Answer

\(\operatorname{SE}=16/\sqrt{64}=2\). Therefore \(z=(52-50)/2=1\).

Exercise 2
One-sample t-test
Easy+

A sample has \(\bar{x}=105\), \(s=12\), \(n=36\). Test against \(\mu_0=100\). Calculate the t statistic.

Answer

\(\operatorname{SE}=12/\sqrt{36}=2\). Therefore \(t=(105-100)/2=2.5\). The degrees of freedom are \(35\).

Exercise 3
Paired t-test
Medium+

In a before-after study with \(n=25\), the mean difference is \(\bar{d}=4\) and \(s_D=10\). Calculate the paired t statistic.

Answer

\(\operatorname{SE}=10/\sqrt{25}=2\). Therefore \(t=4/2=2\), with \(24\) degrees of freedom.

Exercise 4
Welch two-sample statistic
Medium+

Two independent groups have \(\bar{x}_1=70\), \(\bar{x}_2=64\), \(s_1=12\), \(s_2=16\), \(n_1=36\), \(n_2=64\). Calculate the Welch t statistic.

Answer

The difference is \(6\). The SE is \(\sqrt{12^2/36+16^2/64}=\sqrt{4+4}=\sqrt{8}\approx2.828\). Thus \(t=6/2.828\approx2.12\).

Exercise 5
One-sample proportion test
Medium+

In a sample of \(n=200\), \(\hat{p}=0.58\). Test \(H_0:p=0.50\). Calculate the z statistic.

Answer

The null SE is \(\sqrt{0.5(0.5)/200}=\sqrt{0.00125}\approx0.0354\). Thus \(z=(0.58-0.50)/0.0354\approx2.26\).

Exercise 6
Two-sample proportion test
Hard+

Group 1 has \(x_1=40\) successes out of \(n_1=100\). Group 2 has \(x_2=60\) successes out of \(n_2=120\). Calculate \(\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}\).

Answer

\(\hat{p}_{\text{pool}}=(40+60)/(100+120)=100/220\approx0.455\).

Exercise 7
Choose the correct test
Hard+

A study measures anxiety score before and after an intervention in the same 30 participants. Which test is appropriate and why?

Answer

A paired t-test is appropriate because the before and after scores are linked within the same participants. The analysis should be based on within-person differences.

Exercise 8
Common design mistake
Hard+

Why is it wrong to use an independent two-sample t-test for paired before-after data?

Answer

The observations are not independent across conditions because each before score is linked to an after score from the same participant. Ignoring pairing loses information and uses the wrong standard error.

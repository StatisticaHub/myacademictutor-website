Chi-square and non-parametric tests

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~55 min · Final inference foundation lesson
📍 Scene — inference classroom, when usual mean tests are not enough
EM
Emma

We have learned t-tests and proportion tests. But I keep seeing chi-square tests and non-parametric tests. Are these for situations where t-tests do not fit?

MR
Mr. R

Often, yes. Chi-square tests are commonly used for categorical count data. Non-parametric tests are useful when we do not want to rely strongly on normality or when the data are ordinal or heavily skewed.

OL
Oliver

So again, the first question is: what type of outcome and design do we have?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Test choice always begins with the scientific question, the data type and the sampling design.

Why chi-square tests use counts

Chi-square tests compare observed counts with expected counts. They do not work directly with raw continuous measurements. They are designed for frequency tables.

\[ \chi^2=\sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E} \]

Here, \(O\) means observed count and \(E\) means expected count under the null hypothesis.

JA
James

Why square the difference \(O-E\)?

MR
Mr. R

Squaring makes positive and negative deviations both contribute positively. Dividing by \(E\) scales the difference relative to how large the expected count is.

Chi-square goodness-of-fit test

A chi-square goodness-of-fit test asks whether observed counts across categories are consistent with a specified theoretical distribution.

For example, suppose a die is rolled 120 times. Under the null hypothesis of fairness, each face should occur about 20 times.

\[ H_0:\text{the category probabilities match the specified probabilities} \]
SO
Sophia

So this test compares observed counts with expected counts from a claimed model?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. It asks whether the departures are larger than would be expected from random variation alone.

Chi-square test of independence

A chi-square test of independence is used for two categorical variables arranged in a contingency table. It asks whether the variables are associated.

For example, we may record smoking status and disease status. The question is whether disease status is independent of smoking status.

\[ H_0:\text{the two categorical variables are independent} \]

The expected count in row \(i\), column \(j\) is:

\[ E_{ij}=\frac{(\text{row }i\text{ total})(\text{column }j\text{ total})}{\text{grand total}} \]
EM
Emma

So expected counts are what we would expect if the row and column variables had no association?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. If observed counts are very different from these expected counts, that suggests evidence against independence.

Degrees of freedom

For a chi-square test of independence in an \(r\times c\) table, the degrees of freedom are:

\[ \nu=(r-1)(c-1) \]

For a goodness-of-fit test with \(k\) categories and no estimated parameters, the degrees of freedom are:

\[ \nu=k-1 \]

When chi-square approximation may be poor

The chi-square approximation needs expected counts to be sufficiently large. If expected counts are very small, the approximation may be inaccurate. In small \(2\times2\) tables, Fisher's exact test is often considered.

OL
Oliver

So if my table has tiny expected counts, I should be careful with chi-square?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. The test statistic may still be computed, but the reference distribution may not approximate well.

What non-parametric tests mean

Non-parametric tests do not usually mean “no assumptions”. They mean the test does not rely on a fully specified parametric distribution such as a normal distribution for the raw data. Many non-parametric tests work with ranks rather than raw values.

Important distinction

Non-parametric does not mean assumption-free. It often means fewer or different assumptions, especially about distributional shape.

Mann–Whitney U test

The Mann–Whitney U test is commonly used to compare two independent groups when the outcome is ordinal or continuous but not well suited to a two-sample t-test.

It ranks all observations together and compares the rank patterns between groups.

JA
James

Does Mann–Whitney always test whether medians are different?

MR
Mr. R

Not always. That is a common oversimplification. If the two distributions have the same shape and differ mainly by location, it can be interpreted as a location or median shift. More generally, it tests differences in distributional tendency.

Wilcoxon signed-rank test

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is a non-parametric alternative for paired data. It analyses the signs and ranks of within-pair differences.

It is useful when the paired differences are not well modelled by a normal distribution but are still reasonably symmetric around a central location.

Kruskal–Wallis test

The Kruskal–Wallis test extends rank-based comparison to more than two independent groups. It is often viewed as a non-parametric alternative to one-way ANOVA.

If the Kruskal–Wallis test finds evidence of differences, follow-up pairwise comparisons are usually needed to identify which groups differ.

Detailed theoretical notes

Chi-square and non-parametric tests

1. Why we need these tests

Not every research question involves a mean or a proportion in a form suitable for the tests in Lesson 4.4. Many data sets involve counts in categories, ordinal outcomes, skewed measurements, small samples, or ranked information. Chi-square and non-parametric tests extend the inferential toolkit to these situations.

2. Chi-square statistic

The general chi-square statistic compares observed counts \(O\) with expected counts \(E\):

\[ \chi^2=\sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E} \]

Each term measures a squared discrepancy between observed and expected count, scaled by the expected count. Larger discrepancies produce larger \(\chi^2\) values.

3. Chi-square goodness-of-fit test

The goodness-of-fit test is used when there is one categorical variable and a specified expected distribution.

If the expected category probabilities are \(p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_k\), and the total sample size is \(n\), then the expected count in category \(i\) is:

\[ E_i=np_i \]

The hypotheses are:

\[ H_0:\text{the category probabilities are }p_1,p_2,\ldots,p_k \]
\[ H_1:\text{at least one category probability differs} \]

The test statistic is:

\[ \chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^{k}\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i} \]

If no parameters are estimated from the data, the degrees of freedom are:

\[ \nu=k-1 \]

4. Chi-square test of independence

The chi-square test of independence is used with a contingency table containing two categorical variables.

For an \(r\times c\) table, the expected count in cell \((i,j)\) under independence is:

\[ E_{ij}= \frac{(\text{row }i\text{ total})(\text{column }j\text{ total})}{\text{grand total}} \]

The test statistic is:

\[ \chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^{r}\sum_{j=1}^{c} \frac{(O_{ij}-E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}} \]

The degrees of freedom are:

\[ \nu=(r-1)(c-1) \]

Derivation of expected count under independence

If two categorical variables are independent, then:

\[ P(A_i\cap B_j)=P(A_i)P(B_j) \]

In a table, we estimate these probabilities using marginal proportions:

\[ \widehat{P}(A_i)=\frac{\text{row }i\text{ total}}{n}, \qquad \widehat{P}(B_j)=\frac{\text{column }j\text{ total}}{n} \]

Therefore, the expected cell proportion is:

\[ \frac{\text{row }i\text{ total}}{n} \cdot \frac{\text{column }j\text{ total}}{n} \]

Multiplying by \(n\) gives:

\[ E_{ij} = n\left( \frac{\text{row }i\text{ total}}{n} \cdot \frac{\text{column }j\text{ total}}{n} \right) = \frac{(\text{row }i\text{ total})(\text{column }j\text{ total})}{n} \]

5. Assumptions and cautions for chi-square tests

  • The data should be counts, not percentages alone.
  • Categories should be mutually exclusive.
  • Observations should be independent.
  • Expected counts should generally not be too small.
  • The test indicates evidence of discrepancy or association, not its direction or practical importance by itself.
Common warning

A statistically significant chi-square result does not automatically explain which cells caused the association. Inspect observed and expected counts, residuals, and subject-matter context.

6. Fisher's exact test

Fisher's exact test is often used for \(2\times2\) tables when expected counts are small. It calculates probabilities exactly under fixed margins rather than relying on a large-sample chi-square approximation.

It is especially common in small biomedical studies where a rare outcome leads to sparse cells.

7. What are non-parametric tests?

Non-parametric tests avoid specifying a full parametric distribution for the raw data. Many common non-parametric tests transform data into ranks and then test whether rank distributions differ across groups.

Not assumption-free

Non-parametric tests still have assumptions. For example, independence, correct pairing, meaningful ordering, and similar distributional shapes may matter depending on the interpretation.

8. Mann–Whitney U test

The Mann–Whitney U test compares two independent groups using ranks. It is useful for ordinal outcomes or continuous outcomes with strong skewness or outliers.

The broad hypotheses can be described as:

\[ H_0:\text{the two groups have the same distribution} \]
\[ H_1:\text{the distributions differ in location or tendency} \]

If the distributions have similar shape, a difference in ranks may be interpreted as a difference in location, often described informally as a median difference. Without similar shapes, the interpretation is broader.

9. Wilcoxon signed-rank test

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is used for paired or matched data. It ranks the absolute values of non-zero differences and uses their signs to assess whether the differences are centred around zero.

The null hypothesis is often:

\[ H_0:\text{the distribution of paired differences is centred at }0 \]

It is not the same as the Mann–Whitney U test because the data are paired.

10. Kruskal–Wallis test

The Kruskal–Wallis test compares more than two independent groups using ranks. It is a rank-based analogue of one-way ANOVA.

The null hypothesis is:

\[ H_0:\text{all groups have the same distribution} \]

If the test rejects the null, it tells us that not all groups are similar, but it does not identify which specific groups differ. Follow-up comparisons are needed.

12. Common mistakes

  • Using chi-square on percentages without counts: chi-square tests need counts.
  • Ignoring small expected counts: sparse tables may require exact or alternative methods.
  • Thinking chi-square gives direction automatically: inspect cells and residuals.
  • Calling non-parametric tests assumption-free: they still have assumptions.
  • Using Mann–Whitney for paired data: use a paired rank test instead.
  • Saying Mann–Whitney always tests medians: that requires additional distributional conditions.
  • Stopping at Kruskal–Wallis: a significant result usually needs post-hoc comparisons.
Notes summary
  • Chi-square tests are for categorical counts.
  • The chi-square statistic is \(\sum (O-E)^2/E\).
  • Goodness-of-fit compares one categorical distribution to expected probabilities.
  • Independence tests compare observed and expected counts in contingency tables.
  • Non-parametric tests often use ranks and reduce reliance on normality.
  • Correct test choice depends on data type, grouping, pairing and sample size.

Practice exercises

Chi-square and non-parametric tests

Exercise 1
Goodness-of-fit expected counts
Easy+

A fair die is rolled 120 times. Under the null hypothesis of fairness, what is the expected count for each face?

Answer

Each face has probability \(1/6\). Therefore \(E_i=120(1/6)=20\) for each face.

Exercise 2
Chi-square contribution
Easy+

If \(O=30\) and \(E=20\) for one category, calculate that category's contribution to \(\chi^2\).

Answer

The contribution is \((O-E)^2/E=(30-20)^2/20=100/20=5\).

Exercise 3
Degrees of freedom for independence
Medium+

A contingency table has 3 rows and 4 columns. What are the degrees of freedom for a chi-square test of independence?

Answer

\(\nu=(r-1)(c-1)=(3-1)(4-1)=2(3)=6\).

Exercise 4
Expected cell count
Medium+

In a contingency table, a row total is 40, a column total is 50, and the grand total is 200. Calculate the expected count for that cell under independence.

Answer

\(E=(40)(50)/200=2000/200=10\).

Exercise 5
Small expected counts
Medium+

Why should we be cautious using a chi-square test when expected counts are very small?

Answer

The chi-square reference distribution is a large-sample approximation. With very small expected counts, that approximation may be poor, so p-values may be unreliable.

Exercise 6
Choose a non-parametric test
Hard+

Two independent groups are compared on an ordinal pain score. Which non-parametric test is commonly considered?

Answer

The Mann–Whitney U test is commonly considered because there are two independent groups and an ordinal outcome.

Exercise 7
Paired non-parametric design
Hard+

A symptom score is measured before and after treatment in the same 20 patients, and differences are highly skewed. Which test might be considered?

Answer

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test may be considered because the data are paired and the paired differences are not well suited to a paired t-test.

Exercise 8
Mann–Whitney interpretation
Hard+

Why is it too simple to say the Mann–Whitney U test always tests equality of medians?

Answer

The median interpretation is most natural when the distributions have similar shapes and differ mainly by location. More generally, Mann–Whitney compares rank distributions or distributional tendency.

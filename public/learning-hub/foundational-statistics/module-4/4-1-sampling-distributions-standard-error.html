Sampling distributions and standard error

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~45 min · After probability and CLT
📍 Scene — statistics classroom, first inference lesson
EM
Emma

Module 3 ended with the Central Limit Theorem. Now we are starting inference. What exactly changes?

MR
Mr. R

We move from describing probability models to using sample data to learn about population quantities. Inference begins when a statistic calculated from one sample is used to estimate a parameter in a wider population.

OL
Oliver

So if I calculate a sample mean, that sample mean is not the final truth. It is an estimate?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. It is one estimate produced by one sample. A different random sample would usually give a different estimate.

The inferential leap

Suppose we measure systolic blood pressure in 80 patients and find a sample mean of 128 mmHg. That number describes the 80 patients. But if we want to say something about all similar patients, we must recognise that our sample mean is uncertain.

The uncertainty does not mean the calculation is wrong. It means the estimate depends on which sample happened to be selected.

JA
James

So sampling variation is the reason two studies can estimate slightly different means even if they target the same population?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. Random sampling variation is unavoidable. Statistics does not pretend it disappears. It measures it.

SO
Sophia

Is that what standard error measures?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. Standard error measures how much a statistic would vary from sample to sample.

Parameter, statistic, estimator, estimate

Before standard error, we need clear language. A parameter is a population quantity. A statistic is calculated from sample data. An estimator is the rule, and an estimate is the realised number.

TermMeaningExample
ParameterFixed but usually unknown population quantity\(\mu\), the population mean
StatisticQuantity calculated from sample data\(\bar{X}\), the sample mean as a random variable
EstimatorRule for estimating a parameterUse \(\bar{X}\) to estimate \(\mu\)
EstimateObserved numerical value from the sample\(\bar{x}=128\)
EM
Emma

Why do you write capital \(\bar{X}\) and lower-case \(\bar{x}\)?

MR
Mr. R

Capital \(\bar{X}\) represents the sample mean before data are observed, so it is random. Lower-case \(\bar{x}\) is the observed value after data have been collected.

Sampling distribution

The sampling distribution of a statistic is the probability distribution of that statistic across repeated random samples of the same size from the same population.

OL
Oliver

But in real life we usually take one sample, not thousands of samples.

MR
Mr. R

True. The sampling distribution is often theoretical. It describes what would happen if the sampling process were repeated many times.

JA
James

So it is not the distribution of individual patient blood pressures?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. That is a common mistake. The raw data distribution describes individuals. The sampling distribution describes a statistic, such as the sample mean.

Standard error

The standard error is the standard deviation of a sampling distribution. It measures the typical sample-to-sample variation of an estimator.

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

For a sample proportion, the standard error is:

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\hat{p})=\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}} \]
SO
Sophia

So standard deviation describes individuals, but standard error describes estimates?

MR
Mr. R

Perfect. That distinction is essential for interpreting statistical results.

Why \(n\) matters

The standard error of the sample mean decreases as \(1/\sqrt{n}\). This means increasing sample size improves precision, but with diminishing returns.

\[ \text{To halve } \operatorname{SE}(\bar{X}), \text{ multiply } n \text{ by }4. \]
EM
Emma

So doubling the sample size does not halve the standard error?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. Because of the square root, you need four times the sample size to halve the standard error.

Bias versus sampling variability

Standard error measures random sampling variability. It does not measure bias. A huge biased study can estimate the wrong target very precisely.

OL
Oliver

If I survey 50,000 people online, the standard error may be tiny. But if the sample is not representative, the result can still be wrong?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Large sample size reduces random error, not systematic bias.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Inference uses sample statistics to estimate population parameters.
  • A statistic varies from sample to sample.
  • The sampling distribution describes the repeated-sampling behaviour of a statistic.
  • Standard error is the standard deviation of a sampling distribution.
  • Standard deviation describes individuals; standard error describes estimates.
  • For the sample mean, \(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\sigma/\sqrt{n}\).
  • Larger sample size reduces random error but does not remove bias.
Detailed theoretical notes

Sampling distributions and standard error

1. Why inference needs sampling distributions

A sample statistic is only one possible result from a sampling process. If we repeated the sampling process, the statistic would usually change. Inferential statistics studies this variation mathematically.

Core idea

A sampling distribution is the probability distribution of a statistic over repeated random samples of the same size from the same population.

2. Parameter, statistic, estimator, estimate

TermSymbolMeaning
Population mean\(\mu\)Fixed unknown parameter
Sample mean as random variable\(\bar{X}\)Statistic before observing data
Observed sample mean\(\bar{x}\)Numerical estimate after observing data
Population proportion\(p\)Fixed unknown probability/proportion
Sample proportion\(\hat{p}\)Statistic estimating \(p\)

3. Sample mean as an estimator

Let \(X_1,X_2,\ldots,X_n\) be independent observations from a population with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). The sample mean is:

\[ \bar{X}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i \]

Expectation of the sample mean

\[ E(\bar{X})= E\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}E(X_i) = \frac{1}{n}(n\mu) = \mu \]

Because \(E(\bar{X})=\mu\), the sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean.

Variance of the sample mean

Assuming independence:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}) = \operatorname{Var}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\operatorname{Var}(X_i) \]
\[ \operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{1}{n^2}(n\sigma^2) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \]

Standard error of the sample mean

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\bar{X}) = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})} = \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}{n}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

4. Standard deviation versus standard error

QuantityDescribesQuestion answered
Standard deviationSpread of individual observationsHow variable are individuals?
Standard errorSpread of estimates across repeated samplesHow uncertain is the estimate?

5. Estimated standard error

In practice, \(\sigma\) is usually unknown. We estimate it using the sample standard deviation \(s\). Therefore:

\[ \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\bar{X})=\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]

The hat reminds us that the standard error itself is estimated from the data.

6. Sample proportion and its standard error

If \(X\) is the number of successes in \(n\) independent Bernoulli trials, then:

\[ X\sim \operatorname{Binomial}(n,p) \]

The sample proportion is:

\[ \hat{p}=\frac{X}{n} \]

Expectation

\[ E(\hat{p})=E\left(\frac{X}{n}\right)=\frac{1}{n}E(X)=\frac{np}{n}=p \]

Variance

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\hat{p}) = \operatorname{Var}\left(\frac{X}{n}\right) = \frac{1}{n^2}\operatorname{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{n^2}np(1-p) = \frac{p(1-p)}{n} \]

Standard error

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\hat{p})= \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}} \]

Because \(p\) is usually unknown, we often estimate this as:

\[ \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\hat{p})= \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \]

7. Central Limit Theorem connection

Under suitable conditions, the Central Limit Theorem gives:

\[ \bar{X}\approx N\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \]

or after standardisation:

\[ \frac{\bar{X}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\approx N(0,1) \]

This is the mathematical foundation for confidence intervals and many hypothesis tests.

8. Effect of sample size

For the sample mean:

\[ \operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

If sample size is multiplied by \(c\), the standard error is divided by \(\sqrt{c}\). Therefore:

\[ n \text{ multiplied by }4 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \operatorname{SE} \text{ divided by }2 \]

9. Bias and standard error are different

Standard error measures random variation of the estimator. Bias is systematic displacement from the target parameter.

\[ \operatorname{Bias}(\hat{\theta})=E(\hat{\theta})-\theta \]

An estimator can have small standard error but large bias. That means it repeatedly estimates the wrong target very precisely.

Important warning

A large sample size can make random error small, but it cannot automatically repair poor sampling, measurement bias, confounding, or selection bias.

10. Common mistakes

  • Confusing raw-data distribution and sampling distribution: one describes individuals; the other describes a statistic.
  • Confusing standard deviation and standard error: SD is individual spread; SE is estimator uncertainty.
  • Thinking larger \(n\) removes bias: larger \(n\) mainly reduces random error.
  • Using \(\sigma\) when it is unknown without saying it is estimated: in real data, use \(s/\sqrt{n}\).
  • Assuming normality of raw data is always required: for means, CLT often supports approximate normality of \(\bar{X}\), not necessarily of raw data.
Notes summary
  • The sample mean is a random variable before data are observed.
  • The sampling distribution describes repeated-sampling behaviour of a statistic.
  • \(E(\bar{X})=\mu\), so \(\bar{X}\) is unbiased for \(\mu\).
  • \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})=\sigma^2/n\).
  • \(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\sigma/\sqrt{n}\).
  • \(\operatorname{SE}(\hat{p})=\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}\).
  • Standard error measures precision, not validity of study design.

Practice exercises

Sampling distributions and standard error

Exercise 1
Standard error of mean
Easy+

If \(\sigma=18\) and \(n=36\), calculate \(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})\).

Answer

\(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})=\sigma/\sqrt{n}=18/\sqrt{36}=18/6=3\).

Exercise 2
Standard deviation versus standard error
Easy+

Explain the difference between standard deviation and standard error.

Answer

Standard deviation describes variability among individual observations. Standard error describes variability of an estimate across repeated samples.

Exercise 3
Estimated standard error
Medium+

A sample has \(s=12\) and \(n=64\). Estimate \(\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X})\).

Answer

\(\widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\bar{X})=s/\sqrt{n}=12/\sqrt{64}=12/8=1.5\).

Exercise 4
Standard error of a proportion
Medium+

If \(p=0.30\) and \(n=200\), calculate \(\operatorname{SE}(\hat{p})\).

Answer

\(\operatorname{SE}(\hat{p})=\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}=\sqrt{0.30(0.70)/200}=\sqrt{0.00105}\approx0.0324\).

Exercise 5
Square-root law
Medium+

If sample size increases from 50 to 200, what happens to the standard error of the mean?

Answer

The sample size is multiplied by 4. Since standard error is proportional to \(1/\sqrt{n}\), the standard error is divided by 2. It is halved.

Exercise 6
Derive variance of sample mean
Hard+

Derive \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})=\sigma^2/n\) for independent observations with common variance \(\sigma^2\).

Answer

\(\bar{X}=n^{-1}\sum X_i\). Therefore \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})=\operatorname{Var}(n^{-1}\sum X_i)=n^{-2}\sum\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=n^{-2}(n\sigma^2)=\sigma^2/n\), using independence.

Exercise 7
Bias and precision
Hard+

Explain why a very large biased sample can be misleading.

Answer

A large sample can produce a very small standard error, so the estimate may look precise. But if the sampling process is biased, the estimate may be precisely centred around the wrong target.

Exercise 8
Sampling distribution wording
Hard+

A student says, “The sampling distribution is just the histogram of my observed data.” Explain the error.

Answer

The histogram of observed data describes individual observations in one sample. The sampling distribution describes how a statistic, such as \(\bar{X}\), varies across repeated samples.

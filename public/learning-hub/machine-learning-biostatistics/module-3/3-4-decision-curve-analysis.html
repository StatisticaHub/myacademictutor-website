Opening scene: The model meets the decision

“That is the key question. A model should improve decisions, not just metrics.”

Confusion Bug:
“I thought good AUC automatically means clinical usefulness.”

Prof Stat:
“Not always. That is why decision curve analysis can be useful.”

Today’s big question

Does the model help make better clinical decisions?

Decision curve analysis is used to evaluate the clinical usefulness of a prediction model. It asks whether using the model gives more benefit than simple alternatives such as treating everyone or treating no one.

A prediction model is clinically useful only if it improves decisions at relevant risk thresholds.

Intuition first

Prediction should support action

A model is clinically useful only if it changes decisions in a helpful way.

For example, it may help decide:

  • who should receive further testing;
  • who should be referred;
  • who should receive treatment;
  • who should be monitored more closely;
  • who can be reassured.

Good statistical performance does not automatically mean good clinical usefulness.

Threshold probability

The risk level at which action is taken

A threshold probability is the risk level at which clinical action would be taken.

If predicted risk ≥ 20%, refer for further testing.

Here, 20% is the threshold probability.

Curious Learner:
“Why does the threshold matter?”

Dr Clinic:
“Because action depends on risk. I may act at 5% risk for a harmless test, but not for a risky treatment.”

Prof Stat:
“Exactly. The threshold reflects the trade-off between false positives and false negatives.”

Low threshold

Safe, cheap, non-invasive action

Reasonable when missing disease is harmful and follow-up action has low burden.

High threshold

Risky, costly, invasive action

Reasonable when false positives can lead to harm, cost, anxiety, or overtreatment.

Decision strategies

Treat all, treat none and model-based decisions

StrategyMeaning
Treat allEvery patient receives the action.
Treat noneNo patient receives the action.
Model-basedAction depends on predicted risk.

A model is useful if it gives higher net benefit than both treat-all and treat-none across relevant thresholds.

Net benefit

Balancing true positives and false positives

Decision curve analysis uses a measure called net benefit.

Net benefit combines true positives, false positives and threshold probability.

Higher net benefit means a better decision strategy.

A true positive is beneficial because the model correctly identifies a patient who may need action. A false positive is harmful because the model recommends action for a patient who does not need it.

The threshold probability determines how much false positives are penalised. At a low threshold, false positives are penalised less. At a high threshold, false positives are penalised more.

Medical example

Diabetes testing

Suppose a model predicts diabetes risk and the clinic considers further testing if risk is at least 20%.

Possible strategies are:

  • test everyone;
  • test nobody;
  • test only if predicted risk ≥ 20%.

Decision curve analysis asks whether the model-based strategy gives higher net benefit than testing everyone or testing nobody.

Clinical reality check

Before using decision curve analysis, ask:

  • What action will be taken?
  • What threshold risk would trigger action?
  • What are the harms of false positives?
  • What are the harms of false negatives?
  • Is the model useful across realistic thresholds?

AUC and calibration are not enough

Clinical usefulness needs decision context

Common mistake: assuming strong AUC means clinical usefulness

A model can discriminate well but still not improve clinical decisions.

Common mistake: ignoring calibration

If the model is poorly calibrated, threshold-based decisions may be misleading.

Decision curve analysis should focus on clinically relevant thresholds. If clinicians would only act between 10% and 30% risk, usefulness outside that range is less relevant.

Detailed notes

Note 9: Decision Curve Analysis and Clinical Usefulness

A prediction model is clinically useful only if it improves decisions at relevant risk thresholds.

1. Why this note matters

A prediction model can have good statistical performance but still be clinically unhelpful. It may have good AUC, good calibration and acceptable sensitivity and specificity, but this does not automatically mean that using it will improve patient care.

A clinical prediction model should support a decision. Decision curve analysis helps evaluate whether using a model improves decisions compared with simpler strategies.

2. From prediction to decision

Many prediction models estimate risk, such as predicted diabetes risk, cancer recurrence risk or readmission risk. But risk prediction alone is not the final goal. The question is what should be done with the prediction.

Possible actions include further testing, referral, treatment, closer monitoring, reassurance and follow-up planning.

3. The clinical decision

Before using decision curve analysis, define the decision clearly.

  • Should this patient receive further diabetes testing?
  • Should this patient receive additional cancer follow-up?
  • Should this patient be referred to a specialist?

Without a clear decision, decision curve analysis becomes difficult to interpret.

4. Threshold probability

A threshold probability is the risk level at which action is taken.

Refer for further testing if predicted risk ≥ 20%

The threshold reflects the point at which the expected benefit of action is judged to outweigh the potential harm, cost or burden.

5. Treat-all, treat-none and model-based strategies

StrategyMeaningPossible limitation
Treat allEvery patient receives the action.Unnecessary testing, anxiety, cost or overtreatment.
Treat noneNo patient receives the action.Patients who could benefit may be missed.
Model-basedAction depends on predicted risk.Useful only if it improves the benefit-harm balance.

6. Net benefit

Net benefit combines true positives, false positives and threshold probability. It rewards correct identification of patients who should receive action and penalises unnecessary action in patients who do not need it.

At a low threshold, false positives are penalised less. At a high threshold, false positives are penalised more.

7. Medical examples

In diabetes testing, a clinic may refer patients for further testing if predicted risk is at least 20%. Decision curve analysis asks whether the model-based strategy gives higher net benefit than testing everyone or testing nobody.

In cancer follow-up, intensive follow-up may involve cost, anxiety, imaging and clinic visits. Decision curve analysis can assess whether a recurrence model improves decisions across plausible follow-up thresholds.

8. Why AUC is not enough

AUC measures discrimination, but it does not tell us whether using the model improves decisions. A model can have good AUC and still be clinically unhelpful if it does not improve net benefit at relevant thresholds.

9. Why calibration is still important

Decision curve analysis uses predicted probabilities. If the model is poorly calibrated, threshold-based decisions may be misleading. An overestimating model may place too many patients above the treatment threshold, while an underestimating model may miss high-risk patients.

10. Relevant threshold range

Decision curve analysis should focus on clinically relevant thresholds. If clinicians would only act between 10% and 30% predicted risk, then model usefulness outside this range may be less relevant.

11. Interpreting a decision curve

A model is potentially useful when its net benefit is higher than both treat-all and treat-none across thresholds that make clinical sense. If a model has higher net benefit only at unrealistic thresholds, it may not be useful in practice.

12. Clinical usefulness depends on context

The same model may be useful in one setting but not another. Clinical usefulness depends on:

  • the decision;
  • the intervention;
  • harms and benefits;
  • disease prevalence;
  • patient preferences;
  • healthcare resources.

13. Common mistakes

  • Assuming high AUC means clinical usefulness.
  • Using decision curve analysis without a clear decision.
  • Ignoring threshold relevance.
  • Ignoring calibration.
  • Overclaiming clinical value.

14. Reporting decision curve analysis

A clear report should describe:

  • target population;
  • prediction model;
  • clinical decision;
  • action triggered by the model;
  • threshold probability range;
  • why the threshold range is clinically relevant;
  • comparison strategies;
  • validation dataset;
  • interpretation of net benefit.

15. Summary

Decision curve analysis evaluates clinical usefulness. It asks whether using a model improves decisions compared with treating everyone or treating no one.

A decision curve should always be interpreted in context: what decision is being made, what threshold range is realistic, what harms are involved, and whether the model has been validated.

R practical: decision curve analysis

This script implements a simple decision curve analysis manually. It compares a model-based strategy with treat-all and treat-none strategies.

Model strategy

Action is recommended when predicted risk is above the threshold.

Treat all

Every patient receives the action.

Treat none

No patient receives the action; net benefit is zero.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 09: Decision Curve Analysis
#
# This script implements a simple decision curve analysis manually.
# It compares a model-based strategy with treat-all and treat-none strategies.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)

set.seed(123)

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

train_index <- sample(seq_len(nrow(diabetes_data)), size = round(0.80 * nrow(diabetes_data)))

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

model <- glm(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  family = binomial
)

test_data <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_risk = predict(model, newdata = test_data, type = "response"),
    observed = ifelse(diabetes == "pos", 1, 0)
  )

calculate_net_benefit <- function(observed, predicted_risk, threshold) {
  predicted_positive <- ifelse(predicted_risk >= threshold, 1, 0)

  tp <- sum(predicted_positive == 1 & observed == 1)
  fp <- sum(predicted_positive == 1 & observed == 0)

  n <- length(observed)

  net_benefit <- (tp / n) - (fp / n) * (threshold / (1 - threshold))

  net_benefit
}

calculate_treat_all_net_benefit <- function(observed, threshold) {
  prevalence <- mean(observed == 1)

  prevalence - (1 - prevalence) * (threshold / (1 - threshold))
}

thresholds <- seq(0.05, 0.80, by = 0.01)

decision_curve <- tibble(
  threshold = thresholds
) %>%
  mutate(
    model = map_dbl(
      threshold,
      ~ calculate_net_benefit(
        observed = test_data$observed,
        predicted_risk = test_data$predicted_risk,
        threshold = .x
      )
    ),
    treat_all = map_dbl(
      threshold,
      ~ calculate_treat_all_net_benefit(
        observed = test_data$observed,
        threshold = .x
      )
    ),
    treat_none = 0
  )

cat("\nFirst few decision curve results:\n")
print(head(decision_curve))

plot(
  decision_curve$threshold,
  decision_curve$model,
  type = "l",
  lwd = 2,
  ylim = range(decision_curve$model, decision_curve$treat_all, decision_curve$treat_none),
  xlab = "Threshold probability",
  ylab = "Net benefit",
  main = "Decision Curve Analysis"
)

lines(decision_curve$threshold, decision_curve$treat_all, lty = 2, lwd = 2)
lines(decision_curve$threshold, decision_curve$treat_none, lty = 3, lwd = 2)

legend(
  "topright",
  legend = c("Model", "Treat all", "Treat none"),
  lty = c(1, 2, 3),
  lwd = 2,
  bty = "n"
)

relevant_thresholds <- decision_curve %>%
  filter(threshold >= 0.10, threshold <= 0.40) %>%
  summarise(
    mean_model_net_benefit = mean(model),
    mean_treat_all_net_benefit = mean(treat_all),
    mean_treat_none_net_benefit = mean(treat_none)
  )

cat("\nAverage net benefit between thresholds 0.10 and 0.40:\n")
print(relevant_thresholds)

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. Decision curve analysis evaluates clinical usefulness.\n")
cat("2. Threshold probability reflects when action is taken.\n")
cat("3. A model is useful if it improves net benefit over simple alternatives.\n")
cat("4. Good AUC does not automatically mean clinical usefulness.\n")
cat("5. Decision curves must be interpreted using clinically realistic thresholds.\n")

Question 6: Screening context

For a screening test with low cost and low harm, would the threshold probability usually be low or high?

Show suggested answer

Question 7: Treatment context

For a treatment with serious side effects, would the threshold probability usually be low or high?

Show suggested answer

Usually higher, because unnecessary treatment can harm patients.

Question 8: Medical interpretation

A model has high net benefit only between threshold probabilities of 10% and 30%. What should you consider before recommending the model?

Show suggested answer

Consider whether 10% to 30% is a realistic action range for the clinical decision. Also consider calibration, validation, harms of false positives and false negatives, resource use and patient preferences.

Today’s big question

Do predicted probabilities agree with observed risks?

Calibration is about whether predicted probabilities are believable. If a model says a group of patients has 30% risk, then about 30% of those patients should experience the outcome.

Calibration asks whether predicted risks match observed outcome frequencies.

Brier score

Probability prediction error

The Brier score measures the average squared difference between predicted probabilities and observed outcomes.

Brier score = mean((predicted probability - observed outcome)2)

For binary outcomes, observed outcome is:

  • 1 = event occurred;
  • 0 = event did not occur.

Lower Brier scores are better.

Good prediction

Predicted risk = 0.80, observed = 1

Squared error = (0.80 - 1)2 = 0.04

Poor prediction

Predicted risk = 0.80, observed = 0

Squared error = (0.80 - 0)2 = 0.64

Clinical reality check

Poor calibration affects real decisions

Clinical reality check

If a model overestimates risk, patients may receive unnecessary testing or treatment.

If a model underestimates risk, high-risk patients may be missed.

Poor calibration can affect patient communication, clinical decisions, and resource use.

Common mistake: reporting AUC but ignoring calibration

AUC is useful, but it does not tell us whether predicted probabilities are correct. For clinical decision-making, calibration is essential because decisions are often based on absolute risk.

Detailed notes

Note 8: Calibration and Brier Score in Medical Prediction Models

A medical prediction model should not only rank patients correctly. It should also give predicted risks that agree with observed risks.

Learning objectives

  • Explain what calibration means in clinical prediction modelling.
  • Distinguish calibration from discrimination.
  • Interpret calibration plots.
  • Explain overestimation and underestimation of risk.
  • Describe calibration intercept and calibration slope.
  • Explain what the Brier score measures.
  • Understand why calibration is important for clinical decisions.
  • Recognise why a model with high AUC may still be clinically unreliable.

1. Why this note matters

Many medical prediction models produce predicted probabilities, such as predicted diabetes risk, predicted cancer recurrence risk, or predicted hospital readmission risk. These probabilities are useful only if they are meaningful.

If a model says that a group of patients has a 40% risk, then approximately 40% of those patients should experience the outcome. This is the idea of calibration.

2. Discrimination versus calibration

Discrimination asks whether the model can separate patients with the outcome from patients without the outcome. Calibration asks whether predicted probabilities match observed risks.

AUC measures discrimination. Calibration assesses whether predicted risks are numerically reliable. A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration.

3. Example: good AUC but poor calibration

Suppose a diabetes model correctly gives higher predicted risks to most patients with diabetes than to patients without diabetes. This may produce a good AUC. However, if the average predicted risk is 60% but observed risk is 30%, the model ranks patients well but overestimates absolute risk.

4. What does calibration mean?

Calibration asks: when the model predicts x% risk, is the observed risk close to x%?

  • Among patients predicted around 10% risk, about 10% should have the outcome.
  • Among patients predicted around 30% risk, about 30% should have the outcome.
  • Among patients predicted around 70% risk, about 70% should have the outcome.

If this is approximately true across the risk range, the model is well calibrated.

5. Why calibration matters in medicine

Calibration matters because clinical decisions often depend on absolute risk.

Offer treatment if predicted risk is at least 20%.

If the model overestimates risk, patients may receive unnecessary treatment. If the model underestimates risk, high-risk patients may not receive needed treatment.

6. Calibration plot

A calibration plot compares predicted risk with observed risk.

  • x-axis: predicted risk;
  • y-axis: observed risk.

A perfectly calibrated model would follow the diagonal line.

  • Points close to the diagonal suggest good calibration.
  • Points above the diagonal suggest the model is underestimating risk.
  • Points below the diagonal suggest the model is overestimating risk.

7. Grouped and smooth calibration

One simple way to create a calibration plot is to group patients by predicted risk. For each group, we calculate mean predicted risk and observed outcome proportion.

Smooth calibration curves can show patterns across the range of predicted risks, but they require careful interpretation, especially in small datasets.

8. Calibration intercept and slope

Calibration intercept

Ideal value = 0

Measures systematic overprediction or underprediction.

Calibration slope

Ideal value = 1

Measures whether predictions are too extreme or not extreme enough.

A calibration slope less than 1 often suggests overfitting. High risks may be predicted too high, and low risks may be predicted too low.

9. Brier score

The Brier score measures the average squared difference between predicted probabilities and observed outcomes.

Brier score = mean((predicted probability - observed outcome)2)

For binary outcomes:

  • observed outcome = 1 if event occurred;
  • observed outcome = 0 if event did not occur.

Lower Brier scores are better.

10. Simple Brier score examples

Predicted risk Observed outcome Squared error Interpretation
0.80 1 (0.80 - 1)2 = 0.04 Good prediction
0.80 0 (0.80 - 0)2 = 0.64 Poor prediction

11. Interpreting the Brier score

The Brier score captures overall probability prediction error. It reflects aspects of discrimination, calibration, and prediction accuracy. However, it is not always intuitive by itself.

It should usually be interpreted together with AUC, calibration plot, calibration slope, calibration intercept and clinical context.

12. Calibration in external validation

Calibration often changes when a model is applied to a new population. For example, a model developed in one hospital may overestimate risk in another hospital if the outcome is less common there.

External validation should assess calibration, not only AUC. A model may transport reasonably well in terms of ranking but require recalibration before use.

13. Recalibration

Recalibration means updating the model so that predicted risks better match observed risks in a new population. This may involve adjusting the intercept, slope, baseline risk, or model coefficients.

Recalibration should be done carefully and transparently.

14. Clinical examples

In diabetes risk prediction, a predicted average risk of 40% should correspond to roughly 40% observed risk in similar patients. If observed risk is 65%, the model underestimates risk. If observed risk is 20%, the model overestimates risk.

In cancer recurrence prediction, poor calibration could lead to overtreatment, undertreatment, unnecessary anxiety or false reassurance.

15. Common mistakes

  • Reporting only AUC.
  • Assuming high AUC means good calibration.
  • Ignoring calibration in external validation.
  • Using arbitrary risk thresholds without checking calibration.
  • Treating calibration as optional.

16. Reporting calibration

A strong report should include:

  • calibration plot;
  • calibration intercept;
  • calibration slope;
  • Brier score;
  • AUC or other discrimination measure;
  • validation method;
  • explanation of clinical implications.

17. Summary

Calibration is central to medical prediction modelling because predicted probabilities are often used to guide decisions. A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration.

The Brier score summarises probability prediction error, while calibration plots and calibration slope/intercept help diagnose how predicted risks behave.

A model with good AUC is not automatically clinically reliable. For medical prediction, predicted probabilities should be calibrated so that risk estimates are meaningful.

R code

R practical: calibration plot and Brier score

This script assesses calibration and calculates the Brier score for a diabetes prediction model. It fits logistic regression, calculates AUC, groups patients by predicted risk, creates a calibration plot, and estimates a simple calibration intercept and slope.

Brier score

Measures average squared prediction error for predicted probabilities.

Calibration plot

Compares mean predicted risk with observed risk across risk groups.

Calibration model

Estimates a rough calibration intercept and slope from predicted risks.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 08: Calibration and Brier Score
#
# This script assesses calibration and calculates the Brier score
# for a diabetes prediction model.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)
library(pROC)

set.seed(123)

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

train_index <- sample(seq_len(nrow(diabetes_data)), size = round(0.80 * nrow(diabetes_data)))

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

model <- glm(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  family = binomial
)

test_data <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_risk = predict(model, newdata = test_data, type = "response"),
    observed = ifelse(diabetes == "pos", 1, 0)
  )

brier_score <- mean((test_data$predicted_risk - test_data$observed)^2)

cat("\nBrier score:\n")
print(brier_score)

roc_object <- roc(
  response = test_data$diabetes,
  predictor = test_data$predicted_risk,
  levels = c("neg", "pos"),
  quiet = TRUE
)

cat("\nAUC:\n")
print(auc(roc_object))

calibration_data <- test_data %>%
  mutate(risk_group = ntile(predicted_risk, 10)) %>%
  group_by(risk_group) %>%
  summarise(
    mean_predicted_risk = mean(predicted_risk),
    observed_risk = mean(observed),
    n = n(),
    .groups = "drop"
  )

cat("\nCalibration data:\n")
print(calibration_data)

plot(
  calibration_data$mean_predicted_risk,
  calibration_data$observed_risk,
  xlim = c(0, 1),
  ylim = c(0, 1),
  pch = 19,
  xlab = "Mean predicted risk",
  ylab = "Observed risk",
  main = "Calibration Plot"
)

lines(
  calibration_data$mean_predicted_risk,
  calibration_data$observed_risk
)

abline(a = 0, b = 1, lty = 2)

# Calibration model
#
# A simple calibration model regresses observed outcome on the logit
# of predicted risk. This gives a rough calibration intercept and slope.

eps <- 1e-6

test_data <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_risk_safe = pmin(pmax(predicted_risk, eps), 1 - eps),
    logit_predicted_risk = log(predicted_risk_safe / (1 - predicted_risk_safe))
  )

calibration_model <- glm(
  observed ~ logit_predicted_risk,
  data = test_data,
  family = binomial
)

calibration_intercept <- coef(calibration_model)[1]
calibration_slope <- coef(calibration_model)[2]

calibration_summary <- tibble(
  calibration_intercept = calibration_intercept,
  calibration_slope = calibration_slope,
  brier_score = brier_score,
  auc = as.numeric(auc(roc_object))
)

cat("\nCalibration summary:\n")
print(calibration_summary)

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. AUC assesses discrimination, not calibration.\n")
cat("2. Calibration checks whether predicted risks match observed risks.\n")
cat("3. Points above the diagonal suggest underestimation of risk.\n")
cat("4. Points below the diagonal suggest overestimation of risk.\n")
cat("5. The Brier score measures probability prediction error.\n")

Today’s big question

What is supervised learning, and how do we use it correctly with medical data?

Supervised learning means learning a relationship between predictors and an observed outcome.

The data usually look like this:

Patient characteristics → observed outcome

The model uses past examples to learn patterns that may help predict outcomes for future patients.

Intuition first

Supervised learning is like learning from previous clinical cases

Think of supervised learning like clinical teaching.

A trainee clinician learns from previous cases:

  • symptoms;
  • test results;
  • diagnosis;
  • treatment history;
  • outcome.

Over time, the trainee starts recognising patterns.

A supervised learning model does something similar, but using data.

Supervised learning uses examples with known outcomes to learn how predictors relate to the outcome.

Examples:

Medical example

Diabetes prediction

Suppose we want to predict whether a patient has diabetes at clinical assessment.

Possible predictors:

  • glucose;
  • BMI;
  • age;
  • insulin;
  • blood pressure;
  • number of pregnancies.

Outcome: diabetes status: positive or negative.

Prediction time point: at clinical assessment.

This is a supervised learning problem because we have both predictors and known diabetes status in the dataset.

Types of supervised learning

Regression, classification and survival prediction

Regression

Used when the outcome is continuous.

Example: predict systolic blood pressure.

Classification

Used when the outcome is categorical.

Example: predict diabetes positive or negative.

Survival prediction

Used when the outcome is time-to-event.

Example: predict time to cancer recurrence.

Medical data often involve all three types.

Binary classification

Estimating the probability of a positive outcome

Binary classification is very common in medical prediction.

Examples:

  • disease yes/no;
  • hospital readmission yes/no;
  • treatment response yes/no;
  • recurrence yes/no;
  • ICU admission yes/no;
  • mortality yes/no.

The model usually estimates:

P(outcome = positive | predictors)

For example:

P(diabetes = positive | glucose, BMI, age) = 0.72

This means the model estimates a 72% probability that the patient belongs to the diabetes-positive group.

Common mistake

Unclear outcome definition

Common mistake: unclear outcome definition

A prediction model is only as clear as its outcome.

For example, “diabetes” could mean:

  • self-reported diabetes;
  • doctor-diagnosed diabetes;
  • medication use;
  • glucose threshold;
  • HbA1c threshold;
  • hospital record diagnosis.

These are not always equivalent.

If the outcome is unclear, the model interpretation becomes unclear.

Clinical reality check

Before fitting a supervised learning model

Clinical reality check

Before fitting a supervised learning model, ask:

  • What exactly is the outcome?
  • What is the positive class?
  • When is the prediction made?
  • Are all predictors available at that time?
  • What decision could the prediction support?
  • What are the consequences of wrong predictions?

Prediction modelling is not just a technical exercise. It must match a real clinical question.

  3. Define candidate predictors.

1. Introduction

Supervised learning is one of the most important areas of machine learning for biostatistics and medical statistics. Many medical prediction problems are supervised learning problems because we usually have a dataset containing patient characteristics and known outcomes.

For example, we may have data on patients’ age, sex, blood pressure, glucose level, body mass index, treatment history, tumour stage, or biomarker values. We may also know whether each patient developed diabetes, experienced cancer recurrence, died within a certain time period, responded to treatment, or was readmitted to hospital.

The aim of supervised learning is to use the available predictors to learn a relationship with the outcome, and then use that learned relationship to make predictions for new patients.

Y = f(X) + error

where:

  • Y is the outcome;
  • X represents the predictors or features;
  • f(X) is the function learned from the data;
  • error represents unexplained variation.

This notation appears simple, but it captures the core idea behind supervised learning. We use observed examples to learn a mapping between predictors and outcomes. The model is then assessed by how well it performs on new individuals, not simply by how well it fits the training dataset.

2. What makes learning supervised?

The term supervised learning means that the algorithm learns from examples where the correct outcome is already known.

For each individual in the dataset, we observe:

Predictors + Outcome
Patient Age BMI HbA1c Blood pressure Diabetes status
15231.26.8142Yes
23924.85.1118No
36129.47.2151Yes

Here, diabetes status is the known outcome. Age, BMI, HbA1c, and blood pressure are predictors. The model uses these examples to learn patterns. Once trained, the model can be given a new patient’s predictors and return a predicted probability of diabetes.

This is different from unsupervised learning, where there is no labelled outcome. In unsupervised learning, the aim may be to identify clusters, reduce dimensionality, or discover structure in the data. Examples include clustering patients into subgroups or using principal component analysis to summarise high-dimensional omics data.

3. Supervised learning in biostatistics

Supervised learning is central to many medical and biomedical prediction problems.

Medical question Outcome Predictors
Can we predict diabetes within 10 years? Diabetes yes/no Age, BMI, glucose, HbA1c, family history
Can we predict 30-day readmission? Readmission yes/no Diagnosis, comorbidities, admission history, labs
Can we predict cancer recurrence? Recurrence yes/no or time to recurrence Tumour stage, grade, treatment, biomarkers
Can we predict treatment response? Responder/non-responder Clinical characteristics, molecular markers
Can we predict survival? Time to death or relapse Age, disease stage, biomarkers, treatment
Can we classify disease status from methylation data? Case/control CpG methylation markers

These examples show that supervised learning is not a single method. It is a framework. The specific model depends on the outcome type, sample size, predictors, missing data, and scientific question.

4. Outcome variable

The outcome is the variable we want to predict. In medical statistics, defining the outcome carefully is one of the most important steps. A vague or poorly defined outcome can make even a technically sophisticated model clinically meaningless.

OutcomeType
Systolic blood pressureContinuous
Diabetes statusBinary
Hospital readmission within 30 daysBinary
Cancer subtypeMulticlass
Treatment responseBinary or ordinal
Time to deathTime-to-event
Time to cancer recurrenceTime-to-event
DNA methylation level at a CpG siteContinuous

A good outcome definition should specify:

  • what event or measurement is being predicted;
  • how it is measured;
  • over what time horizon it is observed;
  • whether censoring is present;
  • whether the outcome is clinically meaningful;
  • whether the outcome is available consistently across patients.

For example, “mortality” is not specific enough. A better outcome definition would be:

All-cause mortality within 1 year of hospital admission.

5. Predictors

Predictors are variables used to make predictions.

In medical datasets, predictors may include demographic characteristics, symptoms, clinical measurements, laboratory values, imaging features, comorbidities, medication history, tumour characteristics, genetic or molecular markers, DNA methylation sites, gene expression values, and healthcare utilisation history.

The most important rule: predictors must be available at the intended prediction time.

For example, if we want to predict ICU mortality at ICU admission, we can use variables measured at or before ICU admission. We should not use ICU length of stay, discharge destination, or lab values measured after clinical deterioration because those variables would not be available at the time the prediction is made.

6. Prediction time point

The prediction time point is the moment at which the model is intended to make a prediction. This concept is often ignored in poor medical machine learning studies, but it is central to good biostatistical modelling.

Prediction problemPrediction time point
Predict ICU mortalityAt ICU admission
Predict 30-day readmissionAt hospital discharge
Predict 5-year cancer survivalAt diagnosis or treatment start
Predict treatment responseBefore treatment begins
Predict relapse after surgeryAt surgery or shortly after surgery
Predict disease using methylationAt sample collection

The prediction time point determines which predictors are legitimate. If a variable is measured after the prediction time point, it should usually not be used.

7. Regression, classification, and survival prediction

Supervised learning problems are often grouped according to the outcome type.

Regression

Regression is used when the outcome is continuous, such as blood pressure, cholesterol level, biomarker concentration, length of stay, or methylation beta values.

Classification

Classification is used when the outcome is categorical. Binary classification has two categories, such as disease yes/no or responder/non-responder. Multiclass classification has more than two categories, such as cancer subtype or risk group.

Survival prediction

Survival prediction is used when the outcome is time-to-event and censoring is present, such as time to death, relapse, recurrence, progression, or readmission. Survival machine learning will be covered later in the course.

8. Binary classification in medical prediction

Binary classification is one of the most common supervised learning tasks in medical statistics. Examples include disease status, death within 1 year, readmission within 30 days, treatment response, and cancer recurrence within 5 years.

In binary classification, the model usually estimates a probability.

Predicted probability of 30-day readmission = 0.35

This means that, based on the model and available predictors, the patient is estimated to have a 35% probability of readmission within 30 days.

A threshold can be used to convert the predicted probability into a classification. However, the threshold of 0.50 is not automatically appropriate in medicine. The choice of threshold should depend on the clinical context, outcome prevalence, harms of false positives, harms of false negatives, and intended use of the model.

9. Train/test split

To estimate how well a model performs on new patients, we usually need to evaluate it on data that were not used to train it. A simple approach is the train/test split.

  • Training set: used to fit the model.
  • Test set: used to evaluate model performance.

A common split is 80% training and 20% testing.

The test data are held back and should only be used for final evaluation. If we use the test set repeatedly to choose variables, tune hyperparameters, or decide between many models, it is no longer an honest test set.

10. Stratified splitting

In medical classification problems, outcomes are often imbalanced. For example, if disease prevalence is 10%, stratified splitting aims to keep approximately 10% outcome cases in both the training and test datasets.

This is useful because model training and evaluation can be unstable when too few outcome cases appear in either set.

11. Logistic regression as a baseline model

Logistic regression is one of the most important models for binary clinical prediction. Although it is a classical statistical model, it can also be understood as a supervised learning method because it learns a relationship between predictors and a binary outcome.

P(Y = 1 | X)

The model uses the logit link:

logit(p) = log(p / (1 - p))

Logistic regression is often a strong baseline model in medical prediction because it is:

  • interpretable;
  • relatively stable;
  • familiar to clinicians and statisticians;
  • able to produce predicted probabilities;
  • suitable for tabular clinical data;
  • easy to validate and calibrate;
  • extendable through penalisation such as ridge, lasso, and elastic net.

Before using more complex models such as random forests or gradient boosting, it is usually sensible to compare them with logistic regression.

12. From probabilities to classes

Many binary classifiers produce probabilities.

PatientPredicted probability of diseasePredicted class at threshold 0.50
A0.82Disease
B0.61Disease
C0.44No disease
D0.12No disease

In medicine, the threshold should be chosen carefully. For screening, we may use a lower threshold to identify more possible cases. For treatment decisions with serious side effects, we may use a higher threshold to avoid overtreatment.

13. Confusion matrix

A confusion matrix compares predicted classes with observed outcomes.

Predicted positivePredicted negative
Actual positiveTrue positiveFalse negative
Actual negativeFalse positiveTrue negative
  • True positive: the model predicts positive and the outcome is positive.
  • False positive: the model predicts positive but the outcome is negative.
  • True negative: the model predicts negative and the outcome is negative.
  • False negative: the model predicts negative but the outcome is positive.

14. Accuracy

Accuracy is the proportion of all patients correctly classified.

Accuracy = (True positives + True negatives) / Total

Accuracy is simple, but it can be misleading. If a disease has prevalence 2%, a model that predicts “no disease” for everyone will be 98% accurate but detects no disease cases.

15. Sensitivity

Sensitivity is the proportion of actual positive cases correctly identified.

Sensitivity = True positives / (True positives + False negatives)

Sensitivity answers: among patients who truly have the condition, how many did the model detect? High sensitivity is important when missing a case is harmful.

16. Specificity

Specificity is the proportion of actual negative cases correctly identified.

Specificity = True negatives / (True negatives + False positives)

Specificity answers: among patients who truly do not have the condition, how many did the model correctly classify as negative?

17. Positive predictive value

Positive predictive value, or PPV, is the proportion of predicted positives who truly have the outcome.

PPV = True positives / (True positives + False positives)

PPV is strongly affected by prevalence.

18. Negative predictive value

Negative predictive value, or NPV, is the proportion of predicted negatives who truly do not have the outcome.

NPV = True negatives / (True negatives + False negatives)

Like PPV, NPV depends on prevalence and should be interpreted alongside the clinical context.

19. ROC curve and AUC

The ROC curve shows how sensitivity and specificity change across different classification thresholds. It plots sensitivity against 1 - specificity.

The area under the ROC curve is called AUC. AUC measures discrimination: the ability of the model to assign higher risk scores to patients who experience the outcome than to those who do not.

  • AUC = 0.5: no discrimination.
  • AUC = 1.0: perfect discrimination.

AUC is useful, but it is not enough. A model can have a good AUC but still be poorly calibrated.

20. Clinical interpretation of metrics

Clinical taskImportant considerations
ScreeningHigh sensitivity may be important.
Confirmatory diagnosisHigh specificity may be important.
Risk predictionDiscrimination and calibration are both important.
Rare disease predictionPPV and sensitivity need careful interpretation.
Treatment decision-makingClinical utility and net benefit matter.
Patient reassuranceNPV may be important, but prevalence matters.

The key point is that statistical metrics must be interpreted in relation to clinical consequences.

21. Common mistakes in supervised medical learning

  • Defining the outcome vaguely.
  • Including predictors that are not available at prediction time.
  • Splitting rows instead of patients when there are repeated records.
  • Evaluating only training performance.
  • Using accuracy alone in imbalanced data.
  • Ignoring calibration.
  • Choosing thresholds without clinical justification.
  • Trying complex models without a simple baseline.
  • Performing preprocessing before train/test splitting.
  • Reporting results without enough detail for reproducibility.

22. Practical workflow for a first supervised learning analysis

  1. Define the clinical prediction question.
  2. Define the prediction time point.
  3. Define the outcome clearly.
  4. Identify candidate predictors available at prediction time.
  5. Explore missingness and data quality.
  6. Split data into training and test sets.
  7. Fit a baseline logistic regression model.
  8. Generate predicted probabilities.
  9. Choose or examine classification thresholds.
  10. Evaluate confusion matrix-based metrics.
  11. Evaluate discrimination using ROC AUC.
  12. Assess calibration.
  13. Interpret results clinically.
  14. Report limitations clearly.

23. Summary

Supervised learning is the foundation of many medical machine learning applications. In supervised learning, the model learns from examples where both predictors and outcomes are known. In biostatistics, this framework is commonly used for clinical prediction tasks such as disease classification, readmission prediction, treatment response prediction, cancer recurrence prediction, and survival prediction.

The most important point is that supervised learning in medicine is not just about fitting a model. It requires careful definition of the clinical question, outcome, prediction time point, and candidate predictors.

For binary classification, logistic regression is often a useful baseline model. Model performance should be interpreted using clinically meaningful metrics such as sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, ROC AUC, and calibration.

R practical: a basic supervised learning workflow

This R script introduces a basic supervised learning workflow using the Pima Indians Diabetes dataset: define the outcome, define predictors, split the data, fit a logistic regression model, generate predicted probabilities, convert probabilities into classes, and evaluate the model using a confusion matrix and AUC.

Outcome

The outcome is diabetes status: negative or positive.

Predictors

Predictors include glucose, BMI, age, insulin, blood pressure and related clinical variables.

Evaluation

The script evaluates predictions on test data using a confusion matrix and ROC AUC.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 03: Supervised Learning Basics
#
# This script introduces a basic supervised learning workflow:
# define the outcome, define predictors, split the data, fit a model,
# and evaluate predictions.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)
library(pROC)

set.seed(123)

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

cat("\nDataset overview:\n")
print(glimpse(diabetes_data))

cat("\nOutcome distribution:\n")
print(diabetes_data %>% count(diabetes))

predictor_names <- c(
  "pregnant",
  "glucose",
  "pressure",
  "triceps",
  "insulin",
  "mass",
  "pedigree",
  "age"
)

outcome_name <- "diabetes"

cat("\nOutcome:\n")
print(outcome_name)

cat("\nPredictors:\n")
print(predictor_names)

n <- nrow(diabetes_data)

train_index <- sample(seq_len(n), size = round(0.80 * n))

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

cat("\nTraining outcome distribution:\n")
print(train_data %>% count(diabetes))

cat("\nTest outcome distribution:\n")
print(test_data %>% count(diabetes))

model_basic <- glm(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  family = binomial
)

cat("\nModel summary:\n")
print(summary(model_basic))

test_data <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_risk = predict(model_basic, newdata = test_data, type = "response")
  )

cat("\nFirst few predicted risks:\n")
print(
  test_data %>%
    select(diabetes, predicted_risk) %>%
    head(10)
)

threshold <- 0.50

test_data <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_class = factor(
      ifelse(predicted_risk >= threshold, "pos", "neg"),
      levels = c("neg", "pos")
    )
  )

cat("\nConfusion matrix at threshold 0.50:\n")
print(table(Predicted = test_data$predicted_class, Actual = test_data$diabetes))

roc_object <- roc(
  response = test_data$diabetes,
  predictor = test_data$predicted_risk,
  levels = c("neg", "pos"),
  quiet = TRUE
)

cat("\nTest AUC:\n")
print(auc(roc_object))

plot(
  roc_object,
  lwd = 2,
  main = paste0("ROC Curve: Supervised Learning Example, AUC = ", round(auc(roc_object), 3))
)

abline(a = 0, b = 1, lty = 2)

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. Supervised learning requires predictors and a known outcome.\n")
cat("2. The model learns from training data.\n")
cat("3. Test data are used to evaluate performance on unseen patients.\n")
cat("4. Predicted probabilities can be converted into classes using a threshold.\n")
cat("5. AUC summarises discrimination across thresholds.\n")

  • NPV: 50 / (50 + 10) = 0.833 or 83.3%.

Exercise 6: Accuracy problem

A disease has prevalence 2%. A model predicts every patient as disease negative.

Click to see answer

Accuracy: 98%, because 98% of patients do not have the disease.

Sensitivity: 0%, because the model detects none of the true disease cases.

The model is clinically useless for screening because it misses every case. More informative metrics include sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, ROC AUC, calibration and decision-related measures.

Exercise 7: Threshold choice

For screening, should we usually prefer a lower threshold with higher sensitivity, or a higher threshold with higher specificity?

Click to see answer

For screening, we often prefer a lower threshold that gives higher sensitivity, because missing true cases can be harmful. This may increase false positives, so the threshold still needs clinical justification. Screening is often followed by more specific confirmatory testing.

Exercise 8: PPV and prevalence

Two populations are screened using the same model. The model has the same sensitivity and specificity in both populations. Population A has disease prevalence 20%. Population B has disease prevalence 2%.

In which population will the positive predictive value usually be higher?

Click to see answer

PPV will usually be higher in Population A, because disease prevalence is higher. When a disease is rare, a larger proportion of positive predictions may be false positives, even when sensitivity and specificity are unchanged.

Exercise 9: AUC interpretation

AUC = 0.82
Click to see answer

An AUC of 0.82 suggests reasonably good discrimination: the model tends to assign higher predicted risk to patients with the outcome than to patients without the outcome.

It does not tell us whether predicted probabilities are well calibrated. Calibration, sensitivity, specificity, predictive values, thresholds and clinical usefulness should also be assessed.

Exercise 10: Logistic regression as baseline

Why is logistic regression often a good first model for binary clinical prediction?

Click to see answer

Logistic regression is often a good first model because it is interpretable, familiar, stable and suitable for many tabular clinical datasets. It produces predicted probabilities, which are important for risk prediction. It is also easy to validate, calibrate and report transparently. More complex models should usually be compared against this baseline rather than assumed to be superior.

Exercise 11: Short answer questions

Why is the prediction time point important?

Click to see answer

The prediction time point defines when the model is supposed to make the prediction. It determines which predictors are legitimately available and helps avoid leakage from future information.

Why should predictors be available at prediction time?

Click to see answer

A model cannot be used in real practice if it requires information that is not available when the prediction is needed. Using such variables can also make performance look falsely strong.

Why is accuracy not enough in medical classification?

Click to see answer

Accuracy can be misleading when outcomes are imbalanced. A model may achieve high accuracy by predicting the majority class while missing clinically important cases.

Why can PPV be low for rare diseases?

Click to see answer

When disease prevalence is low, even a small false positive rate can produce many false positives relative to true positives. Therefore, PPV can be low even if sensitivity and specificity look reasonable.

Why should thresholds be chosen based on clinical context?

Click to see answer

Thresholds control the trade-off between false positives and false negatives. The best threshold depends on the purpose of the model, disease severity, treatment harms, resource limits and patient safety.

Exercise 12: Mini case study

A team develops a model to predict 30-day readmission. The model is intended to be used at hospital discharge.

Variables:

  • Age
  • Sex
  • Admission diagnosis
  • Comorbidities
  • Length of stay
  • Discharge medications
  • Number of previous admissions
  • Discharge destination
  • Readmission status
Click to see answer

Appropriate predictors include age, sex, admission diagnosis, comorbidities, length of stay, discharge medications, number of previous admissions, and discharge destination, assuming the prediction is made at discharge.

Readmission status should not be used as a predictor because it is the outcome.

Length of stay is appropriate if the prediction is made at discharge because it is known at that time. If the prediction time point was hospital admission, length of stay, discharge medications, and discharge destination would not be available and would become leakage-risk variables.

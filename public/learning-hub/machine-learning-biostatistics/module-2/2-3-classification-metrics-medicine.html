Today’s big question

Why is accuracy not enough in medical prediction?

Accuracy is simple:

Accuracy = proportion of all patients correctly classified

But in medicine, different errors have different consequences.

Missing a true disease case is not the same as sending a healthy person for extra testing.

That is why medical classification models need more than accuracy.

Core metrics

Accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, PPV and NPV

Accuracy

Overall correctness

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / all patients

Accuracy can be useful, but it can be misleading when the outcome is imbalanced.

Sensitivity

Detecting true positives

Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN)

High sensitivity is important when missing disease is harmful.

Specificity

Identifying true negatives

Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)

High specificity is important when false positives cause harm, anxiety, or unnecessary cost.

Predictive values

Trustworthiness of predictions

PPV = TP / (TP + FP) NPV = TN / (TN + FN)

Accuracy trap

If only 5% of patients have a disease, a model could predict everyone as disease-negative and still achieve 95% accuracy. That model would be clinically poor for detecting disease.

ROC AUC and calibration

Ranking is not the same as risk accuracy

The ROC curve shows how sensitivity and specificity change across thresholds.

AUC measures discrimination.

Discrimination asks: Does the model assign higher predicted risk to patients with the outcome than to patients without the outcome?

A higher AUC means better ranking ability.

But AUC tells us about ranking, not whether predicted probabilities are accurate.

“AUC tells us whether the model ranks patients well. But I care about whether the predicted risks are believable.”

Concept Question
Discrimination Can the model rank high-risk patients above low-risk patients?
Calibration Are the predicted probabilities close to observed risks?

A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration. For clinical decision-making, both matter.

Threshold choice

A threshold converts predicted probabilities into predicted classes

Predicted risk ≥ 0.50 → positive Predicted risk < 0.50 → negative

But threshold choice should depend on clinical context.

For screening, we may choose a lower threshold to increase sensitivity.

For confirmatory decisions, we may choose a higher threshold to increase specificity.

Clinical reality check

In medicine, model errors have consequences.

  • False negative: missed diagnosis, delayed treatment, unsafe reassurance.
  • False positive: unnecessary testing, anxiety, cost, overtreatment.
  • Poor calibration: misleading risk communication and poor shared decision-making.

Detailed notes

Note 5: Classification Metrics in Medicine

Classification metrics in medicine are not just mathematical calculations. They are tools for understanding clinical trade-offs. A good medical prediction model should be assessed using a combination of discrimination, calibration, threshold-based performance and clinical usefulness.

1. Introduction

After building a classification model, the next question is not simply whether the model ran successfully. The more important question is whether the model is useful, reliable and clinically meaningful.

In medical machine learning, classification models are often used to predict binary outcomes such as disease status, hospital readmission, treatment response, recurrence, or mortality within a fixed time period.

A model with high accuracy may still be clinically useless. A model with high AUC may still give poorly calibrated probabilities. A model with high sensitivity may produce many false positives. A model with high specificity may miss many true cases.

2. Binary classification in medical prediction

In binary classification, the outcome has two categories.

Medical problemPositive classNegative class
Diabetes predictionDiabetesNo diabetes
Cancer diagnosisMalignantBenign
Hospital readmissionReadmittedNot readmitted
Treatment responseResponderNon-responder
5-year recurrenceRecurrenceNo recurrence
Exposure prediction from methylationExposedUnexposed

The positive class does not always mean something good. In medical prediction, the positive class usually means the event or condition of interest. Before calculating metrics, it is essential to define the positive class clearly.

3. Predicted probabilities and predicted classes

Many classification models produce predicted probabilities, which can be converted into predicted classes using a threshold.

PatientPredicted probabilityPredicted class at threshold 0.50
A0.82Positive
B0.61Positive
C0.44Negative
D0.12Negative

A threshold of 0.50 is not automatically appropriate in medicine. For some clinical problems, a lower threshold may be preferred because missing a true case is dangerous. For other problems, a higher threshold may be preferred because false positives may lead to unnecessary invasive testing or harmful treatment.

4. The confusion matrix

Most threshold-based classification metrics are derived from the confusion matrix.

Predicted positivePredicted negative
Actual positiveTrue positiveFalse negative
Actual negativeFalse positiveTrue negative
  • True positive: the model predicts positive and the patient truly has the outcome.
  • False positive: the model predicts positive but the patient does not have the outcome.
  • True negative: the model predicts negative and the patient truly does not have the outcome.
  • False negative: the model predicts negative but the patient truly has the outcome.

5. Accuracy and why it can mislead

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + FP + TN + FN)

Accuracy answers: out of all patients, how many did the model classify correctly? Accuracy is simple and easy to understand, but it can be misleading in medical datasets, especially when the outcome is rare.

Suppose a disease has prevalence 2%. A model that predicts “no disease” for every patient will have 98% accuracy, but sensitivity of 0%. This model is clinically useless for screening because it fails to identify every patient with disease.

6. Sensitivity and specificity

Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN) Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)

Sensitivity answers: among patients who truly have the outcome, how many did the model detect? Specificity answers: among patients who truly do not have the outcome, how many did the model correctly classify as negative?

Sensitivity is especially important when missing a case is harmful. Specificity is especially important when false positives are harmful.

7. Sensitivity-specificity trade-off

If we lower the classification threshold, more patients are classified as positive. This usually increases sensitivity and decreases specificity.

If we raise the classification threshold, fewer patients are classified as positive. This usually decreases sensitivity and increases specificity.

ThresholdSensitivitySpecificityTypical use
0.300.900.60Screening
0.700.550.92Confirmatory decision

8. PPV and NPV

PPV = TP / (TP + FP) NPV = TN / (TN + FN)

PPV answers: among patients predicted positive, how many truly have the outcome? NPV answers: among patients predicted negative, how many truly do not have the outcome?

PPV and NPV depend strongly on disease prevalence. If disease prevalence is high, PPV tends to be higher. If disease prevalence is low, PPV tends to be lower.

9. Worked example

Suppose a model predicts disease status in 200 patients.

True positives = 40 False positives = 20 True negatives = 120 False negatives = 20
  • Accuracy: (40 + 120) / 200 = 0.80.
  • Sensitivity: 40 / (40 + 20) = 0.667.
  • Specificity: 120 / (120 + 20) = 0.857.
  • PPV: 40 / (40 + 20) = 0.667.
  • NPV: 120 / (120 + 20) = 0.857.

This model is better at identifying negative cases than positive cases. Whether this is acceptable depends on the clinical task.

10. ROC curve and AUC

The ROC curve shows how sensitivity and specificity change across all possible classification thresholds. It plots sensitivity against 1 - specificity.

AUC is a measure of discrimination. It asks whether the model can assign higher predicted risks to patients who experience the outcome than to patients who do not.

  • AUC = 0.5: no discrimination.
  • AUC = 1.0: perfect discrimination.

AUC is useful, but it does not measure calibration, it does not tell us which threshold should be used, and it may not reflect clinical usefulness.

11. Precision-recall curve

The precision-recall curve is another way to evaluate classification models, especially when the positive outcome is rare. Precision is PPV and recall is sensitivity.

This can be more informative than the ROC curve when the outcome is rare because it focuses more directly on the performance among predicted positives.

12. Discrimination versus calibration

Discrimination

Ranking ability

Can the model separate patients who experience the outcome from those who do not?

Calibration

Probability accuracy

Do predicted probabilities agree with observed risks?

A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration. In medicine, calibration is essential because clinical decisions often depend on absolute risk.

13. Brier score

The Brier score is a measure of prediction error for probabilistic predictions.

Brier score = mean((predicted probability - observed outcome)^2)

The observed outcome is coded as 1 if the event occurred and 0 if the event did not occur. A lower Brier score is better.

14. Breast cancer survival example

Sometimes a survival question is converted into a binary classification question, such as recurrence within 5 years: yes/no. Then classification metrics may be used.

However, if follow-up times differ and censoring is present, ordinary binary classification metrics may be inappropriate or incomplete. Survival-specific metrics such as the C-index, time-dependent AUC and time-dependent Brier score may be more appropriate.

The evaluation metric should match the outcome structure.

15. Methylation and EWAS-style prediction

In methylation-based classification, the number of predictors may be very large.

n = 800 participants p = 450,000 CpG sites

This creates a serious risk of overfitting. Feature selection can cause leakage if it is done before the train/test split or before cross-validation. Accuracy may also be misleading if the outcome is imbalanced.

Methylation-based classification should report more than accuracy: sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, ROC AUC, calibration where probabilities are used, and ideally external validation in an independent cohort.

16. Balanced accuracy and F1 score

Balanced accuracy = (Sensitivity + Specificity) / 2 F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

Balanced accuracy can be useful when outcome classes are imbalanced because it gives equal weight to positive and negative classes. F1 score combines precision and recall, but it ignores true negatives, so it should not be the only metric in medical evaluation.

17. Choosing metrics according to clinical purpose

Clinical purposeMetrics that may matter
ScreeningSensitivity, NPV, PPV, burden of false positives
DiagnosisSensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV
Risk predictionAUC, calibration, Brier score, decision curve analysis
Treatment decisionCalibration, net benefit, threshold-specific performance
Rare disease predictionPPV, sensitivity, precision-recall curve
Patient reassuranceNPV, calibration, safety of false negatives

18. Common mistakes

  • Reporting accuracy alone.
  • Ignoring class imbalance.
  • Reporting AUC without calibration.
  • Not defining the positive class.
  • Choosing a threshold without explanation.
  • Reporting PPV and NPV without discussing prevalence.
  • Using test data repeatedly to choose the best threshold.
  • Applying ordinary binary metrics to censored survival outcomes.

19. Practical interpretation framework

  • What is the clinical question?
  • What is the positive class?
  • What is the prediction time point?
  • What is the outcome prevalence?
  • Is the dataset imbalanced?
  • What threshold was used?
  • Is sensitivity high enough for the intended use?
  • Is specificity high enough for the intended use?
  • Is PPV acceptable?
  • Is NPV acceptable?
  • Does the model discriminate well?
  • Is the model calibrated?
  • Was the model validated properly?
  • Could performance be inflated by leakage?

20. Summary

Classification metrics are central to evaluating medical machine learning models, but they must be interpreted carefully. Accuracy is easy to understand but can be misleading when outcomes are imbalanced. Sensitivity and specificity describe how well the model identifies true positives and true negatives. PPV and NPV describe how trustworthy positive and negative predictions are, but they depend strongly on prevalence.

ROC AUC measures discrimination, but it does not measure calibration or clinical usefulness. Threshold choice should depend on clinical context and the consequences of false positives and false negatives.

In medical machine learning, model performance should be judged by a combination of metrics that reflect the clinical purpose, outcome structure, prevalence, validation design, calibration and consequences of decision-making.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 05: Classification Metrics in Medicine
#
# This script shows why accuracy is not enough.
# We calculate sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, accuracy, and AUC.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)
library(pROC)

set.seed(123)

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

train_index <- sample(seq_len(nrow(diabetes_data)), size = round(0.80 * nrow(diabetes_data)))

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

model <- glm(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  family = binomial
)

test_data <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_risk = predict(model, newdata = test_data, type = "response")
  )

calculate_classification_metrics <- function(actual, risk, threshold) {
  predicted <- factor(
    ifelse(risk >= threshold, "pos", "neg"),
    levels = c("neg", "pos")
  )

  tab <- table(Predicted = predicted, Actual = actual)

  tn <- tab["neg", "neg"]
  fp <- tab["pos", "neg"]
  fn <- tab["neg", "pos"]
  tp <- tab["pos", "pos"]

  accuracy <- (tp + tn) / sum(tab)
  sensitivity <- tp / (tp + fn)
  specificity <- tn / (tn + fp)
  ppv <- tp / (tp + fp)
  npv <- tn / (tn + fn)

  tibble(
    threshold = threshold,
    accuracy = accuracy,
    sensitivity = sensitivity,
    specificity = specificity,
    ppv = ppv,
    npv = npv,
    true_positive = tp,
    false_positive = fp,
    false_negative = fn,
    true_negative = tn
  )
}

metrics_050 <- calculate_classification_metrics(
  actual = test_data$diabetes,
  risk = test_data$predicted_risk,
  threshold = 0.50
)

cat("\nClassification metrics at threshold 0.50:\n")
print(metrics_050)

threshold_grid <- seq(0.20, 0.80, by = 0.10)

threshold_results <- map_dfr(
  threshold_grid,
  ~ calculate_classification_metrics(
    actual = test_data$diabetes,
    risk = test_data$predicted_risk,
    threshold = .x
  )
)

cat("\nMetrics across different thresholds:\n")
print(threshold_results)

roc_object <- roc(
  response = test_data$diabetes,
  predictor = test_data$predicted_risk,
  levels = c("neg", "pos"),
  quiet = TRUE
)

cat("\nAUC:\n")
print(auc(roc_object))

plot(
  roc_object,
  lwd = 2,
  main = paste0("ROC Curve: Classification Metrics, AUC = ", round(auc(roc_object), 3))
)

abline(a = 0, b = 1, lty = 2)

plot(
  threshold_results$threshold,
  threshold_results$sensitivity,
  type = "b",
  ylim = c(0, 1),
  xlab = "Threshold",
  ylab = "Metric value",
  main = "Sensitivity and Specificity Across Thresholds"
)

lines(
  threshold_results$threshold,
  threshold_results$specificity,
  type = "b",
  lty = 2
)

legend(
  "right",
  legend = c("Sensitivity", "Specificity"),
  lty = c(1, 2),
  pch = 1,
  bty = "n"
)

cat("\nClinical interpretation:\n")
cat("1. Lower thresholds usually increase sensitivity.\n")
cat("2. Higher thresholds usually increase specificity.\n")
cat("3. Screening often prioritises sensitivity.\n")
cat("4. Confirmatory decisions may require higher specificity.\n")
cat("5. Accuracy alone can hide clinically important errors.\n")

Exercise 13: Short answer questions

Why is accuracy not enough in medical prediction?

Click to see answer

Accuracy can hide clinically important errors, especially when outcomes are rare. A model may be accurate overall but miss most true cases. Medical evaluation requires sensitivity, specificity, predictive values, calibration and clinical interpretation.

Why does PPV depend on prevalence?

Click to see answer

When the outcome is rare, true positives are uncommon, so many positive predictions may be false positives. As prevalence increases, a positive prediction is more likely to be a true positive.

Why can AUC be misleading if reported alone?

Click to see answer

AUC measures ranking ability but not calibration, threshold performance or clinical usefulness. A model can have high AUC but give risk probabilities that are too high or too low.

What is the difference between discrimination and calibration?

Click to see answer

Discrimination asks whether the model ranks higher-risk patients above lower-risk patients. Calibration asks whether predicted probabilities agree with observed risks.

Why should threshold choice depend on clinical context?

Click to see answer

Thresholds change the balance between false positives and false negatives. The best threshold depends on the harms, costs and benefits of action in the intended clinical setting.

Why is external validation important?

Click to see answer

External validation tests whether model performance generalises to a new setting, population or time period. This matters because prevalence, measurement and clinical workflows can differ across settings.

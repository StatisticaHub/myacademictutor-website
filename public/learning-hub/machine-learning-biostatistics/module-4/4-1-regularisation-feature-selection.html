Lesson 10: Regularisation and Feature Selection

Biomedical datasets often contain many predictors and relatively few patients. This lesson explains how regularisation can reduce overfitting, how ridge, lasso and elastic net differ, and why selected features must be interpreted carefully.

Today’s big question

How can we build prediction models when there are many predictors?

In biomedical data, we often have many possible predictors.

  • clinical variables;
  • lab measurements;
  • imaging features;
  • genetic markers;
  • methylation sites;
  • gene expression features.

When the number of predictors is large, overfitting becomes a major risk.

Intuition first

Too many predictors can make noise look meaningful

Imagine trying to explain a small group of patients using hundreds of variables.

The model may find patterns that are not real.

It may learn noise instead of signal.

Regularisation reduces overfitting by discouraging overly complex models.

What is regularisation?

A penalty that discourages overly complex models

Regularisation adds a penalty to the model-fitting process. Instead of only trying to fit the data as closely as possible, the model is also encouraged to keep coefficients smaller or simpler.

Fit the data well + keep the model controlled

This can improve performance on new data.

Three important methods

Ridge, lasso and elastic net

Shrinks coefficients

Ridge shrinks coefficients towards zero. It usually keeps all predictors in the model but reduces their influence.

Lasso regression

Can select predictors

Elastic net

Combines ridge and lasso

Elastic net can shrink coefficients and select variables. It is often useful when predictors are correlated.

Feature selection versus clinical reasoning

Statistical selection should be checked against context

Feature selection can be statistical, clinical, or both.

A good modelling strategy should consider:

  • clinical relevance;
  • predictor availability;
  • measurement quality;
  • missingness;
  • correlation between predictors;
  • sample size;
  • validation performance.

Common mistake: selecting features before validation

If feature selection is performed before cross-validation or train/test splitting, the validation process becomes biased. Feature selection must be included inside the resampling or validation workflow.

Medical example

Methylation prediction

Suppose we use methylation data to predict an exposure or disease outcome.

300 participants 450,000 CpG sites

A standard logistic regression model cannot handle all of them directly with a small sample size.

Regularisation methods such as lasso, ridge, or elastic net can help build more stable prediction models.

Clinical reality check

A selected predictor is not automatically clinically important.

Before interpreting selected features strongly, consider:

  • biological plausibility;
  • stability across resamples;
  • external validation;
  • measurement reliability;
  • whether the predictor is available in practice.

Detailed notes

Note 10: Regularisation and Feature Selection in Biomedical Prediction

Regularisation can reduce overfitting in high-dimensional prediction, but feature selection must be validated and interpreted cautiously.

1. Why this note matters

Medical and biomedical datasets often contain many possible predictors, including clinical history, laboratory results, imaging features, genetic variants, gene expression, DNA methylation sites, proteomics markers and electronic health record variables.

When the number of predictors is large compared with the number of patients or outcome events, ordinary modelling can become unstable. The model may fit random noise instead of real signal.

2. The problem of too many predictors

Suppose we have 150 patients and 500 candidate predictors.

A model may find apparent patterns that look strong in the development data, but many of these patterns may be accidental. This is especially important when the outcome is rare.

150 patients 30 outcome events 500 predictors

There are many more possible predictor patterns than outcome events. Without careful modelling, overfitting is likely.

3. High-dimensional biomedical data

Biomedical datasets can be extremely high-dimensional.

  • Gene expression: thousands of genes.
  • DNA methylation: hundreds of thousands of CpG sites.
  • Genetics: thousands or millions of variants.
  • Imaging: many extracted features.
  • Electronic health records: many coded variables.

4. What is regularisation?

Regularisation adds a penalty to the model-fitting process. Instead of only fitting the data as closely as possible, the model is encouraged to be simpler or more stable. The penalty discourages overly large coefficients.

Regularisation is useful when:

  • there are many predictors;
  • predictors are correlated;
  • sample size is limited;
  • overfitting risk is high;
  • prediction stability is important.

5. Ridge regression

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients towards zero. It usually keeps all predictors in the model, but reduces their influence. Ridge is useful when many predictors may each contain a small amount of information and when predictors are correlated.

6. Lasso regression

Lasso regression also shrinks coefficients. However, lasso can shrink some coefficients exactly to zero. This means lasso can perform feature selection.

This can make the model simpler, but selected predictors should not automatically be treated as clinically important or causal. Selection can be unstable, especially when predictors are correlated.

7. Elastic net

Elastic net combines ridge and lasso penalties. It can shrink coefficients and perform variable selection. Elastic net is often useful when predictors are correlated, which is common in omics, imaging and electronic health record data.

LassoShrinks some coefficients to zeroYes

9. Feature selection

Feature selection means choosing a subset of predictors. It may be based on clinical reasoning, previous literature, statistical association, prediction performance, regularisation, measurement quality or availability in practice.

In medical prediction, feature selection should not be purely mechanical. A predictor should also make sense in the intended clinical workflow.

10. Feature selection and data leakage

An incorrect workflow is:

1. Use the full dataset to select predictors.
2. Split data into training and test sets.
3. Fit the model using selected predictors.
4. Evaluate performance on the test set.

This is wrong because the test set influenced predictor selection. The test set is no longer independent, so performance may be too optimistic.

A safer workflow is:

1. Split data into training and test sets.
2. Select features using only the training set.
3. Fit the model using selected features.
4. Evaluate once on the untouched test set.

For cross-validation, feature selection must happen inside each training fold.

11. Prediction versus causation

A selected feature is not automatically causal. A selected feature may be:

  • biologically relevant;
  • correlated with a causal factor;
  • a proxy for another variable;
  • selected due to noise;
  • affected by measurement artefacts;
  • specific to one dataset.

Prediction and causal explanation are different goals.

12. Medical examples

In methylation prediction, a dataset may contain 300 participants and 450,000 CpG sites. Standard logistic regression cannot handle all predictors directly, but ridge, lasso and elastic net may help. Selected CpG sites should be interpreted cautiously as predictive markers first, not causal effects.

In breast cancer recurrence prediction, regularisation may improve stability when many biomarkers or gene expression features are available but recurrence events are limited. The final model still needs internal validation, calibration assessment, external validation and clinical usefulness evaluation.

13. Choosing the penalty strength

Regularised models require a tuning parameter that controls the amount of shrinkage. If the penalty is too weak, the model may overfit. If the penalty is too strong, the model may underfit. Cross-validation is commonly used to choose the penalty strength.

14. Stability of selected features

Selected features may change across samples. One bootstrap sample may select one biomarker, while another sample selects a different but correlated biomarker. This does not necessarily mean the method is wrong; it means feature selection can be unstable.

15. Interpreting selected features

Before claiming that a selected feature is important, ask:

  • Is it biologically plausible?
  • Is it selected consistently across resamples?
  • Does it improve validated prediction performance?
  • Is it measured reliably?
  • Is it available in clinical practice?
  • Does it validate externally?
  • Could it be a proxy for another variable?

16. Regularisation and calibration

Regularisation can reduce overfitting, which may improve calibration. However, regularised models can still be miscalibrated. After fitting a regularised model, we should still assess AUC, calibration plot, calibration slope, calibration intercept, Brier score and clinical usefulness.

17. Common mistakes

  • Too many predictors for too few events.
  • Feature selection before validation.
  • Treating selected predictors as causal.
  • Ignoring correlated predictors.
  • Reporting selected features without validation.
  • Assuming regularisation solves everything.

18. Reporting regularised models

A clear report should describe:

  • candidate predictors;
  • sample size;
  • number of outcome events;
  • missing data handling;
  • regularisation method;
  • tuning process;
  • validation method;
  • selected predictors, if applicable;
  • discrimination;
  • calibration;
  • limitations;
  • clinical interpretation.

19. Summary

Regularisation is useful when there are many predictors and limited data. It reduces overfitting by shrinking coefficients and controlling model complexity.

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients but usually keeps all predictors. Lasso can shrink some coefficients to zero and perform feature selection. Elastic net combines ridge and lasso and is often useful when predictors are correlated.

Selected features should be interpreted as prediction features first, not automatically as causal or clinically proven factors.

R practical: comparing ordinary, ridge, lasso and elastic net models

This script compares ordinary logistic regression, ridge regression, lasso regression and elastic net for diabetes prediction. It also prints selected features from lasso and elastic net.

Lasso

Can shrink some coefficients to zero and select features.

Elastic net

Combines ridge and lasso behaviour.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 10: Regularisation and Feature Selection
#
# This script compares ordinary logistic regression, ridge regression,
# lasso regression, and elastic net for diabetes prediction.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)
library(pROC)
library(glmnet)

set.seed(123)

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

train_index <- sample(seq_len(nrow(diabetes_data)), size = round(0.80 * nrow(diabetes_data)))

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

x_train <- model.matrix(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data
)[, -1]

x_test <- model.matrix(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = test_data
)[, -1]

y_train <- ifelse(train_data$diabetes == "pos", 1, 0)
y_test_factor <- test_data$diabetes

ordinary_model <- glm(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  family = binomial
)

ordinary_risk <- predict(
  ordinary_model,
  newdata = test_data,
  type = "response"
)

ridge_model <- cv.glmnet(
  x = x_train,
  y = y_train,
  family = "binomial",
  alpha = 0
)

ridge_risk <- as.numeric(
  predict(
    ridge_model,
    newx = x_test,
    s = "lambda.min",
    type = "response"
  )
)

lasso_model <- cv.glmnet(
  x = x_train,
  y = y_train,
  family = "binomial",
  alpha = 1
)

lasso_risk <- as.numeric(
  predict(
    lasso_model,
    newx = x_test,
    s = "lambda.min",
    type = "response"
  )
)

elastic_net_model <- cv.glmnet(
  x = x_train,
  y = y_train,
  family = "binomial",
  alpha = 0.5
)

elastic_net_risk <- as.numeric(
  predict(
    elastic_net_model,
    newx = x_test,
    s = "lambda.min",
    type = "response"
  )
)

calculate_auc <- function(actual, risk) {
  roc_object <- roc(
    response = actual,
    predictor = risk,
    levels = c("neg", "pos"),
    quiet = TRUE
  )

  as.numeric(auc(roc_object))
}

model_comparison <- tibble(
  model = c(
    "Ordinary logistic regression",
    "Ridge regression",
    "Lasso regression",
    "Elastic net"
  ),
  auc = c(
    calculate_auc(y_test_factor, ordinary_risk),
    calculate_auc(y_test_factor, ridge_risk),
    calculate_auc(y_test_factor, lasso_risk),
    calculate_auc(y_test_factor, elastic_net_risk)
  )
)

cat("\nModel comparison using test AUC:\n")
print(model_comparison)

lasso_coefficients <- coef(lasso_model, s = "lambda.min")

lasso_selected <- tibble(
  term = rownames(lasso_coefficients),
  coefficient = as.numeric(lasso_coefficients)
) %>%
  filter(coefficient != 0)

cat("\nFeatures selected by lasso:\n")
print(lasso_selected)

elastic_net_coefficients <- coef(elastic_net_model, s = "lambda.min")

elastic_net_selected <- tibble(
  term = rownames(elastic_net_coefficients),
  coefficient = as.numeric(elastic_net_coefficients)
) %>%
  filter(coefficient != 0)

cat("\nFeatures selected by elastic net:\n")
print(elastic_net_selected)

plot(ridge_model, main = "Ridge Cross-Validation")
plot(lasso_model, main = "Lasso Cross-Validation")
plot(elastic_net_model, main = "Elastic Net Cross-Validation")

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. Ridge shrinks coefficients but usually keeps predictors.\n")
cat("2. Lasso can shrink some coefficients to zero.\n")
cat("3. Elastic net combines ridge and lasso behaviour.\n")
cat("4. Selected features are prediction features, not automatically causal factors.\n")
cat("5. Feature selection must be included inside validation to avoid leakage.\n")

Exercises

Exercise 10: Regularisation and Feature Selection

This exercise helps you understand why regularisation is useful when there are many predictors and why feature selection must be interpreted carefully.

Question 1: Concept check

Why does overfitting become more likely when there are many predictors and few patients?

Show suggested answer

With many predictors and few patients, the model has many ways to fit random noise. It may find accidental patterns that look predictive in the development data but fail in new patients.

Question 2: Ridge regression

What does ridge regression do to model coefficients?

Show suggested answer

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients towards zero, usually keeping all predictors in the model but reducing their influence.

Question 3: Lasso regression

How is lasso regression different from ridge regression?

Show suggested answer

Lasso can shrink some coefficients exactly to zero, so it can perform feature selection. Ridge usually keeps all predictors.

Question 4: Elastic net

Why might elastic net be useful for omics data?

Show suggested answer

Omics predictors are often numerous and correlated. Elastic net combines ridge and lasso, so it can select predictors while being more stable than lasso alone when predictors are correlated.

Question 5: Interpretation

Why should selected features from a lasso model not automatically be interpreted as causal factors?

Show suggested answer

Lasso selection is based on prediction in a dataset, not causal identification. A selected feature may be a proxy, correlated with another variable, selected due to noise, or unstable across samples.

Question 6: Validation

Why is it problematic to perform feature selection before train/test splitting?

Show suggested answer

The test set influences feature selection, so it is no longer independent. This creates feature-selection leakage and can make test performance too optimistic.

Question 7: Clinical reasoning

A lasso model selects a biomarker for cancer recurrence prediction. What should you check before claiming that this biomarker is clinically important?

Show suggested answer

Check biological plausibility, stability across resamples, validated prediction performance, measurement reliability, external validation, clinical availability, and whether it could be a proxy for another factor.

Question 8: Many predictors

100 patients 20,000 gene expression features

Why would ordinary logistic regression be problematic?

Show suggested answer

There are far more predictors than patients, so ordinary logistic regression would be unstable or impossible to fit reliably. It would have a very high risk of overfitting.

Question 9: Prediction versus explanation

A regularised model selects methylation sites that predict exposure status. Does this prove those sites are caused by the exposure?

Show suggested answer

No. Prediction does not prove causation. The selected sites may be markers, proxies, confounded associations, or sample-specific features. Causal analysis requires a different design and assumptions.

Reflection

In a biomedical prediction model, how would you balance statistical feature selection with clinical or biological reasoning?

Show reflection answer

I would use statistical feature selection as one source of evidence, but not as final proof. I would check biological plausibility, measurement quality, predictor availability, feature stability, validation performance, calibration, external validation, and whether selected features make sense in the intended clinical or biomedical setting.

Big idea

High-dimensional data need constrained models

High-dimensional data often contain far more predictors than patients. This is common in biomedical research. Penalised regression methods such as ridge, lasso and elastic net help build prediction models when ordinary regression would be unstable or impossible.

Penalised regression makes high-dimensional prediction possible, but high internal performance is not credible unless validation and leakage prevention are handled carefully.

Why ordinary regression can fail

Too many predictors create too many opportunities to overfit

Ordinary regression may fail in high-dimensional data because of:

  • unstable coefficients;
  • overfitting;
  • perfect or near-perfect separation;
  • inability to estimate all parameters;
  • excellent training performance but poor validation performance.

Clinical meaning

A high-dimensional model may look excellent in the development data but fail when applied to new patients, a new hospital, a new assay platform or a different laboratory workflow.

Penalised regression methods

Ridge, lasso and elastic net

Penalised regression adds a penalty to the model-fitting process. The model is asked to predict the outcome well while avoiding excessive model complexity.

Ridge

Shrinks all coefficients

Useful when many correlated predictors may each contribute small amounts of information.

Lasso

Can select predictors

Useful when a sparse selected feature set is desired, but selected features may be unstable.

Elastic net

Combines ridge and lasso

Useful when predictors are numerous and correlated, as in many omics settings.

Tuning parameters

Lambda and alpha must be chosen honestly

Lambda

Penalty strength

Low penalty means a more complex model. High penalty means a simpler model.

Alpha

Ridge-lasso balance

Used in elastic net to control whether the model behaves more like ridge or lasso.

Tuning must be performed using training data or inner validation. The final test set should remain untouched.

Nested validation

Separate model selection from performance estimation

Leakage and batch effects

High-dimensional models are very vulnerable

Preprocessing leakage

Performing PCA, normalisation, filtering, scaling, imputation or batch correction using the full dataset before validation allows validation data to influence model development.

Correct principle

Learn preprocessing steps inside each training fold, then apply the learned transformation to the validation fold.

Batch effects are technical differences unrelated to biology, such as sequencing batch, methylation plate, processing date, scanner, technician or hospital site.

Common mistake

Do not trust a high-dimensional model simply because it has a high internal AUC. High-dimensional models are very vulnerable to leakage, overfitting and batch effects.

Calibration and interpretation

AUC alone is not enough

High-dimensional models should not be evaluated only by AUC. A model may rank patients well but produce risk estimates that are too high or too low.

Assessment should include:

  • calibration plot;
  • calibration slope;
  • calibration intercept;
  • Brier score;
  • clinical usefulness where relevant;
  • external validation if possible.

Detailed notes

Note 15: Penalised Regression for High-Dimensional Data

Penalised regression makes high-dimensional prediction possible, but trustworthy results require careful validation, leakage prevention and cautious interpretation.

Learning objectives

  • Explain what high-dimensional data means.
  • Describe why ordinary regression can fail when the number of predictors is much larger than the number of observations.
  • Explain how penalised regression helps control overfitting.
  • Compare ridge, lasso, and elastic net in high-dimensional settings.
  • Understand the role of tuning parameters.
  • Explain why nested validation may be needed.
  • Recognise the risks of batch effects and preprocessing leakage.
  • Interpret high-dimensional prediction models cautiously.

1. Why this note matters

High-dimensional biomedical data are now common in medical research. Examples include gene expression, DNA methylation, proteomics, metabolomics, microbiome data, imaging radiomics and large electronic health record feature sets.

These datasets may contain thousands or even hundreds of thousands of predictors, while the number of patients may be much smaller. This creates a major risk of overfitting.

2. What high-dimensional means

High-dimensional data means the number of predictors is large. Often, the number of predictors is much larger than the number of observations. This is commonly written as p >> n.

3. Why high-dimensional data are difficult

When there are many predictors, some will appear related to the outcome by chance. The model may find patterns that are not real, especially when sample size is small, events are limited, predictors are noisy, predictors are correlated or batch effects are present.

4. Why ordinary regression can fail

Ordinary regression may fail when there are too many predictors. Problems include unstable coefficients, overfitting, perfect or near-perfect separation, inability to estimate all parameters, and excellent training performance but poor validation performance.

5. What penalised regression does

Penalised regression adds a penalty to the model-fitting process. The model is asked to predict the outcome well and avoid excessive model complexity. This helps control overfitting.

6. Ridge, lasso and elastic net

MethodMain behaviourUseful when
RidgeShrinks all coefficientsMany correlated predictors may contribute.
LassoShrinks some coefficients to zeroA sparse predictor set is desired.
Elastic netCombines ridge and lassoPredictors are numerous and correlated.

There is no universally best method. The best method depends on the prediction task, data structure and validation results.

7. Tuning parameters

Penalised regression methods require tuning. Lambda controls penalty strength, while alpha controls ridge-lasso mixing in elastic net. Tuning should be done using training data or inner cross-validation.

8. Why nested validation may be needed

High-dimensional workflows often involve many choices: feature filtering, preprocessing, penalty type, lambda value, alpha value, model comparison and threshold choice. Nested validation separates model selection from performance estimation.

Inner loop: tune model and select features
Outer loop: estimate model performance

9. Preprocessing leakage

High-dimensional data usually require preprocessing, such as normalisation, transformation, filtering, batch correction, PCA, feature scaling and missing data handling. If preprocessing is learned from the full dataset before validation, leakage can occur.

For example, performing PCA using all samples before cross-validation is biased because validation folds influence the PCA components.

10. Batch effects

Batch effects are technical differences unrelated to biology, such as sequencing batch, methylation array plate, laboratory technician, processing date, scanner type or hospital site.

If batch is associated with outcome, the model may learn batch differences instead of disease biology, producing excellent internal performance but poor external performance.

11. External validation

External validation is especially important in high-dimensional prediction. A model may perform well in one dataset because of cohort-specific patterns, platform-specific measurements, batch effects, local preprocessing choices or population differences.

12. Calibration in high-dimensional models

High-dimensional models should not be evaluated only using AUC. A model may rank patients well but produce risk estimates that are too high or too low. For clinical prediction, calibration matters because predicted probabilities may guide decisions.

13. Interpretation of selected features

Selected features from high-dimensional penalised regression should be interpreted cautiously. They may represent true biological signal, correlated proxies, technical artefacts, batch effects, cohort-specific patterns or random noise.

A selected feature should be considered a candidate for further study, not definitive proof.

14. Practical checklist

  • What is the exact prediction question?
  • What is the prediction time point?
  • How many patients are available?
  • How many outcome events are available?
  • How many candidate predictors are available?
  • Was preprocessing done inside validation?
  • Was feature selection done inside validation?
  • Was tuning performed without touching the test set?
  • Were batch effects assessed?
  • Was calibration assessed?
  • Was external validation performed?
  • Are selected features stable?
  • Are biological claims appropriately cautious?

15. Common mistakes

  • Using ordinary regression with too many predictors.
  • Selecting features before validation.
  • Preprocessing using the full dataset.
  • Tuning on the test set.
  • Ignoring batch effects.
  • Reporting only AUC.
  • Interpreting selected features as causal.
  • Claiming clinical usefulness without external validation.

16. Summary

High-dimensional biomedical prediction is challenging because the number of predictors may be far larger than the number of patients. Penalised regression methods help by shrinking coefficients and controlling model complexity.

Ridge shrinks all coefficients. Lasso can select predictors. Elastic net combines ridge and lasso. These methods do not remove the need for careful validation, leakage prevention, calibration assessment and cautious interpretation.

R practical: simulated high-dimensional prediction

This script simulates a high-dimensional biomedical prediction problem and compares ridge, lasso and elastic net. It also counts selected features and prints selected features from lasso and elastic net.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 15: Penalised Regression for High-Dimensional Data
#
# This script simulates a high-dimensional biomedical prediction problem
# and compares ridge, lasso, and elastic net.

library(tidyverse)
library(pROC)
library(glmnet)

set.seed(123)

n_patients <- 250
n_features <- 600
n_signal_features <- 10

x <- matrix(
  rnorm(n_patients * n_features),
  nrow = n_patients,
  ncol = n_features
)

colnames(x) <- paste0("feature_", seq_len(n_features))

true_coefficients <- c(
  rep(0.8, n_signal_features),
  rep(0, n_features - n_signal_features)
)

linear_predictor <- x %*% true_coefficients

probability <- 1 / (1 + exp(-linear_predictor))

outcome <- rbinom(
  n = n_patients,
  size = 1,
  prob = probability
)

train_index <- sample(seq_len(n_patients), size = round(0.80 * n_patients))

x_train <- x[train_index, ]
x_test <- x[-train_index, ]

y_train <- outcome[train_index]
y_test <- outcome[-train_index]

ridge_model <- cv.glmnet(
  x = x_train,
  y = y_train,
  family = "binomial",
  alpha = 0
)

lasso_model <- cv.glmnet(
  x = x_train,
  y = y_train,
  family = "binomial",
  alpha = 1
)

elastic_net_model <- cv.glmnet(
  x = x_train,
  y = y_train,
  family = "binomial",
  alpha = 0.5
)

ridge_risk <- as.numeric(
  predict(
    ridge_model,
    newx = x_test,
    s = "lambda.min",
    type = "response"
  )
)

lasso_risk <- as.numeric(
  predict(
    lasso_model,
    newx = x_test,
    s = "lambda.min",
    type = "response"
  )
)

elastic_net_risk <- as.numeric(
  predict(
    elastic_net_model,
    newx = x_test,
    s = "lambda.min",
    type = "response"
  )
)

calculate_auc <- function(actual, risk) {
  roc_object <- roc(
    response = actual,
    predictor = risk,
    quiet = TRUE
  )

  as.numeric(auc(roc_object))
}

count_selected_features <- function(model) {
  coefficients <- coef(model, s = "lambda.min")

  coefficient_table <- tibble(
    term = rownames(coefficients),
    coefficient = as.numeric(coefficients)
  )

  coefficient_table %>%
    filter(term != "(Intercept)", coefficient != 0) %>%
    nrow()
}

model_comparison <- tibble(
  model = c(
    "Ridge regression",
    "Lasso regression",
    "Elastic net"
  ),
  alpha = c(0, 1, 0.5),
  lambda_min = c(
    ridge_model$lambda.min,
    lasso_model$lambda.min,
    elastic_net_model$lambda.min
  ),
  test_auc = c(
    calculate_auc(y_test, ridge_risk),
    calculate_auc(y_test, lasso_risk),
    calculate_auc(y_test, elastic_net_risk)
  ),
  selected_features = c(
    count_selected_features(ridge_model),
    count_selected_features(lasso_model),
    count_selected_features(elastic_net_model)
  )
)

cat("\nHigh-dimensional dataset summary:\n")
cat("Number of patients:", n_patients, "\n")
cat("Number of predictors:", n_features, "\n")
cat("Number of true signal predictors:", n_signal_features, "\n")
cat("Outcome prevalence:", round(mean(outcome), 3), "\n")

cat("\nModel comparison:\n")
print(model_comparison)

lasso_coefficients <- coef(lasso_model, s = "lambda.min")

lasso_selected <- tibble(
  term = rownames(lasso_coefficients),
  coefficient = as.numeric(lasso_coefficients)
) %>%
  filter(term != "(Intercept)", coefficient != 0)

cat("\nFeatures selected by lasso:\n")
print(lasso_selected)

elastic_net_coefficients <- coef(elastic_net_model, s = "lambda.min")

elastic_net_selected <- tibble(
  term = rownames(elastic_net_coefficients),
  coefficient = as.numeric(elastic_net_coefficients)
) %>%
  filter(term != "(Intercept)", coefficient != 0)

cat("\nFeatures selected by elastic net:\n")
print(elastic_net_selected)

plot(lasso_model, main = "High-Dimensional Lasso Cross-Validation")
plot(elastic_net_model, main = "High-Dimensional Elastic Net Cross-Validation")

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. High-dimensional data can have more predictors than patients.\n")
cat("2. Ordinary regression is often unstable in high-dimensional settings.\n")
cat("3. Ridge shrinks many coefficients but usually keeps predictors.\n")
cat("4. Lasso can select a smaller set of features.\n")
cat("5. Elastic net combines ridge and lasso behaviour.\n")
cat("6. High-dimensional models need careful validation and leakage prevention.\n")
cat("7. Selected features are not automatically causal or biologically confirmed.\n")

Part C: High-dimensional scenario

A dataset contains n = 300 participants and p = 450,000 CpG methylation sites.

Question 7

Why is this a high-dimensional problem?

Show suggested answer

There are far more predictors than participants: 450,000 CpG sites and only 300 participants. This is a p >> n problem.

Question 8

Why would ordinary logistic regression not be appropriate here?

Show suggested answer

Ordinary logistic regression cannot safely estimate so many coefficients from so few observations. It would overfit or fail to estimate stable coefficients.

Question 9

Why might lasso be attractive in this setting?

Show suggested answer

Lasso may be attractive because it can shrink many coefficients to zero and select a smaller set of CpG sites.

Question 10

Why might elastic net be preferable to pure lasso?

Show suggested answer

Elastic net may be preferable because neighbouring CpG sites are often correlated. Elastic net can handle correlated predictors more smoothly than pure lasso.

Part D: Tuning and validation

Question 11

What does lambda control in penalised regression?

Show suggested answer

Lambda controls the strength of the penalty.

Question 12

What does alpha control in elastic net?

Show suggested answer

Alpha controls the balance between ridge-like and lasso-like behaviour.

Question 13

A researcher tunes lambda repeatedly using the final test set. Why is this a problem?

Show suggested answer

The test set becomes part of model development, so the final test performance may be optimistic.

Question 14

What is nested cross-validation?

Show suggested answer

Nested cross-validation separates model tuning from performance estimation. The inner loop tunes the model, while the outer loop estimates performance.

Question 15

Why is nested cross-validation useful in high-dimensional prediction?

Show suggested answer

It is useful because high-dimensional workflows involve many choices, such as feature selection, preprocessing and tuning. Nested validation helps avoid optimistic performance estimates.

Part E: Preprocessing leakage

Question 16

A researcher performs PCA using the full dataset, then cross-validates using PCA components. What is wrong?

Show suggested answer

The validation folds influenced the PCA components because PCA was performed using the full dataset. This creates preprocessing leakage.

Question 17

How should PCA or other preprocessing be handled in cross-validation?

Show suggested answer

PCA or preprocessing should be learned inside each training fold and then applied to the validation fold.

Question 18

Give three examples of preprocessing steps that can cause leakage if performed before validation.

Show suggested answer

Examples include PCA, normalisation, scaling, feature filtering, batch correction, imputation and outcome-based feature selection.

Part F: Batch effects

Question 19

What is a batch effect?

Show suggested answer

A batch effect is technical variation unrelated to biology.

Question 20

Give three examples of batch effects in biomedical data.

Show suggested answer

Examples include sequencing run, methylation plate, lab technician, processing date, scanner, hospital site or assay platform.

Question 21

Why can batch effects be especially dangerous in high-dimensional prediction?

Part G: Calibration and interpretation

Question 22

Why is AUC alone not enough to evaluate a high-dimensional medical prediction model?

Show suggested answer

AUC measures discrimination but not calibration or clinical usefulness. A model may rank patients well but give inaccurate risk probabilities.

Question 23

List four additional assessments that should be considered.

Show suggested answer

Additional assessments include calibration plot, calibration slope, calibration intercept, Brier score, decision curve analysis, external validation and feature stability.

Question 24

A penalised model selects 20 genes. Can we conclude these genes are causal mechanisms?

Show suggested answer

No. Selected genes may be causal, but they may also be proxies, correlated features, batch artefacts or selected by chance. Prediction selection is not causal proof.

Part H: Full case analysis

A team builds a recurrence prediction model using 500 cancer patients, 80 recurrence events and 20,000 gene expression features. They use elastic net and report internal cross-validated AUC = 0.91 but no calibration or external validation.

Question 25

Why should we be cautious about this result?

Show suggested answer

The AUC may be optimistic, especially if preprocessing, tuning, or feature selection were not properly nested. Calibration and external validation are missing.

Question 26

What additional information should be reported?

Show suggested answer

They should report preprocessing details, feature selection procedure, tuning method, validation design, calibration, Brier score, selected features, feature stability, batch handling and external validation if available.

Question 27

Show suggested answer

Define the prediction question and time point. Split data or define outer cross-validation folds. Within each training fold, perform preprocessing and feature filtering. Tune elastic net using inner cross-validation. Evaluate predictions in the outer validation fold. Assess AUC, calibration, Brier score and clinical usefulness. Check feature stability. Validate externally if possible. Interpret selected genes cautiously.

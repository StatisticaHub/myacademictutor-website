Lesson 11: Ridge Regression

Ridge regression is a regularisation method that shrinks coefficients towards zero. It is especially useful when ordinary regression becomes unstable because predictors are correlated, sample size is limited, or the number of candidate predictors is large.

Today’s big question

How can ridge regression make a prediction model more stable?

Ridge regression is a regularised regression method. It keeps the usual goal of predicting the outcome, but it adds a penalty that discourages very large coefficient values.

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients towards zero, usually keeping all predictors in the model while reducing overfitting risk.

Why ridge is needed

Ordinary regression can become unstable

Ordinary regression can work well when the dataset is large, the number of predictors is modest, and predictors are not strongly correlated. But in medical prediction, this ideal situation is not always present.

Patients = 180
Outcome events = 28
Candidate predictors = 24

In this situation, ordinary logistic regression may fit random patterns in the data. Coefficients can become large, unstable, or sensitive to small changes in the sample.

Clinical meaning

If a model changes greatly when a few patients are added or removed, it may not be reliable enough for future clinical use.

Shrinkage

Ridge pulls coefficients towards zero

Ridge regression adds a penalty for large coefficients. This means the model is discouraged from relying too strongly on any one predictor.

Predictor Ordinary coefficient Ridge coefficient
Glucose1.300.95
BMI0.850.62
Age0.500.36
Blood pressure0.300.18

The coefficients have moved closer to zero, but none of the predictors has been removed. This is the key ridge idea: shrink, do not usually select.

Correlated predictors

Ridge is useful when predictors overlap

Clinical predictors often carry overlapping information.

Body size measures

BMI, waist circumference, weight, body fat percentage

These variables may all reflect related biological information.

Biomedical markers

Inflammatory biomarkers from the same pathway

Several biomarkers may each contain small amounts of related predictive information.

Ordinary regression may struggle to separate the individual effects of correlated predictors. Ridge can stabilise the model by shrinking their coefficients rather than allowing one correlated variable to dominate by chance.

Lambda

The tuning parameter controlling shrinkage

Ridge regression has a tuning parameter usually called lambda. Lambda controls how strongly coefficients are shrunk.

Small lambda

Less shrinkage

The model behaves more like ordinary regression and may still overfit.

Large lambda

More shrinkage

The model may become too simple and underfit if shrinkage is too strong.

Lambda is commonly chosen using cross-validation inside the training data.

Common mistake: using the final test set repeatedly to choose lambda

If the final test set is used repeatedly to choose lambda, it becomes part of model development. The reported test performance may then be too optimistic.

Dr Clinic:

Clinical scenario

1-year hospital readmission prediction

A team is developing a model using:

  • age and sex;
  • number of previous admissions;
  • length of hospital stay;
  • number of medications;
  • comorbidity score;
  • blood pressure and heart rate;
  • creatinine and haemoglobin.

Several predictors are correlated. Ridge may be appropriate because it can reduce instability, shrink coefficients, and allow related predictors to contribute without allowing extreme coefficient values.

But ridge does not solve everything

Ridge does not automatically fix leakage, poor outcome definition, missing important predictors, poor calibration, poor data quality, or lack of external validation.

Detailed notes

Note 11: Ridge Regression

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients towards zero to improve model stability.

Learning objectives

  • Explain why ordinary regression can overfit.
  • Describe coefficient shrinkage.
  • Explain why ridge usually keeps all predictors.
  • Understand the role of lambda.
  • Explain why ridge helps with correlated predictors.
  • Recognise why validation and calibration are still needed.

1. Why this note matters

Ridge regression is useful when ordinary regression is too unstable for reliable medical prediction. This can happen when predictors are correlated, when there are many predictors, or when the number of outcome events is limited.

In clinical prediction, the aim is not simply to fit the development data well. The aim is to produce predictions that are reliable for new patients.

2. Why ordinary regression can overfit

Ordinary regression estimates coefficients by trying to fit the observed data as well as possible. If the dataset is small or has many predictors, the model may assign large coefficients to patterns that are partly random.

This can make the model look strong in the training data but weak in future patients. The problem is worse when there are few outcome events relative to the number of candidate predictors.

3. Core concept: coefficient shrinkage

Ridge regression adds a penalty for large coefficients. The model still tries to predict the outcome, but it is discouraged from assigning very large effects to individual predictors.

Shrinkage means coefficients are pulled towards zero. They become smaller in magnitude, which can reduce overfitting and make predictions less extreme.

4. Ridge usually keeps all predictors

Unlike lasso, ridge regression does not usually shrink coefficients exactly to zero. This means ridge is not mainly a feature selection method.

Ridge is useful when many predictors may each contain small amounts of predictive information. It lets them contribute while limiting their coefficient sizes.

5. Lambda controls the amount of shrinkage

The amount of shrinkage is controlled by a tuning parameter, usually called lambda.

LambdaShrinkageRisk
Small lambdaLess shrinkageModel may still overfit
Large lambdaMore shrinkageModel may underfit if too strong

Lambda is commonly chosen using cross-validation. The final test set should not be repeatedly used to choose lambda, because then the test set becomes part of model development.

6. Medical and biomedical motivation

Clinical predictors are often correlated. For example, BMI, waist circumference, body fat percentage and weight all carry related information. Tumour size and tumour stage may also overlap. Biomarkers from the same pathway may be correlated.

Ordinary regression may struggle to separate the individual contribution of each correlated predictor. Ridge can stabilise coefficients in this setting.

7. Ridge and correlated predictors

When predictors are strongly correlated, ordinary regression coefficients can become unstable. One variable may receive a large positive coefficient while another related variable receives a small or unexpected coefficient.

Ridge reduces this instability by shrinking coefficients. It can allow correlated predictors to share predictive information instead of forcing the model to rely too heavily on one variable.

8. Ridge in omics and biomarker prediction

In omics and biomarker datasets, many predictors may be weakly related to the outcome. Ridge can be useful when the signal is spread across many variables rather than concentrated in a small feature set.

However, because ridge usually keeps many predictors, it may be less useful if the goal is to create a short, sparse biomarker panel. In that case, lasso or elastic net may be considered.

9. Validation issues

Ridge tuning must be done using training data or resampling. The final test set should be used only once for final evaluation.

Performance assessment should include:

  • discrimination such as AUC;
  • calibration plot;
  • Brier score;
  • calibration slope and intercept;
  • threshold-specific performance;
  • clinical usefulness;
  • external validation if possible.

10. Interpretation issues

Ridge coefficients are prediction coefficients. They should not automatically be interpreted as causal effects. A ridge coefficient may reflect correlation, proxy information, measurement structure, or model stabilisation.

11. Common mistakes

  • Calling ridge a feature selection method.
  • Assuming shrinkage solves data leakage.
  • Reporting only training performance.
  • Reporting only AUC.
  • Ignoring calibration.
  • Using the final test set repeatedly to choose lambda.
  • Interpreting ridge coefficients as causal effects.

12. Worked example idea

A practical workflow is to fit ordinary logistic regression and ridge regression to the same diabetes dataset, compare test AUC, inspect the coefficient sizes, and then assess calibration.

If ridge gives similar discrimination but less extreme predictions and better calibration, it may be preferable for clinical prediction.

13. Summary

Ridge regression is a regularised method that reduces overfitting by shrinking coefficients. It is especially helpful when predictors are correlated or when many predictors each contain small predictive information.

Ridge improves stability, but it does not replace validation, calibration assessment, or clinical judgement.

R practical: ordinary logistic regression versus ridge regression

This script fits ordinary logistic regression and ridge regression for diabetes prediction, compares test AUC, compares coefficients, and plots the ridge cross-validation curve.

Ridge model

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 11: Ridge Regression
#
# This script fits ordinary logistic regression and ridge regression
# for diabetes prediction and compares their test AUC.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)
library(pROC)
library(glmnet)

set.seed(123)

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

train_index <- sample(seq_len(nrow(diabetes_data)), size = round(0.80 * nrow(diabetes_data)))

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

x_train <- model.matrix(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data
)[, -1]

x_test <- model.matrix(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = test_data
)[, -1]

y_train <- ifelse(train_data$diabetes == "pos", 1, 0)
y_test_factor <- test_data$diabetes

ordinary_model <- glm(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  family = binomial
)

ordinary_risk <- predict(
  ordinary_model,
  newdata = test_data,
  type = "response"
)

ridge_model <- cv.glmnet(
  x = x_train,
  y = y_train,
  family = "binomial",
  alpha = 0
)

ridge_risk <- as.numeric(
  predict(
    ridge_model,
    newx = x_test,
    s = "lambda.min",
    type = "response"
  )
)

calculate_auc <- function(actual, risk) {
  roc_object <- roc(
    response = actual,
    predictor = risk,
    levels = c("neg", "pos"),
    quiet = TRUE
  )

  as.numeric(auc(roc_object))
}

model_comparison <- tibble(
  model = c(
    "Ordinary logistic regression",
    "Ridge regression"
  ),
  auc = c(
    calculate_auc(y_test_factor, ordinary_risk),
    calculate_auc(y_test_factor, ridge_risk)
  )
)

cat("\nModel comparison using test AUC:\n")
print(model_comparison)

ordinary_coefficients <- tibble(
  term = names(coef(ordinary_model)),
  ordinary_coefficient = as.numeric(coef(ordinary_model))
)

ridge_coefficients <- coef(ridge_model, s = "lambda.min")

ridge_coefficients_table <- tibble(
  term = rownames(ridge_coefficients),
  ridge_coefficient = as.numeric(ridge_coefficients)
)

coefficient_comparison <- ordinary_coefficients %>%
  left_join(ridge_coefficients_table, by = "term")

cat("\nOrdinary logistic regression coefficients versus ridge coefficients:\n")
print(coefficient_comparison)

plot(ridge_model, main = "Ridge Cross-Validation")

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. Ridge regression shrinks coefficients towards zero.\n")
cat("2. Ridge usually keeps predictors in the model.\n")
cat("3. Ridge can be useful when predictors are correlated.\n")
cat("4. Ridge may reduce overfitting compared with ordinary logistic regression.\n")
cat("5. Ridge models still need validation and calibration assessment.\n")

Exercise 11: Ridge Regression

This exercise helps you understand why ridge regression is useful in medical prediction modelling, especially when ordinary regression may overfit or become unstable.

Part A: Conceptual understanding

Question 1

In your own words, explain what ridge regression does. Try to use shrinkage, coefficients, overfitting and prediction.

Show suggested answer

Ridge regression is a regularisation method that shrinks regression coefficients towards zero. This helps reduce overfitting by preventing the model from relying too strongly on patterns that may be specific to the training data. It can make predictions more stable in new patients.

Question 2

Which statement is most correct?

  1. Ridge regression removes weak predictors from the model.
  2. Ridge regression usually keeps all predictors but shrinks their coefficients.
  3. Ridge regression always improves AUC.
  4. Ridge regression removes the need for validation.
Show suggested answer

B. Ridge usually keeps all predictors but shrinks their coefficients.

Question 3

Why might ordinary logistic regression become unstable with 180 patients, 28 events and 24 predictors?

Show suggested answer

The model may be unstable because there are relatively few outcome events compared with the number of candidate predictors. With only 28 events and 24 predictors, the model may fit random patterns in the data. This can produce large or unstable coefficients and poor performance in new patients.

Question 4

A student says ridge is useful because it selects the most important clinical predictors. Explain why this is not fully correct.

Show suggested answer

Ridge regression is not mainly a feature selection method. It usually keeps predictors in the model and shrinks their coefficients. Lasso is more commonly used when the aim is to set some coefficients to zero and select a smaller predictor set.

Show suggested answer

The coefficients were shrunk towards zero. No predictors were removed. Smaller coefficients may reduce overfitting and make predictions less extreme, which may improve performance in new patients.

Question 6

Explain the difference between fitting training data very well and generalising well to new patients.

Show suggested answer

A model that fits the training data very well may have learned random noise. A model that generalises well performs well on new patients who were not used to build the model. In medical prediction, generalisation is more important because the model will be used on future patients.

Part C: Correlated predictors

Question 7

BMI, waist circumference, body fat percentage and weight are strongly correlated. Why might ordinary logistic regression coefficients become unstable?

Show suggested answer

When predictors are strongly correlated, ordinary regression may struggle to separate their individual effects. This can produce unstable coefficients, where one variable receives a large coefficient and another correlated variable receives a small or unexpected coefficient.

Question 8

Why might ridge regression be useful when predictors are correlated?

Show suggested answer

Ridge regression can shrink correlated predictors and make the model less sensitive to small changes in the dataset. This can produce more stable predictions.

Question 9

A model includes 10 biomarkers from the same inflammatory pathway. Ridge keeps all 10 but shrinks them. Is this necessarily a problem?

Show suggested answer

No, not necessarily. If all 10 biomarkers contain small amounts of predictive information, ridge may allow them to contribute while controlling their coefficient sizes. However, the model may be less simple to interpret than a smaller selected model.

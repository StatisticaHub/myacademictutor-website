Today’s big question

How can a decision tree split patients into clinically meaningful risk groups?

A decision tree predicts outcomes by repeatedly splitting patients into groups using predictor values.

A tree may start with all patients together. It then asks a question such as: is glucose above a certain value? The answer sends each patient down a branch. The tree keeps splitting until it reaches final groups called terminal nodes or leaves.

Intuition first

A decision tree is a sequence of questions

Decision trees are attractive in medical prediction because they can look like clinical reasoning. They do not require the relationship between predictors and outcome to be a straight line. They can also represent interactions naturally.

Root node
Is glucose high?
No
Check BMI and age
Lower estimated risk group
Yes
Check BMI and insulin
Higher estimated risk group

The exact split values are learned from the development data. That is useful, but it also creates risk: the tree may learn random patterns that do not generalise.

Tree anatomy

The main parts of a decision tree

Term Meaning Clinical interpretation
Root node The first node containing all patients. The starting point of the prediction rule.
Decision node A node where a split is made. A clinical question such as glucose above a threshold.
Branch The path after a split. The route followed by a patient.
Terminal node The final node where prediction is made. A final risk group.
Tree depth How many levels of splitting the tree has. How complex the rule becomes.

Clinical reality check

A tree can be easy to explain, but explanation is not the same as validity. A tree still needs internal validation, calibration assessment and preferably external validation.

How splitting works

The tree looks for useful patient groups

At each node, the algorithm searches across predictors and possible cut-points. It chooses a split that makes the resulting groups more different in outcome risk.

For a binary outcome, the split may try to make one child node contain a higher proportion of patients with the outcome and the other child node contain a lower proportion.

Parent node → split → child node 1 + child node 2

The model repeats this process. Each split is locally chosen to improve separation. This is powerful, but it also means the model can chase noise if it is not constrained.

Medical example

Diabetes prediction using a decision tree

Suppose we want to predict whether a patient has diabetes using:

  • glucose;
  • BMI;
  • age;
  • blood pressure;
  • insulin;
  • family diabetes pedigree.

A simple tree may first split on glucose. Among patients with high glucose, it may then split on BMI. Among patients with lower glucose, it may split on age or pedigree.

Why this is useful

Interpretability

Clinicians can follow the sequence of splits and understand how a patient reaches a risk group.

Why this is risky

Instability

A slightly different dataset may produce a different first split or different risk groups.

Overfitting

A tree can become too specific

If a tree is allowed to keep splitting, it may create many small terminal nodes. Some leaves may contain only a few patients. These tiny groups may reflect noise rather than real clinical structure.

Captain Overfit:
“Give me unlimited depth and I will make the training accuracy look amazing.”

Curious Learner:
“But will that help future patients?”

Prof Stat:
“Not necessarily. A very deep tree may memorise the development dataset.”

Dr Clinic:
“So we need to control depth and test the model honestly.”

Common ways to control overfitting include:

  • limiting maximum depth;
  • requiring a minimum number of patients before a split;
  • requiring a minimum number of patients in terminal nodes;
  • using a complexity penalty;
  • pruning the tree;
  • validating the model on unseen data.

Performance assessment

A tree needs the same clinical evaluation as other models

A decision tree should not be judged only by how simple it looks or how high its training accuracy is. It should be evaluated using:

  • test or cross-validated discrimination such as AUC;
  • confusion matrix at clinically meaningful thresholds;
  • sensitivity and specificity;
  • calibration plot;
  • Brier score;
  • clinical usefulness if linked to a decision;
  • external validation if possible.

A clinically interpretable tree is not automatically a clinically reliable tree.

Detailed notes

Note 16: Decision Trees for Clinical Prediction

Decision trees are intuitive prediction models that split patients into risk groups, but they are unstable unless their complexity is controlled and their performance is validated.

Learning objectives

  • Explain how a decision tree makes predictions.
  • Define root node, decision node, branch and terminal node.
  • Explain how trees split patients into risk groups.
  • Understand why trees can model non-linear relationships and interactions.
  • Explain why single trees can be unstable.
  • Recognise why tree depth can lead to overfitting.
  • Describe how decision trees should be evaluated in clinical prediction.

1. Why this note matters

Decision trees are often one of the first machine learning methods that learners find intuitive. They produce rules that can be drawn as a flowchart, which makes them attractive in clinical settings.

However, the simplicity of the diagram can be misleading. A tree can still overfit, behave unstably, and produce poorly calibrated predictions.

2. What a decision tree does

A decision tree recursively partitions the data. It starts with all patients in one group. It then chooses a predictor and cut-point that separates patients into groups with different outcome risks. This process continues until stopping rules are reached.

3. Classification trees and regression trees

A classification tree predicts a categorical outcome, such as diabetes present versus absent. A regression tree predicts a continuous outcome, such as blood pressure or hospital length of stay.

4. Splits and risk groups

Each split divides patients according to a rule such as glucose less than a threshold or BMI above a threshold. The final terminal nodes define risk groups. In a binary prediction problem, each terminal node can estimate the probability of the outcome.

5. Why trees can capture interactions

Trees naturally represent interactions because later splits depend on earlier splits. For example, BMI may matter differently among patients with high glucose than among patients with low glucose.

6. Why trees can capture non-linearity

A tree does not assume a straight-line relationship between a predictor and risk. It can split glucose into ranges and assign different risks to those ranges.

7. Instability of single trees

A single decision tree can be unstable. A small change in the data may change the first split, which then changes many later splits. This is one reason random forests are used later in this module.

8. Overfitting in decision trees

If a tree grows too deep, it can create many small patient groups. These groups may fit the development data well but fail in future patients. Controlling maximum depth, minimum split size and pruning can reduce this risk.

9. Clinical interpretation

A tree may be clinically appealing because it shows a clear pathway. But the splits are selected by an algorithm, not by clinical truth. Therefore, each split should be checked for clinical plausibility and prediction-time availability.

10. Evaluation

Decision trees should be evaluated using discrimination, calibration, Brier score, threshold-based performance and clinical usefulness. External validation is important before strong clinical claims are made.

Common mistakes

  • Assuming a simple-looking tree is automatically valid.
  • Reporting training accuracy only.
  • Letting a tree grow too deep without validation.
  • Interpreting a split as causal evidence.
  • Ignoring calibration because the tree is easy to visualise.

Summary

Decision trees are useful for building intuition about machine learning. They can create clear patient risk groups and model non-linear patterns. Their main weaknesses are instability and overfitting.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 16: Decision Trees for Clinical Prediction
#
# This script fits a decision tree for diabetes prediction.
# It also compares the tree with logistic regression and checks
# discrimination, calibration and simple threshold-based classification.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)
library(pROC)
library(rpart)
library(rpart.plot)

set.seed(123)

# 1. Load and prepare data

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

candidate_predictors <- c(
  "pregnant",
  "glucose",
  "pressure",
  "triceps",
  "insulin",
  "mass",
  "pedigree",
  "age"
)

cat("\nDataset summary:\n")
cat("Number of patients:", nrow(diabetes_data), "\n")
cat("Outcome distribution:\n")
print(table(diabetes_data$diabetes))

# 2. Training/test split

train_index <- sample(
  seq_len(nrow(diabetes_data)),
  size = round(0.80 * nrow(diabetes_data))
)

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

cat("\nTraining patients:", nrow(train_data), "\n")
cat("Test patients:", nrow(test_data), "\n")

# 3. Fit a decision tree

tree_model <- rpart(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  method = "class",
  control = rpart.control(
    cp = 0.01,
    minsplit = 20,
    minbucket = 7,
    maxdepth = 4
  )
)

cat("\nDecision tree complexity table:\n")
printcp(tree_model)

cat("\nDecision tree variable importance:\n")
print(tree_model$variable.importance)

# 4. Plot the tree

rpart.plot(
  tree_model,
  type = 2,
  extra = 104,
  fallen.leaves = TRUE,
  main = "Decision Tree for Diabetes Prediction"
)

# 5. Predict risk on test data

tree_risk <- predict(
  tree_model,
  newdata = test_data,
  type = "prob"
)[, "pos"]

test_predictions <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_risk_tree = tree_risk,
    predicted_class_tree = factor(
      ifelse(predicted_risk_tree >= 0.50, "pos", "neg"),
      levels = c("neg", "pos")
    )
  )

cat("\nFirst few tree predictions:\n")
print(
  test_predictions %>%
    select(diabetes, predicted_risk_tree, predicted_class_tree) %>%
    head()
)

# 6. Confusion matrix at threshold 0.50

confusion_matrix <- table(
  observed = test_predictions$diabetes,
  predicted = test_predictions$predicted_class_tree
)

cat("\nConfusion matrix at threshold 0.50:\n")
print(confusion_matrix)

sensitivity <- confusion_matrix["pos", "pos"] /
  sum(confusion_matrix["pos", ])

specificity <- confusion_matrix["neg", "neg"] /
  sum(confusion_matrix["neg", ])

cat("\nSensitivity at threshold 0.50:", round(sensitivity, 3), "\n")
cat("Specificity at threshold 0.50:", round(specificity, 3), "\n")

# 7. ROC curve and AUC

tree_roc <- roc(
  response = test_predictions$diabetes,
  predictor = test_predictions$predicted_risk_tree,
  levels = c("neg", "pos"),
  quiet = TRUE
)

tree_auc <- as.numeric(auc(tree_roc))

cat("\nDecision tree test AUC:\n")
print(tree_auc)

plot(
  tree_roc,
  main = "Decision Tree ROC Curve",
  legacy.axes = TRUE
)

# 8. Calibration-style grouped risk summary

calibration_table <- test_predictions %>%
  mutate(risk_group = ntile(predicted_risk_tree, 5)) %>%
  group_by(risk_group) %>%
  summarise(
    mean_predicted_risk = mean(predicted_risk_tree),
    observed_risk = mean(diabetes == "pos"),
    n = n(),
    .groups = "drop"
  )

cat("\nGrouped calibration summary:\n")
print(calibration_table)

plot(
  calibration_table$mean_predicted_risk,
  calibration_table$observed_risk,
  xlim = c(0, 1),
  ylim = c(0, 1),
  xlab = "Mean predicted risk",
  ylab = "Observed risk",
  main = "Grouped Calibration: Decision Tree"
)

abline(0, 1, lty = 2)

# 9. Compare with logistic regression

logistic_model <- glm(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  family = binomial
)

logistic_risk <- predict(
  logistic_model,
  newdata = test_data,
  type = "response"
)

logistic_roc <- roc(
  response = test_data$diabetes,
  predictor = logistic_risk,
  levels = c("neg", "pos"),
  quiet = TRUE
)

model_comparison <- tibble(
  model = c("Decision tree", "Logistic regression"),
  test_auc = c(
    as.numeric(auc(tree_roc)),
    as.numeric(auc(logistic_roc))
  ),
  brier_score = c(
    mean((tree_risk - as.numeric(test_data$diabetes == "pos"))^2),
    mean((logistic_risk - as.numeric(test_data$diabetes == "pos"))^2)
  )
)

cat("\nModel comparison:\n")
print(model_comparison)

# 10. Learning points

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. A decision tree splits patients into risk groups using predictor values.\n")
cat("2. Tree splits are easy to visualise and can resemble clinical decision rules.\n")
cat("3. A single tree can be unstable and may overfit if it grows too deep.\n")
cat("4. Test AUC assesses discrimination, but calibration should also be checked.\n")
cat("5. A clinically interpretable model is not automatically a clinically valid model.\n")

Exercise 16: Decision Trees for Clinical Prediction

Try these questions before checking the suggested answers.

Part A: Conceptual understanding

Question 1

Explain what a decision tree does in your own words.

Show suggested answer

A decision tree predicts an outcome by asking a sequence of questions about predictor values and sending patients down branches until they reach a terminal node where a prediction is made.

Question 2

Define root node, decision node and terminal node.

Show suggested answer

The root node is the starting point containing all patients. A decision node is a point where the tree makes a split. A terminal node is a final node where the predicted class or risk is assigned.

Question 3

Why might decision trees be attractive in clinical prediction?

Show suggested answer

They are visual, intuitive and can resemble clinical decision pathways. They can also show how patients are split into risk groups.

Question 4

Why can a decision tree capture interactions?

Show suggested answer

Later splits depend on earlier splits, so the effect of a predictor can differ across patient subgroups created by previous splits.

Part B: Clinical interpretation

Question 5

A diabetes tree first splits on glucose and then on BMI. How would you explain this to a clinician?

Show suggested answer

The model first uses glucose because it separates patients into groups with different diabetes risk. Within those groups, BMI provides additional information for refining the risk estimate.

Question 6

If a tree uses hospital site as the first split, what concern might you have?

Show suggested answer

Hospital site may reflect differences in data collection, case mix or practice patterns rather than patient biology. It could also indicate site-related leakage or poor generalisability.

Question 7

Does a split on glucose prove that glucose causes the outcome?

Show suggested answer

No. A split shows that glucose helped prediction in this model and dataset. It does not by itself prove causality.

Part C: Overfitting and validation

Question 8

Why can a very deep tree overfit?

Show suggested answer

A very deep tree can create many tiny patient groups that fit noise in the training data. These rules may not work for new patients.

Question 9

Give three ways to control tree complexity.

Show suggested answer

Limit maximum depth, require a minimum number of patients for a split, require a minimum terminal node size, use a complexity penalty or prune the tree.

Question 10

Why is training accuracy not enough?

Show suggested answer

Training accuracy can be optimistic because the tree was built using the training data. We need validation or test performance to estimate how it may work for new patients.

Question 11

What else should be assessed besides AUC?

Show suggested answer

Calibration, Brier score, sensitivity, specificity, threshold-specific performance, clinical usefulness and external validation should be considered.

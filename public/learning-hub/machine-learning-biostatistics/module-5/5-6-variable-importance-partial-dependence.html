Today’s big question

How can we interpret complex tree-based models without overclaiming?

Variable importance and partial dependence help describe model behaviour, but they do not prove biological, clinical or causal mechanisms.

Key caution

A PDP is an average. It may hide patient-level differences. ICE curves can reveal whether the same average pattern applies similarly to different patients.

ICE plots

Individual conditional expectation shows heterogeneity

Partial dependence averages over patients. Individual conditional expectation, or ICE, shows how the prediction changes for individual patients as a predictor changes.

ICE plots can reveal whether an average PDP is hiding different patterns. For example, glucose may increase risk sharply for one subgroup but only slightly for another.

Correlation caution

Correlated predictors can mislead interpretation

Interpretation becomes difficult when predictors are correlated. The model may split importance between predictors, or one predictor may appear important because it acts as a proxy for another.

Example: correlated biomedical predictors

BMI and waist circumference

Both may contain overlapping information about body composition. Importance may be divided between them.

Hospital site and measurement pattern

Hospital site may predict outcome because of case mix or recording practice, not because the site itself causes the outcome.

Common mistake

Do not say “this variable is biologically important” just because it has high model importance. Say “this variable was important for prediction in this model and dataset.”

Clinical interpretation

Good interpretation is careful, limited and clinically grounded

Interpretation tools should be used to understand model behaviour, generate hypotheses and communicate patterns. They should not replace validation, calibration or causal study design.

Safe reporting

The model relied strongly on glucose and BMI, and PDPs suggested increasing predicted risk at higher values.

Unsafe reporting

The model proves that glucose and BMI are causal drivers of diabetes in this cohort.

Better conclusion

These findings are clinically plausible but should be interpreted as predictive model behaviour.

Interpretation plots explain the fitted model. They do not automatically explain nature, biology or causality.

Detailed notes

Note 21: Variable Importance and Partial Dependence

Variable importance and partial dependence help describe how a model behaves, but they must be interpreted cautiously because they do not prove causality and can be distorted by correlation, leakage and instability.

Learning objectives

  • Explain what a partial dependence plot shows.

1. Why this note matters

As models become more flexible, interpretation becomes more difficult. A single decision tree can be drawn as a pathway, but random forests, gradient boosting and XGBoost combine many trees. Their predictions are not easily reduced to one simple rule.

Variable importance and partial dependence are commonly used to explain such models. They can be helpful, but they are often overinterpreted. In medical prediction, overinterpretation can be dangerous because it can turn a predictive pattern into an unsupported clinical or biological claim.

2. What variable importance means

Variable importance measures how strongly a predictor contributes to prediction in a fitted model. It answers a model-specific question: which variables did this model use most?

It does not answer a causal question. It does not prove that changing the predictor would change the outcome.

3. Predictive importance versus causal importance

Predictive importance means a variable helps the model predict. Causal importance means a variable has a causal effect on the outcome. These are different concepts.

A variable can predict an outcome without causing it. For example, hospital site may predict mortality because hospitals treat different types of patients or record data differently. That does not mean the hospital site itself biologically causes mortality.

4. Impurity-based importance

Some tree-based models use impurity-based importance. This measures how much a predictor improves the splits in trees. In random forests, this may be reported as mean decrease in Gini impurity.

This measure can be biased toward variables with many possible split points or variables measured on certain scales.

5. Permutation importance

Permutation importance measures how much model performance decreases when a predictor is randomly shuffled. If shuffling a predictor strongly worsens performance, the predictor is important for prediction.

Permutation importance is intuitive, but it can be affected by correlated predictors. If another variable contains similar information, shuffling one predictor may not reduce performance much.

6. Gain, cover and frequency in boosted trees

Boosted tree methods such as XGBoost may report gain, cover and frequency. Gain measures improvement in the model due to splits using the predictor. Cover relates to the number of observations affected by those splits. Frequency counts how often a predictor is used.

These are useful summaries of model behaviour, not biological proof.

7. Why importance can be unstable

Importance can change when the training data change, when correlated predictors are added or removed, or when tuning parameters change. Therefore, importance should not be treated as a fixed truth.

A stronger workflow checks whether important predictors remain important across resamples, folds or external validation datasets.

8. Partial dependence plots

A partial dependence plot shows the average predicted outcome as one predictor changes while averaging over the observed distribution of other predictors.

For example, a PDP for glucose may show that average predicted diabetes risk increases as glucose rises.

9. What PDPs do not show

A PDP does not show individual patient behaviour. It shows an average pattern. Different patients may have different patterns.

A PDP also does not prove that changing glucose would causally change risk. It shows how the fitted model responds when glucose values are varied.

10. Unrealistic predictor combinations

PDPs can evaluate combinations of predictors that may be rare or unrealistic. This is especially problematic when predictors are correlated.

For example, a PDP might average predictions for high glucose values across patients whose other characteristics make that combination uncommon.

11. Individual conditional expectation plots

ICE plots show prediction curves for individual patients. Each line represents how one patient's predicted risk changes as a predictor changes.

ICE plots can reveal heterogeneity hidden by a PDP. If the ICE lines are very different, the average PDP may be too simple.

12. Accumulated local effects

Accumulated local effects, or ALE plots, are another interpretation method. ALE plots can be less problematic than PDPs when predictors are correlated because they focus on local changes within the observed data distribution.

ALE is not covered in depth here, but it is useful to know that PDPs are not the only option.

13. Correlated predictors

Correlation between predictors is common in biomedical data. BMI and waist circumference, multiple blood markers, repeated measurements, methylation probes and gene-expression features may contain overlapping information.

Correlation can distort both variable importance and partial dependence. Importance may be shared between correlated predictors, and PDPs may show patterns involving unrealistic combinations.

14. Leakage and interpretation

If a leaked predictor enters a model, interpretation plots may make it appear highly important. This can be misleading because the model is using information that would not be available at prediction time.

Therefore, interpretation should happen only after careful checks for leakage.

15. Clinical plausibility

Interpretation should be checked against clinical knowledge. If glucose is important in a diabetes prediction model, that is clinically plausible. If hospital site or date of data entry is highly important, that may signal site effects, case-mix differences or data artefacts.

Clinical plausibility does not prove causality, but implausible findings should trigger careful investigation.

16. Stability of explanations

A model explanation is more credible if it is stable across resampling or validation datasets. If the top predictors change drastically across folds, the interpretation should be reported cautiously.

17. Interpretation and calibration

A model can have plausible importance plots but still be poorly calibrated. Interpretation tools explain model behaviour. They do not tell us whether predicted probabilities match observed risks.

Calibration assessment remains essential for clinical prediction.

18. Interpretation and clinical usefulness

A model can have convincing explanation plots but still not improve clinical decisions. Clinical usefulness depends on the decision context, threshold probabilities and consequences of false positives and false negatives.

19. How to write safe interpretations

Use careful language such as:

  • “The model relied strongly on glucose for prediction.”
  • “Partial dependence suggested higher predicted risk at higher glucose values.”
  • “This pattern is clinically plausible but should not be interpreted as causal evidence.”
  • “Importance rankings may be affected by correlated predictors.”

20. How not to write interpretations

Avoid unsupported claims such as:

  • “The model discovered the causal drivers of disease.”
  • “The most important variable must be the best intervention target.”
  • “This PDP proves that changing the predictor changes the outcome.”
  • “The model is clinically useful because the plots look plausible.”

21. Reporting model interpretation

A clear report should describe:

  • which interpretation method was used;
  • whether importance was impurity-based, permutation-based, gain-based or another type;
  • whether predictors were correlated;
  • whether explanations were stable across validation;
  • how PDP or ICE plots were generated;
  • clinical plausibility of the patterns;
  • limitations of interpretation;
  • whether calibration and clinical usefulness were assessed separately.

22. Common mistakes

  • Interpreting variable importance as causality.
  • Ignoring correlated predictors.
  • Assuming PDPs apply equally to every patient.
  • Ignoring unrealistic predictor combinations.
  • Using interpretation plots before checking leakage.
  • Reporting explanation plots without validation or calibration.
  • Making biological claims from prediction models alone.

23. Summary

Variable importance and partial dependence are useful tools for understanding complex models. They help describe how the fitted model behaves, which predictors it uses and how average predictions vary across predictor values.

But these tools must be used carefully. They do not prove causality, may be distorted by correlated predictors, and do not replace validation, calibration or clinical usefulness assessment.

R practical: variable importance and partial dependence

This script fits a random forest, calculates variable importance, produces partial dependence plots, explores two-way partial dependence and checks calibration.

# Machine Learning in Biostatistics
# Script 21: Variable Importance and Partial Dependence
#
# This script fits a random forest and uses variable importance
# and partial dependence to interpret the model.
#
# Main ideas:
# 1. Variable importance describes how strongly a model uses predictors.
# 2. Importance is predictive importance, not causal importance.
# 3. Partial dependence shows average predicted risk across predictor values.
# 4. Partial dependence can be misleading when predictors are correlated.
# 5. Interpretation should be combined with clinical knowledge and validation.

library(tidyverse)
library(mlbench)
library(pROC)
library(randomForest)
library(pdp)

set.seed(123)

data("PimaIndiansDiabetes")

diabetes_data <- PimaIndiansDiabetes %>%
  mutate(
    diabetes = factor(diabetes, levels = c("neg", "pos"))
  )

cat("\nDataset summary:\n")
cat("Number of patients:", nrow(diabetes_data), "\n")
cat("Outcome distribution:\n")
print(table(diabetes_data$diabetes))

train_index <- sample(
  seq_len(nrow(diabetes_data)),
  size = round(0.80 * nrow(diabetes_data))
)

train_data <- diabetes_data[train_index, ]
test_data <- diabetes_data[-train_index, ]

cat("\nTraining patients:", nrow(train_data), "\n")
cat("Test patients:", nrow(test_data), "\n")

forest_model <- randomForest(
  diabetes ~ pregnant + glucose + pressure + triceps +
    insulin + mass + pedigree + age,
  data = train_data,
  ntree = 500,
  mtry = 3,
  importance = TRUE
)

forest_risk <- predict(
  forest_model,
  newdata = test_data,
  type = "prob"
)[, "pos"]

forest_roc <- roc(
  response = test_data$diabetes,
  predictor = forest_risk,
  levels = c("neg", "pos"),
  quiet = TRUE
)

forest_auc <- as.numeric(auc(forest_roc))
forest_brier <- mean((forest_risk - as.numeric(test_data$diabetes == "pos"))^2)

cat("\nRandom forest test AUC:\n")
print(forest_auc)

cat("\nRandom forest Brier score:\n")
print(forest_brier)

importance_table <- importance(forest_model) %>%
  as.data.frame() %>%
  rownames_to_column("predictor") %>%
  arrange(desc(MeanDecreaseGini))

cat("\nVariable importance table:\n")
print(importance_table)

varImpPlot(
  forest_model,
  main = "Random Forest Variable Importance"
)

glucose_partial <- partial(
  object = forest_model,
  pred.var = "glucose",
  train = train_data,
  prob = TRUE,
  which.class = "pos"
)

mass_partial <- partial(
  object = forest_model,
  pred.var = "mass",
  train = train_data,
  prob = TRUE,
  which.class = "pos"
)

age_partial <- partial(
  object = forest_model,
  pred.var = "age",
  train = train_data,
  prob = TRUE,
  which.class = "pos"
)

plotPartial(
  glucose_partial,
  main = "Partial Dependence of Diabetes Risk on Glucose",
  xlab = "Glucose",
  ylab = "Average predicted probability"
)

plotPartial(
  mass_partial,
  main = "Partial Dependence of Diabetes Risk on BMI / Mass",
  xlab = "BMI / Mass",
  ylab = "Average predicted probability"
)

plotPartial(
  age_partial,
  main = "Partial Dependence of Diabetes Risk on Age",
  xlab = "Age",
  ylab = "Average predicted probability"
)

two_way_partial <- partial(
  object = forest_model,
  pred.var = c("glucose", "mass"),
  train = train_data,
  prob = TRUE,
  which.class = "pos"
)

plotPartial(
  two_way_partial,
  levelplot = TRUE,
  main = "Two-Way Partial Dependence: Glucose and BMI"
)

forest_predictions <- test_data %>%
  mutate(
    predicted_risk = forest_risk,
    observed_numeric = as.numeric(diabetes == "pos")
  )

calibration_table <- forest_predictions %>%
  mutate(risk_group = ntile(predicted_risk, 5)) %>%
  group_by(risk_group) %>%
  summarise(
    mean_predicted_risk = mean(predicted_risk),
    observed_risk = mean(observed_numeric),
    n = n(),
    .groups = "drop"
  )

cat("\nGrouped calibration summary:\n")
print(calibration_table)

plot(
  calibration_table$mean_predicted_risk,
  calibration_table$observed_risk,
  xlim = c(0, 1),
  ylim = c(0, 1),
  xlab = "Mean predicted risk",
  ylab = "Observed risk",
  main = "Grouped Calibration: Random Forest"
)

abline(0, 1, lty = 2)

cat("\nInterpretation prompts:\n")
cat("1. Which predictors appear important for prediction?\n")
cat("2. Are important predictors clinically plausible?\n")
cat("3. Could correlated predictors distort importance rankings?\n")
cat("4. Do partial dependence plots show clinically plausible risk patterns?\n")
cat("5. Are the displayed predictor values realistic combinations of patients?\n")
cat("6. Is the model calibrated well enough for risk-based decisions?\n")

cat("\nLearning points:\n")
cat("1. Variable importance describes model use of predictors, not causality.\n")
cat("2. Partial dependence describes average model behaviour, not individual patient biology.\n")
cat("3. Correlated predictors can make importance and partial dependence misleading.\n")
cat("4. Interpretation should be checked against clinical knowledge and validation data.\n")
cat("5. Complex model interpretation should not replace calibration and clinical usefulness assessment.\n")

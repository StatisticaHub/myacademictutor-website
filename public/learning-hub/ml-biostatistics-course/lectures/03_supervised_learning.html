Today’s big question

What is supervised learning, and how do we use it correctly with medical data?

Supervised learning means learning a relationship between predictors and an observed outcome.

The data usually look like this:

Patient characteristics → observed outcome

The model uses past examples to learn patterns that may help predict outcomes for future patients.

Intuition first

Think of supervised learning like clinical teaching.

A trainee clinician learns from previous cases:

  • symptoms;
  • test results;
  • diagnosis;
  • treatment history;
  • outcome.

Over time, the trainee starts recognising patterns.

A supervised learning model does something similar, but using data.

Supervised learning uses examples with known outcomes to learn how predictors relate to the outcome.

Outcomes and predictors

Every supervised learning problem needs two main ingredients.

Term Meaning
Outcome What we want to predict
Predictors Variables used to make the prediction

For example:

Clinical task Outcome Predictors
Diabetes prediction Diabetes yes/no Glucose, BMI, age
Hospital readmission prediction Readmission yes/no Previous admissions, diagnosis, length of stay
Cancer recurrence prediction Recurrence yes/no Tumour stage, treatment, biomarkers
Survival prediction Time to event Age, tumour stage, molecular markers

The model learns from the relationship between predictors and outcome.

Mini conversation: can we use every variable?

Curious Learner:
“So should I include every variable in the dataset?”

Dr Clinic:
“Not necessarily. Some variables may not be available when the model is supposed to make the prediction.”

Leakage Monster:
“But post-outcome variables make the model look amazing.”

Prof Stat:
“That is exactly the problem. A predictor must be available at the prediction time point.”

In medical machine learning, predictor availability matters.

A model is not useful if it needs information that would not be available in real practice.

Prediction time point

The prediction time point answers:

When is the model supposed to make the prediction?

Examples:

At hospital admission
At diagnosis
Before treatment starts
At discharge
At first antenatal visit

Once the prediction time point is defined, only information available at or before that time should be used.

This protects the analysis from data leakage.

Medical example: diabetes prediction

Suppose we want to predict whether a patient has diabetes at clinical assessment.

Possible predictors:

  • glucose;
  • BMI;
  • age;
  • insulin;
  • blood pressure;
  • number of pregnancies.

Outcome:

Diabetes status: positive or negative

Prediction time point:

At clinical assessment

This is a supervised learning problem because we have both predictors and known diabetes status in the dataset.

Types of supervised learning problems

Supervised learning includes different types of prediction problems.

Regression

Used when the outcome is continuous.

Example:

Predict systolic blood pressure

Classification

Used when the outcome is categorical.

Example:

Predict diabetes positive or negative

Survival prediction

Used when the outcome is time-to-event.

Example:

Predict time to cancer recurrence

Medical data often involve all three types.

Binary classification

Binary classification is very common in medical prediction.

Examples:

  • disease yes/no;
  • hospital readmission yes/no;
  • treatment response yes/no;
  • recurrence yes/no;
  • ICU admission yes/no;
  • mortality yes/no.

The model usually estimates:

P(outcome = positive | predictors)

For example:

P(diabetes = positive | glucose, BMI, age) = 0.72

This means the model estimates a 72% probability that the patient belongs to the diabetes-positive group.

Common mistake

Common mistake: unclear outcome definition

A prediction model is only as clear as its outcome.

For example, “diabetes” could mean:

  • self-reported diabetes;
  • doctor-diagnosed diabetes;
  • medication use;
  • glucose threshold;
  • HbA1c threshold;
  • hospital record diagnosis.

These are not always equivalent.

If the outcome is unclear, the model interpretation becomes unclear.

Clinical reality check

Clinical reality check

Before fitting a supervised learning model, ask:

  • What exactly is the outcome?
  • What is the positive class?
  • When is the prediction made?
  • Are all predictors available at that time?
  • What decision could the prediction support?
  • What are the consequences of wrong predictions?

Prediction modelling is not just a technical exercise. It must match a real clinical question.

The supervised learning workflow

A simple supervised learning workflow is:

  1. Define the clinical question.
  2. Define the outcome.
  3. Define candidate predictors.
  4. Define the prediction time point.
  5. Split data into training and test sets.
  6. Fit the model on the training data.
  7. Evaluate performance on the test data.
  8. Interpret performance clinically.
  9. Check limitations.
  10. Consider external validation.

This workflow will be used throughout the course.

Mini conversation: model first or question first?

Curious Learner:
“Should I start by choosing the model?”

Prof Stat:
“No. Start with the clinical question.”

Dr Clinic:
“If the clinical question is unclear, the model cannot rescue the analysis.”

Prof Stat:
“Exactly. The method should follow the question, not the other way around.”

Key takeaway

Supervised learning in medical data starts with:

  • a clear outcome;
  • clinically sensible predictors;
  • a defined prediction time point;
  • proper model validation;
  • careful clinical interpretation.

Before fitting any model, ask:

What are we predicting?
For whom?
At what time?
Using what information?
For what decision?

Reflection question

Suppose you want to predict cancer recurrence after diagnosis.

What would be:

  1. the outcome;
  2. the predictors;
  3. the prediction time point;
  4. the clinical use of the prediction?
Show suggested answer The outcome could be recurrence within a defined time period, such as recurrence within 5 years. Predictors could include tumour stage, grade, treatment information available at diagnosis, age, biomarkers, and baseline clinical characteristics. The prediction time point might be at diagnosis or after surgery, depending on the intended use. The prediction could support follow-up planning, risk stratification, or treatment discussion, but it would need proper validation before clinical use.

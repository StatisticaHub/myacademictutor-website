Today’s big question

Why is accuracy not enough in medical prediction?

Accuracy is simple:

Accuracy = proportion of all patients correctly classified

But in medicine, different errors have different consequences.

Missing a true disease case is not the same as sending a healthy person for extra testing.

That is why medical classification models need more than accuracy.

Confusion matrix

For a binary outcome, predictions can be summarised using a confusion matrix.

  Actually positive Actually negative
Predicted positive True positive False positive
Predicted negative False negative True negative

This table is the foundation for many classification metrics.

Prof Stat:
“Yes. It separates correct positives, correct negatives, false positives, and false negatives.”

Dr Clinic:
“And those errors have different clinical consequences.”

Prof Stat:

Accuracy

Accuracy answers:

What proportion of all patients did the model classify correctly?

It is calculated as:

Accuracy = (true positives + true negatives) / all patients

Accuracy can be useful, but it can be misleading when the outcome is imbalanced.

For example, if only 5% of patients have a disease, a model could predict everyone as disease-negative and still achieve 95% accuracy.

That model would be clinically poor for detecting disease.

Sensitivity

Sensitivity answers:

Among patients who truly have the disease, how many did the model detect?

Sensitivity = true positives / all actual positives

High sensitivity is important when missing disease is harmful.

Examples:

  • screening for cancer;
  • detecting sepsis;
  • identifying high-risk pregnancy;
  • flagging patients at risk of deterioration;
  • screening for diabetes.

Specificity

Specificity answers:

Among patients who truly do not have the disease, how many did the model correctly identify?

Specificity = true negatives / all actual negatives

High specificity is important when false positives cause harm, anxiety, or unnecessary cost.

Examples:

  • avoiding unnecessary invasive testing;
  • reducing false alarms;
  • preventing overtreatment;
  • avoiding unnecessary referrals;
  • reducing patient anxiety.

PPV and NPV

Positive predictive value answers:

Among patients predicted positive, how many are truly positive?

Negative predictive value answers:

Among patients predicted negative, how many are truly negative?

These values depend strongly on disease prevalence.

A model may have the same sensitivity and specificity but different PPV in different populations.

This matters in medical practice because the same model can feel more or less useful depending on how common the disease is in the target population.

ROC curve and AUC

The ROC curve shows how sensitivity and specificity change across thresholds.

AUC measures discrimination.

Discrimination asks:

Does the model assign higher predicted risk to patients with the outcome than to patients without the outcome?

A higher AUC means better ranking ability.

But AUC does not tell us everything.

AUC tells us about ranking, not whether predicted probabilities are accurate.

Enter Ms Calibration

Ms Calibration:
“AUC tells us whether the model ranks patients well. But I care about whether the predicted risks are believable.”

Curious Learner:
“What does believable mean?”

Ms Calibration:
“If the model says 30% risk, then around 30% of similar patients should actually have the outcome.”

Prof Stat:
“That is calibration.”

Calibration asks:

Do predicted probabilities agree with observed risks?

For example, if 100 patients are given an average predicted risk of 30%, then about 30 of them should have the outcome if the model is well calibrated.

Discrimination versus calibration

Discrimination and calibration are different.

Concept Question
Discrimination Can the model rank high-risk patients above low-risk patients?
Calibration Are the predicted probabilities close to observed risks?

A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration.

For clinical decision-making, both matter.

Common mistake 1

Common mistake: thinking high accuracy means clinical usefulness

High accuracy can be misleading when the disease is rare or when one class dominates.

A model may be accurate overall but still fail to detect the patients who need attention.

Always check sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, ROC AUC, and calibration.

Common mistake 2

Common mistake: thinking high AUC means good risk prediction

A model can have a high AUC but still give risk estimates that are too high or too low.

This matters because clinical decisions are often based on absolute risk.

For example, if a patient is told they have 40% risk, that number should be meaningful.

Threshold choice

A threshold converts predicted probabilities into predicted classes.

For example:

Predicted risk ≥ 0.50 → positive
Predicted risk < 0.50 → negative

But threshold choice should depend on clinical context.

For screening, we may choose a lower threshold to increase sensitivity.

For confirmatory decisions, we may choose a higher threshold to increase specificity.

Clinical reality check

Clinical reality check

In medicine, model errors have consequences.

A false negative may mean:

  • missed diagnosis;
  • delayed treatment;
  • unsafe reassurance.

A false positive may mean:

  • unnecessary testing;
  • anxiety;
  • cost;
  • overtreatment.

A poorly calibrated risk may mean:

  • misleading risk communication;
  • wrong treatment threshold;
  • poor shared decision-making.

This is why classification metrics must be interpreted together.

Which metric should we report?

There is no single best metric for every situation.

A transparent report should usually include:

  • outcome definition;
  • positive class;
  • threshold used;
  • confusion matrix;
  • sensitivity;
  • specificity;
  • PPV;
  • NPV;
  • ROC AUC;
  • calibration assessment;
  • validation method.

The final interpretation should connect the numbers to the clinical decision.

Key takeaway

A medical classification model should not be judged only by accuracy.

A proper evaluation should consider:

  • confusion matrix;
  • sensitivity;
  • specificity;
  • PPV;
  • NPV;
  • ROC AUC;
  • calibration;
  • threshold choice;
  • clinical consequences.

A model is useful only if its performance supports the intended medical decision.

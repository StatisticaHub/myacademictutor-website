Lecture 9: Decision Curve Analysis

Opening scene

Scene: The model meets the decision

Curious Learner:
“My model has good AUC and decent calibration. Is that enough?”

Dr Clinic:
“Maybe not. What decision will the model actually support?”

Prof Stat:
“That is the key question. A model should improve decisions, not just metrics.”

Confusion Bug:
“I thought good AUC automatically means clinical usefulness.”

Prof Stat:
“Not always. That is why decision curve analysis can be useful.”

Today’s big question

Does the model help make better clinical decisions?

Decision curve analysis is used to evaluate the clinical usefulness of a prediction model.

It asks whether using the model gives more benefit than simple alternatives such as:

Treat everyone
Treat no one

Intuition first

A model is clinically useful only if it changes decisions in a helpful way.

For example:

  • who should receive further testing?
  • who should be referred?
  • who should receive treatment?
  • who should be monitored more closely?

Good statistical performance does not automatically mean good clinical usefulness.

Threshold probability

Decision curve analysis uses the idea of a threshold probability.

A threshold probability is the risk level at which action would be taken.

For example:

If predicted risk ≥ 20%, refer for further testing

Here, 20% is the threshold probability.

Different clinical settings may use different thresholds.

Mini conversation

Curious Learner:
“Why does the threshold matter?”

Dr Clinic:
“Because action depends on risk. I may act at 5% risk for a harmless test, but not for a risky treatment.”

Prof Stat:
“Exactly. The threshold reflects the trade-off between false positives and false negatives.”

Net benefit

Decision curve analysis uses a measure called net benefit.

Net benefit combines:

  • true positives;
  • false positives;
  • threshold probability.

The idea is to reward correct identification of patients who need action, while penalising unnecessary action in patients who do not need it.

Medical example

Suppose a model predicts diabetes risk.

A clinic may decide:

Refer for further testing if predicted risk ≥ 20%

Decision curve analysis asks:

  • Is using the model better than testing everyone?
  • Is using the model better than testing no one?
  • Across which risk thresholds is the model useful?

Treat all versus treat none

Decision curves often compare three strategies:

Strategy Meaning
Treat all Everyone receives the action
Treat none Nobody receives the action
Model-based Action depends on predicted risk

A model is useful if it gives higher net benefit than both treat-all and treat-none across relevant thresholds.

Common mistake

Common mistake: assuming strong AUC means clinical usefulness

A model can discriminate well but still not improve clinical decisions.

Clinical usefulness depends on the decision, threshold, harms, benefits, and target population.

Clinical reality check

Clinical reality check

A prediction model should support a real decision.

Before using decision curve analysis, ask:

  • What action will be taken?
  • What threshold risk would trigger action?
  • What are the harms of false positives?
  • What are the harms of false negatives?
  • Is the model useful across realistic thresholds?

Key takeaway

Decision curve analysis connects prediction modelling with clinical decision-making.

It asks whether using the model improves decisions compared with simple alternatives.

Reflection question

Why might a model with good AUC and calibration still have limited clinical usefulness?

Show suggested answer Because it may not improve decisions at clinically relevant thresholds. A model must be linked to a real action, and the benefits of correct decisions must outweigh the harms of false positives and false negatives.

Lecture 8: Calibration and Brier Score

Opening scene

Scene: The risk prediction clinic

Curious Learner:
“My model has a good AUC. So the predicted risks are reliable, right?”

Ms Calibration:
“Not necessarily. AUC tells you about ranking, not whether the probabilities are accurate.”

Dr Clinic:
“If I tell a patient they have 40% risk, that number must mean something.”

Prof Stat:
“That is why calibration matters.”

Today’s big question

Do predicted probabilities agree with observed risks?

Calibration is about whether predicted probabilities are believable.

If a model says a group of patients has 30% risk, then about 30% of those patients should experience the outcome.

Intuition first

Imagine a weather forecast says:

Chance of rain = 80%

If it rains only 20% of the time when the forecast says 80%, the forecast is poorly calibrated.

Medical risk prediction works similarly.

Calibration asks whether predicted risks match observed outcome frequencies.

Discrimination versus calibration

Discrimination and calibration are different.

Concept Question
Discrimination Can the model rank high-risk patients above low-risk patients?
Calibration Are the predicted probabilities close to observed risks?

A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration.

Mini conversation

Curious Learner:
“So high AUC does not guarantee good calibration?”

Ms Calibration:
“Exactly. A model may rank patients correctly but still overestimate or underestimate risk.”

Dr Clinic:
“And if risk estimates are wrong, decisions may be wrong.”

Calibration plot

A calibration plot compares:

Predicted risk

against:

Observed risk

A well-calibrated model should have points close to the diagonal line.

If points are above the diagonal, observed risk is higher than predicted risk. The model may be underestimating risk.

If points are below the diagonal, observed risk is lower than predicted risk. The model may be overestimating risk.

Medical example

Suppose a diabetes model predicts that a group of patients has average risk:

Predicted risk = 40%

If the observed diabetes rate in that group is close to 40%, calibration is good.

If the observed rate is 65%, the model is underestimating risk.

If the observed rate is 20%, the model is overestimating risk.

Brier score

The Brier score measures the average squared difference between predicted probabilities and observed outcomes.

For binary outcomes:

Brier score = mean((predicted probability - observed outcome)^2)

where observed outcome is:

1 = event occurred
0 = event did not occur

Lower Brier scores are better.

Common mistake

Common mistake: reporting AUC but ignoring calibration

AUC is useful, but it does not tell us whether predicted probabilities are correct.

For clinical decision-making, calibration is essential because decisions are often based on absolute risk.

Clinical reality check

Clinical reality check

If a model overestimates risk, patients may receive unnecessary testing or treatment.

If a model underestimates risk, high-risk patients may be missed.

Poor calibration can affect patient communication, clinical decisions, and resource use.

Key takeaway

Calibration is central to clinical prediction modelling.

A model should not only rank patients well. It should also give predicted risks that match observed risks.

Today’s big question

How can we build prediction models when there are many predictors?

In biomedical data, we often have many possible predictors.

Examples include:

  • clinical variables;
  • lab measurements;
  • imaging features;
  • genetic markers;
  • methylation sites;
  • gene expression features.

When the number of predictors is large, overfitting becomes a major risk.

Intuition first

Imagine trying to explain a small group of patients using hundreds of variables.

The model may find patterns that are not real.

It may learn noise instead of signal.

Regularisation helps control model complexity.

Regularisation reduces overfitting by discouraging overly complex models.

What is regularisation?

Regularisation adds a penalty to the model-fitting process.

Instead of only trying to fit the data as closely as possible, the model is also encouraged to keep coefficients smaller or simpler.

This can improve performance on new data.

Ridge regression

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients towards zero.

It usually keeps all predictors in the model, but reduces their influence.

This can be useful when many predictors are correlated.

Example:

Many biomarkers are weakly related to the outcome

Ridge can stabilise the model.

Lasso regression

Lasso regression can shrink some coefficients exactly to zero.

This means it can perform variable selection.

Example:

Only a subset of biomarkers may be selected

This can make the model simpler, but selection can be unstable if predictors are highly correlated.

Elastic net

Elastic net combines ridge and lasso.

It can shrink coefficients and select variables.

It is often useful when there are many correlated predictors, such as omics features.

Feature selection versus clinical reasoning

Feature selection can be statistical, clinical, or both.

Purely data-driven selection may choose variables that do not generalise.

Purely clinical selection may miss useful patterns.

A good modelling strategy should consider:

  • clinical relevance;
  • predictor availability;
  • measurement quality;
  • missingness;
  • correlation between predictors;
  • sample size;
  • validation performance.

Common mistake

Common mistake: selecting features before validation

If feature selection is performed before cross-validation or train/test splitting, the validation process becomes biased.

Feature selection must be included inside the resampling or validation workflow.

Otherwise, performance estimates may be too optimistic.

Medical example

Suppose we use methylation data to predict an exposure or disease outcome.

There may be hundreds of thousands of methylation sites.

A standard logistic regression model cannot handle all of them directly with a small sample size.

Regularisation methods such as lasso, ridge, or elastic net can help build more stable prediction models.

But careful validation is essential.

Clinical reality check

Clinical reality check

A selected predictor is not automatically clinically important.

It may be selected because it improves prediction in one dataset.

Before interpreting selected features strongly, consider:

  • biological plausibility;
  • stability across resamples;
  • external validation;
  • measurement reliability;
  • whether the predictor is available in practice.

Key takeaway

Regularisation helps reduce overfitting when there are many predictors.

Ridge shrinks coefficients.

Lasso can select predictors.

Elastic net combines both ideas.

But feature selection must be validated carefully and interpreted cautiously.

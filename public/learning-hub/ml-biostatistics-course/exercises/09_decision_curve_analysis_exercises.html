Exercise 9: Decision Curve Analysis

Learning aim

This exercise helps you understand why good statistical performance does not automatically mean clinical usefulness.

By the end of this exercise, you should be able to explain:

  • what decision curve analysis is used for;
  • what threshold probability means;
  • why “treat all” and “treat none” are useful comparisons;
  • why AUC and calibration are not enough by themselves;
  • how decision curve analysis connects prediction models to clinical decisions.

Question 1: Concept check

What is the main purpose of decision curve analysis?

Show suggested answer Decision curve analysis evaluates whether using a prediction model improves clinical decision-making compared with simple strategies such as treating everyone or treating no one. It focuses on clinical usefulness, not only statistical performance.

Question 2: Threshold probability

What is a threshold probability?

Show suggested answer A threshold probability is the risk level at which a clinical action would be taken. For example: ```text Refer for further testing if predicted risk ≥ 20% ``` Here, 20% is the threshold probability.

Question 3: Clinical example

A diabetes risk model is used to decide whether patients should receive further testing.

The clinic chooses:

Refer if predicted risk ≥ 20%

What does the 20% represent?

Show suggested answer The 20% represents the threshold probability. It is the risk level at which the clinic believes the expected benefit of further testing outweighs the possible harms, cost, inconvenience, or anxiety.

Question 4: Treat all versus treat none

Why are “treat all” and “treat none” included in decision curve analysis?

Show suggested answer They are simple comparison strategies. A prediction model is clinically useful only if it gives greater net benefit than simple alternatives. The model should improve decision-making compared with: - giving the action to everyone; - giving the action to nobody.

Question 5: Interpretation

Why might a model with good AUC and calibration still have limited clinical usefulness?

Show suggested answer A model with good AUC and calibration may still not improve decisions at clinically relevant thresholds. Clinical usefulness depends on: - the action being considered; - the threshold probability; - harms of false positives; - harms of false negatives; - availability of simpler alternatives; - whether the model changes management in a helpful way.

Question 6: Screening context

For a screening test with low cost and low harm, would the threshold probability usually be low or high?

Show suggested answer The threshold probability may be relatively low. If the screening action is safe, cheap, and easy, clinicians may be willing to act even at lower predicted risk. This is especially true when missing true cases is harmful.

Question 7: Treatment context

For a treatment with serious side effects, would the threshold probability usually be low or high?

Show suggested answer The threshold probability may be higher. If treatment has serious side effects, clinicians may require stronger evidence of risk before recommending treatment. This avoids unnecessary harm in false-positive patients.

Question 8: Medical interpretation

A model has high net benefit only between threshold probabilities of 10% and 30%.

What should you consider before recommending the model?

Show suggested answer You should consider whether 10% to 30% is a clinically realistic threshold range for the decision. If clinicians would only act at much higher or much lower thresholds, the model may not be useful in practice. Decision curve analysis must be interpreted in the context of the actual clinical decision.

Reflection

Think of a screening model. What are the possible harms of false positives and false negatives?

Show reflection answer False positives may lead to unnecessary testing, anxiety, cost, and possible over-treatment. False negatives may lead to missed disease, delayed diagnosis, delayed treatment, and unsafe reassurance. The relative importance of these harms depends on the disease, the test, and the clinical decision.

Exercise 11: Ridge Regression

Aim

This exercise helps you understand why ridge regression is useful in medical prediction modelling, especially when ordinary regression may overfit or become unstable.

You will practise thinking about:

  • coefficient shrinkage;
  • overfitting;
  • correlated predictors;
  • the role of lambda;
  • why ridge is not mainly a feature selection method;
  • how ridge should be evaluated in clinical prediction.

Part G: Short written answer

Question 17

Write a short paragraph explaining ridge regression to a clinician.

Your explanation should avoid heavy mathematics and focus on why ridge may make a prediction model more stable.

Suggested answers

Answer 1

Ridge regression is a regularisation method that shrinks regression coefficients towards zero. This helps reduce overfitting by preventing the model from relying too strongly on patterns that may be specific to the training data. It can make predictions more stable in new patients.

Answer 2

B. Ridge regression usually keeps all predictors but shrinks their coefficients.

Answer 3

The model may be unstable because there are relatively few outcome events compared with the number of candidate predictors. With only 28 events and 24 predictors, the model may fit random patterns in the data. This can produce large or unstable coefficients and poor performance in new patients.

Answer 4

Ridge regression is not mainly a feature selection method. It usually keeps predictors in the model and shrinks their coefficients. Lasso is more commonly used when the aim is to set some coefficients to zero and select a smaller predictor set.

Answer 5

  1. The coefficients were shrunk towards zero.
  2. No predictors were removed.
  3. Smaller coefficients may reduce overfitting and make predictions less extreme, which may improve performance in new patients.

Answer 6

A model that fits the training data very well may have learned random noise. A model that generalises well performs well on new patients who were not used to build the model. In medical prediction, generalisation is more important because the model will be used on future patients, not only the development dataset.

Answer 7

When predictors are strongly correlated, ordinary regression may struggle to separate their individual effects. This can produce unstable coefficients, where one variable receives a large coefficient and another correlated variable receives a small or unexpected coefficient.

Answer 8

Ridge regression can shrink correlated predictors and make the model less sensitive to small changes in the dataset. This can produce more stable predictions.

Answer 9

No, not necessarily. If all 10 biomarkers contain small amounts of predictive information, ridge may allow them to contribute while controlling their coefficient sizes. However, the model may be less simple to interpret than a smaller selected model.

Answer 10

B. The amount of coefficient shrinkage.

Answer 11

Small lambda  → less shrinkage
Large lambda  → more shrinkage

Answer 12

If lambda is too small, the model may still overfit. If lambda is too large, the coefficients may be shrunk too strongly and the model may underfit.

Answer 13

If the final test set is used repeatedly to choose lambda, it becomes part of model development. The reported test performance may then be too optimistic.

Answer 14

Ridge may be appropriate because several predictors are correlated and because the model includes several candidate predictors. Ridge can shrink coefficients and make predictions more stable.

Answer 15

Ridge will not automatically solve data leakage, poor outcome definition, missing important predictors, poor calibration, poor data quality, or lack of external validation.

Answer 16

AUC only measures discrimination. The team should also assess calibration, Brier score, calibration slope/intercept, clinical usefulness, and external validation if possible.

Answer 17

Ridge regression is a way of making a prediction model less overconfident. It keeps the clinical predictors in the model but reduces very large coefficient values. This can help when predictors overlap or when the dataset is small, because the model becomes less sensitive to random patterns in the training data.

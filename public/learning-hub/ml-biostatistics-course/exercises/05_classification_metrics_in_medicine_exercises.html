Exercise 2: Calculate classification metrics

A model gives the following results:

True positives = 40
False positives = 20
True negatives = 120
False negatives = 20

Calculate:

  1. Accuracy
  2. Sensitivity
  3. Specificity
  4. Positive predictive value
  5. Negative predictive value
Show answer Total: ```text 40 + 20 + 120 + 20 = 200 ``` Accuracy: ```text (TP + TN) / Total = (40 + 120) / 200 = 0.80 ``` Sensitivity: ```text TP / (TP + FN) = 40 / (40 + 20) = 0.667 ``` Specificity: ```text TN / (TN + FP) = 120 / (120 + 20) = 0.857 ``` Positive predictive value: ```text TP / (TP + FP) = 40 / (40 + 20) = 0.667 ``` Negative predictive value: ```text TN / (TN + FN) = 120 / (120 + 20) = 0.857 ```

Exercise 3: Accuracy and rare disease

A disease has prevalence 1%.

A model predicts every patient as disease negative.

Answer:

  1. What is the accuracy?
  2. What is the sensitivity?
  3. Why is this model not clinically useful for screening?
Show answer The accuracy is 99%, because 99% of patients do not have the disease. The sensitivity is 0%, because the model identifies none of the true disease cases. The model is not useful for screening because it misses every patient with the disease.

Exercise 5: PPV and prevalence

Two populations are tested with the same model.

The model has the same sensitivity and specificity in both populations.

Population A prevalence = 20%
Population B prevalence = 2%

Question:

In which population will PPV usually be higher, and why?

Show answer PPV will usually be higher in Population A because the outcome is more common. When prevalence is low, many positive predictions may be false positives, even if sensitivity and specificity are reasonable.

Exercise 6: NPV and rare disease

A model has very high NPV in a rare disease setting.

Question:

Does this automatically mean the model is excellent? Explain.

Show answer No. NPV can be high simply because the disease is rare. If most people do not have the disease, many negative predictions will be correct even for a weak model. NPV should be interpreted alongside prevalence, sensitivity, specificity, PPV, and clinical purpose.

Exercise 7: Threshold trade-off

A model predicts probability of disease.

At threshold 0.20:

Sensitivity = 0.92
Specificity = 0.55

At threshold 0.70:

Sensitivity = 0.50
Specificity = 0.94

Answer:

  1. Which threshold may be preferred for screening?
  2. Which threshold may be preferred before giving a risky treatment?
  3. Why?
Show answer For screening, threshold 0.20 may be preferred because it gives higher sensitivity and misses fewer true cases. Before giving a risky treatment, threshold 0.70 may be preferred because it gives higher specificity and reduces false positives. The preferred threshold depends on the consequences of false positives and false negatives.

Exercise 8: AUC interpretation

A model has:

AUC = 0.84

Answer:

  1. What does this tell us?
  2. Does it tell us whether predicted probabilities are well calibrated?
  3. Does it tell us which threshold should be used?
Show answer An AUC of 0.84 suggests good discrimination. The model is reasonably good at ranking patients who experience the outcome above those who do not. AUC does not tell us whether predicted probabilities are calibrated. AUC also does not tell us which threshold should be used. Threshold choice depends on clinical context.

Exercise 9: Calibration problem

A model predicts 40% risk for a group of patients.

The observed event rate in that group is 15%.

Answer:

  1. Is the model well calibrated?
  2. Is it overestimating or underestimating risk?
  3. Why could this be harmful?
Show answer The model is not well calibrated in this group. It is overestimating risk because predicted risk is 40%, but observed risk is 15%. This could lead to unnecessary treatment, monitoring, anxiety, or inefficient use of healthcare resources.

Exercise 10: Breast cancer recurrence example

A model predicts 5-year breast cancer recurrence.

Answer:

  1. What is the positive class?
  2. What would sensitivity mean?
  3. What would specificity mean?
  4. Why might ordinary binary classification metrics be incomplete if censoring is present?
Show answer The positive class is recurrence within 5 years. Sensitivity means: among patients who truly recur within 5 years, how many are classified as high risk. Specificity means: among patients who do not recur within 5 years, how many are classified as low risk. If censoring is present, some patients may not have known 5-year recurrence status. In that case, survival-specific metrics such as C-index, time-dependent AUC, and time-dependent Brier score may be more appropriate.

Exercise 11: Methylation classification example

Suppose a model uses DNA methylation markers to classify disease status.

n = 600 participants
p = 450,000 CpG sites

Answer:

  1. Why might accuracy be misleading?
  2. Why is feature-selection leakage a risk?
  3. Which metrics would you report?
Show answer Accuracy may be misleading if the disease classes are imbalanced. Feature-selection leakage is a risk because selecting CpG sites using the full dataset before cross-validation allows outcome information from the test folds to influence model development. Useful metrics include sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, ROC AUC, calibration if predicted probabilities are used, and external validation where possible.

Exercise 14: Mini case study

A hospital develops a model to identify patients at risk of sepsis.

At threshold 0.30:

Sensitivity = 0.95
Specificity = 0.50

At threshold 0.70:

Sensitivity = 0.60
Specificity = 0.90

The clinical team wants to use the model as an early warning alert.

Answer:

  1. Which threshold seems more appropriate?
  2. What is the trade-off?
  3. What additional information would you want before making a final decision?
Show answer Threshold 0.30 may be more appropriate because the model is being used as an early warning alert, where missing sepsis cases could be dangerous. The trade-off is that specificity is lower, meaning more false positives and more alerts for patients who do not develop sepsis. Additional information needed includes alert burden, clinician workload, harms of false alarms, PPV, calibration, time-to-alert, clinical workflow, and external validation.

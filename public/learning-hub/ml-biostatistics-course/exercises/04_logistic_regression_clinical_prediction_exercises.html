Exercise 3: Interpreting coefficients

A logistic regression model gives the following coefficient for age:

β_age = 0.05

Answer:

  1. Is age associated with higher or lower odds of the outcome?
  2. What is the odds ratio?
  3. Interpret the odds ratio.
Show answer The coefficient is positive, so higher age is associated with higher odds of the outcome. The odds ratio is: ```text exp(0.05) = 1.051 ``` This means each one-year increase in age is associated with approximately 5.1% higher odds of the outcome, holding other predictors constant.

Exercise 4: Interpreting an odds ratio

A logistic regression model for diabetes gives:

Odds ratio for BMI = 1.12

Answer:

  1. What does this mean?
  2. Does this mean the risk increases by 12%?
  3. Why should odds ratios be interpreted carefully?
Show answer An odds ratio of 1.12 means each one-unit increase in BMI is associated with 12% higher odds of diabetes, holding other variables constant. It does not necessarily mean the risk increases by 12%. Odds and risk are different, especially when the outcome is common. Odds ratios should be interpreted carefully because they can exaggerate the impression of effect size compared with risk ratios when outcomes are common.

Exercise 7: Breast cancer prediction

Suppose you want to predict whether a breast cancer patient will experience recurrence within 5 years.

Answer:

  1. What is the binary outcome?
  2. List five possible predictors.
  3. What is the prediction time point?
  4. Why might survival analysis sometimes be better than logistic regression?
Show answer The binary outcome is recurrence within 5 years: yes/no. Possible predictors include age at diagnosis, tumour size, tumour grade, lymph node status, receptor status, treatment, comorbidities, and baseline biomarkers. The prediction time point could be diagnosis or treatment initiation. Survival analysis may be better if patients have different follow-up times or censoring. Logistic regression ignores timing and may discard information when the outcome is time-to-event.

Exercise 8: Methylation prediction

Suppose you want to predict disease status using DNA methylation data.

You have:

n = 500 participants
p = 450,000 CpG sites

Answer:

  1. Why is ordinary logistic regression problematic?
  2. What type of logistic regression may be more suitable?
  3. What validation issue is especially important?
Show answer Ordinary logistic regression is problematic because the number of predictors is much larger than the number of participants. This creates severe overfitting risk and may make the model impossible to estimate. Penalised logistic regression, such as lasso or elastic net, may be more suitable. Feature selection and preprocessing must be done inside resampling to avoid data leakage.

Exercise 9: Calibration interpretation

A logistic regression model predicts 40% risk for a group of patients.

Observed event rate in this group is 20%.

Answer:

  1. Is the model well calibrated in this group?
  2. Is it overestimating or underestimating risk?
  3. Why could this be clinically harmful?
Show answer The model is not well calibrated in this group. It is overestimating risk because predicted risk is 40% but observed risk is 20%. This could lead to unnecessary treatment, unnecessary monitoring, patient anxiety, or inefficient use of clinical resources.

Exercise 10: Separation

A dataset contains 80 patients.

All 10 patients with a particular biomarker value experienced the outcome. None of the 70 patients without that biomarker value experienced the outcome.

Answer:

  1. What problem might occur in logistic regression?
  2. Why is this a problem?
  3. What could be done?
Show answer This is a separation problem. The predictor perfectly separates the outcome groups, which can lead to unstable or infinite coefficient estimates. Possible solutions include penalised logistic regression, Firth correction, reducing model complexity, or collecting more data.

Exercise 12: Mini case study

A researcher builds a logistic regression model to predict 1-year mortality after cancer diagnosis.

They include:

Age at diagnosis
Tumour stage
Tumour grade
Treatment type
Baseline biomarker level
Recurrence status during follow-up
End-of-life care referral

Answer:

  1. Which predictors are appropriate?
  2. Which predictors are leakage risks?
  3. Why are they leakage risks?
  4. How would you improve the predictor list?
Show answer Appropriate predictors may include age at diagnosis, tumour stage, tumour grade, treatment type if known at prediction time, and baseline biomarker level. Leakage risks include recurrence status during follow-up and end-of-life care referral. These occur after baseline and are closely related to the outcome. They are leakage risks because they would not be available when predicting 1-year mortality at diagnosis. The predictor list should include only variables available at the defined prediction time point. The model should clearly state whether prediction is made at diagnosis, after treatment planning, or at another time.

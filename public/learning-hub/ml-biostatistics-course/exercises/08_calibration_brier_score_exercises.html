Exercise 8: Calibration and Brier Score

Learning aim

This exercise helps you understand calibration, predicted probabilities, and the Brier score.

By the end of this exercise, you should be able to explain:

  • what calibration measures;
  • why AUC does not assess calibration;
  • how to interpret a calibration plot;
  • what the Brier score measures;
  • why calibration matters for clinical risk communication.

Question 1: Concept check

What does calibration measure?

Show suggested answer Calibration measures whether predicted probabilities agree with observed risks. For example, if a group of patients has an average predicted risk of 30%, then approximately 30% of those patients should experience the outcome if the model is well calibrated.

Question 2: Discrimination versus calibration

A model has a high AUC but poor calibration.

What does this mean?

Show suggested answer This means the model may rank patients well but give unreliable predicted probabilities. A high AUC means the model can often assign higher risk to patients with the outcome than to patients without the outcome. Poor calibration means the actual risk estimates may be too high or too low.

Question 3: Calibration plot

In a calibration plot, points are mostly above the diagonal line.

What does this suggest?

Show suggested answer If points are above the diagonal line, observed risk is higher than predicted risk. This suggests that the model may be underestimating risk. For example, if the model predicts 30% risk but observed risk is 50%, the model is predicting too low.

Question 4: Calibration plot

In a calibration plot, points are mostly below the diagonal line.

What does this suggest?

Show suggested answer If points are below the diagonal line, observed risk is lower than predicted risk. This suggests that the model may be overestimating risk. For example, if the model predicts 60% risk but observed risk is 35%, the model is predicting too high.

Question 5: Brier score

For a binary outcome, what values are used for the observed outcome in the Brier score?

Show suggested answer The observed outcome is usually coded as: ```text 1 = event occurred 0 = event did not occur ``` The Brier score is the mean squared difference between the predicted probability and the observed outcome.

Question 6: Simple calculation

A patient has:

Predicted risk = 0.80
Observed outcome = 1

What is the squared error contribution to the Brier score?

Show suggested answer The squared error is: ```text (0.80 - 1)^2 = (-0.20)^2 = 0.04 ``` So this patient contributes: ```text 0.04 ``` to the Brier score.

Question 7: Simple calculation

A patient has:

Predicted risk = 0.80
Observed outcome = 0

What is the squared error contribution to the Brier score?

Show suggested answer The squared error is: ```text (0.80 - 0)^2 = 0.80^2 = 0.64 ``` So this patient contributes: ```text 0.64 ``` to the Brier score. This is much larger because the model predicted high risk but the event did not occur.

Question 8: Clinical interpretation

Why is calibration important when communicating risk to patients?

Show suggested answer Calibration is important because predicted probabilities are often used for communication and decision-making. If a model tells a patient that their risk is 40%, that number should be meaningful. Poor calibration can lead to: - unnecessary anxiety; - false reassurance; - over-treatment; - under-treatment; - poor shared decision-making.

Reflection

Why might calibration be especially important for models used to guide treatment decisions?

Show reflection answer Treatment decisions often depend on absolute risk. For example, a treatment may be recommended if predicted risk is above a certain threshold. If the model overestimates risk, patients may receive unnecessary treatment. If the model underestimates risk, high-risk patients may miss beneficial treatment. Therefore, calibration is central when predicted probabilities guide clinical action.

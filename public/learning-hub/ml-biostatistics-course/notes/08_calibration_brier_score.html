Calibration and Brier Score in Medical Prediction Models

Learning objectives

By the end of this note, you should be able to:

  1. Explain what calibration means in clinical prediction modelling.
  2. Distinguish calibration from discrimination.
  3. Interpret calibration plots.
  4. Explain overestimation and underestimation of risk.
  5. Describe calibration intercept and calibration slope.
  6. Explain what the Brier score measures.
  7. Understand why calibration is important for clinical decisions.
  8. Recognise why a model with high AUC may still be clinically unreliable.

1. Why this note matters

Many medical prediction models produce predicted probabilities.

For example:

Predicted diabetes risk = 40%
Predicted cancer recurrence risk = 25%
Predicted hospital readmission risk = 18%

These probabilities are useful only if they are meaningful.

If a model says that a group of patients has a 40% risk, then approximately 40% of those patients should experience the outcome.

This is the idea of calibration.

The practical message of this note is:

A medical prediction model should not only rank patients correctly. It should also give predicted risks that agree with observed risks.

2. Discrimination versus calibration

Discrimination and calibration are different.

Discrimination asks:

Can the model separate patients with the outcome from patients without the outcome?

Calibration asks:

Do the predicted probabilities match observed risks?

AUC measures discrimination.

Calibration assesses whether predicted risks are numerically reliable.

A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration.

3. Example: good AUC but poor calibration

Suppose a diabetes model correctly gives higher predicted risks to most patients with diabetes than to patients without diabetes.

This may produce a good AUC.

However, suppose the model predicts:

Average predicted risk = 60%
Observed risk = 30%

The model ranks patients well, but it overestimates absolute risk.

This is poor calibration.

In clinical practice, this matters because a patient may be told they are at much higher risk than they really are.

4. What does calibration mean?

Calibration asks:

When the model predicts x% risk, is the observed risk close to x%?

For example:

  • among patients predicted around 10% risk, about 10% should have the outcome;
  • among patients predicted around 30% risk, about 30% should have the outcome;
  • among patients predicted around 70% risk, about 70% should have the outcome.

If this is approximately true across the risk range, the model is well calibrated.

5. Why calibration matters in medicine

Calibration matters because clinical decisions often depend on absolute risk.

For example:

Offer treatment if predicted risk is at least 20%.

If the model overestimates risk, patients may receive unnecessary treatment.

If the model underestimates risk, high-risk patients may not receive needed treatment.

Poor calibration can affect:

  • treatment decisions;
  • screening decisions;
  • referral decisions;
  • follow-up intensity;
  • patient counselling;
  • shared decision-making;
  • resource allocation.

6. Calibration plot

A calibration plot compares predicted risk with observed risk.

Usually:

x-axis: predicted risk
y-axis: observed risk

A perfectly calibrated model would follow the diagonal line.

Interpretation:

  • points close to the diagonal suggest good calibration;
  • points above the diagonal suggest the model is underestimating risk;
  • points below the diagonal suggest the model is overestimating risk.

7. Points above the diagonal

If points are above the diagonal:

Observed risk > predicted risk

This means the model predicted too low.

The model is underestimating risk.

Example:

Predicted risk = 30%
Observed risk = 50%

The model may give false reassurance.

8. Points below the diagonal

If points are below the diagonal:

Observed risk < predicted risk

This means the model predicted too high.

The model is overestimating risk.

Example:

Predicted risk = 60%
Observed risk = 35%

The model may cause unnecessary concern or intervention.

9. Grouped calibration

One simple way to create a calibration plot is to group patients by predicted risk.

For example, patients may be divided into ten groups based on predicted risk.

For each group, we calculate:

Mean predicted risk
Observed outcome proportion

These values are plotted against each other.

This approach is easy to understand but can be unstable when sample size is small.

10. Smooth calibration curves

Instead of grouped calibration, a smooth calibration curve can be fitted.

This can show patterns across the range of predicted risks.

Smooth curves may be more informative than grouped points, but they also require careful interpretation.

In small datasets, calibration curves may be noisy.

11. Calibration intercept

The calibration intercept measures whether predictions are systematically too high or too low.

An ideal calibration intercept is:

0

If the intercept is greater than 0, the model may be underestimating risk overall.

If the intercept is less than 0, the model may be overestimating risk overall.

The exact interpretation can depend on how the calibration model is specified, but the ideal value is usually 0.

12. Calibration slope

The calibration slope measures whether predictions are too extreme or not extreme enough.

An ideal calibration slope is:

1

A slope less than 1 often suggests overfitting.

This means:

  • high risks are predicted too high;
  • low risks are predicted too low.

A slope greater than 1 can suggest that predictions are not extreme enough.

13. Overfitting and calibration slope

Overfitted models often produce predictions that are too confident.

For example, the model may classify some patients as extremely high risk and others as extremely low risk.

In new data, these extremes may not be justified.

A calibration slope less than 1 warns that the model may be overfitted.

This is common when too many predictors are used for the available sample size.

14. Brier score

The Brier score measures the average squared difference between predicted probabilities and observed outcomes.

For a binary outcome:

Brier score = mean((predicted probability - observed outcome)^2)

where:

observed outcome = 1 if event occurred
observed outcome = 0 if event did not occur

Lower Brier scores are better.

15. Simple Brier score example

Suppose a patient has:

Predicted risk = 0.80
Observed outcome = 1

The squared error is:

(0.80 - 1)^2 = 0.04

This is a good prediction because the event occurred and the predicted risk was high.

Now suppose another patient has:

Predicted risk = 0.80
Observed outcome = 0

The squared error is:

(0.80 - 0)^2 = 0.64

This is a poor prediction because the model predicted high risk but the event did not occur.

16. Interpreting the Brier score

The Brier score captures overall probability prediction error.

It reflects aspects of:

  • discrimination;
  • calibration;
  • prediction accuracy.

However, the Brier score is not always intuitive by itself.

It should usually be interpreted together with:

  • AUC;
  • calibration plot;
  • calibration slope;
  • calibration intercept;
  • clinical context.

17. Calibration in external validation

Calibration often changes when a model is applied to a new population.

For example, a model developed in one hospital may overestimate risk in another hospital if the outcome is less common there.

This is why external validation should assess calibration, not only AUC.

A model may transport reasonably well in terms of ranking but require recalibration before use.

18. Recalibration

Recalibration means updating the model so that predicted risks better match observed risks in a new population.

This may involve adjusting:

  • intercept;
  • slope;
  • baseline risk;
  • model coefficients.

Recalibration should be done carefully and transparently.

A model should not be silently recalibrated without reporting what was changed.

19. Clinical example: diabetes risk

Suppose a diabetes model predicts that a group of patients has an average risk of 40%.

If the observed diabetes rate is 42%, the model is well calibrated in that group.

If the observed rate is 65%, the model is underestimating risk.

If the observed rate is 20%, the model is overestimating risk.

This matters because different decisions may be made depending on whether the predicted risk is 20%, 40%, or 65%.

20. Clinical example: cancer recurrence

Suppose a cancer recurrence model predicts a 5-year recurrence risk of 30%.

A clinician may use this estimate to discuss follow-up intensity or treatment options.

If the model is poorly calibrated, the patient may receive misleading information.

This could lead to overtreatment, undertreatment, unnecessary anxiety, or false reassurance.

21. Common mistakes

21.1 Reporting only AUC

AUC does not assess predicted probability accuracy.

21.2 Assuming high AUC means good calibration

A model can rank patients well but still overestimate or underestimate risk.

21.3 Ignoring calibration in external validation

Calibration may change across populations.

21.4 Using arbitrary risk thresholds without checking calibration

Threshold-based decisions require meaningful predicted risks.

21.5 Treating calibration as optional

For clinical prediction, calibration is usually essential.

22. Reporting calibration

A strong report should include:

  • calibration plot;
  • calibration intercept;
  • calibration slope;
  • Brier score;
  • AUC or other discrimination measure;
  • validation method;
  • explanation of clinical implications.

Calibration should be interpreted in the context of the intended use.

23. Summary

Calibration is central to medical prediction modelling because predicted probabilities are often used to guide decisions.

A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration. This means it may rank patients correctly but give risk estimates that are too high or too low.

The Brier score summarises probability prediction error, while calibration plots and calibration slope/intercept help diagnose how predicted risks behave.

In clinical applications, calibration is not a technical extra. It directly affects risk communication and decision-making.

Key terms

Term Meaning
Calibration Agreement between predicted probabilities and observed risks
Discrimination Ability to rank patients with the outcome above patients without the outcome
AUC Measure of discrimination for binary classification
Calibration plot Plot comparing predicted risk with observed risk
Underestimation Predicted risk is lower than observed risk
Overestimation Predicted risk is higher than observed risk
Calibration intercept Measure of systematic overprediction or underprediction
Calibration slope Measure of whether predictions are too extreme or not extreme enough
Brier score Mean squared error of predicted probabilities
Recalibration Updating model predictions to better match a new population

Key takeaway

A model with good AUC is not automatically clinically reliable. For medical prediction, predicted probabilities should be calibrated so that risk estimates are meaningful for patients, clinicians, and decision-making.

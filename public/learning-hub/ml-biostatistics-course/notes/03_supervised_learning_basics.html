Supervised Learning for Medical Data

Learning objectives

By the end of this note, you should be able to:

  1. Define supervised learning in the context of biostatistics.
  2. Identify outcomes and predictors in medical datasets.
  3. Distinguish regression, classification, and survival prediction problems.
  4. Explain why the prediction time point matters in medical machine learning.
  5. Understand train/test splitting and why test-set performance matters.
  6. Explain why logistic regression is often used as a baseline prediction model.
  7. Interpret the confusion matrix, sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, negative predictive value, ROC curve, and AUC.
  8. Understand why classification metrics must be interpreted in the clinical context.

1. Introduction

Supervised learning is one of the most important areas of machine learning for biostatistics and medical statistics. Many medical prediction problems are supervised learning problems because we usually have a dataset containing patient characteristics and known outcomes.

For example, we may have data on patients’ age, sex, blood pressure, glucose level, body mass index, treatment history, tumour stage, or biomarker values. We may also know whether each patient developed diabetes, experienced cancer recurrence, died within a certain time period, responded to treatment, or was readmitted to hospital.

The aim of supervised learning is to use the available predictors to learn a relationship with the outcome, and then use that learned relationship to make predictions for new patients.

In simple notation, we often write this as:

Y = f(X) + error

where:

  • Y is the outcome;
  • X represents the predictors or features;
  • f(X) is the function learned from the data;
  • error represents unexplained variation.

This notation appears simple, but it captures the core idea behind supervised learning. We use observed examples to learn a mapping between predictors and outcomes. The model is then assessed by how well it performs on new individuals, not simply by how well it fits the training dataset.

This distinction is especially important in biostatistics. In medicine, a model may appear impressive in the development dataset but fail when used in another hospital, another cohort, or another patient population. Therefore, supervised learning in medical statistics must always be connected to validation, calibration, clinical usefulness, and transparent reporting (Steyerberg, 2019; Collins et al., 2024).

2. What makes learning “supervised”?

The term supervised learning means that the algorithm learns from examples where the correct outcome is already known.

For each individual in the dataset, we observe:

Predictors + Outcome

For example:

Patient Age BMI HbA1c Blood pressure Diabetes status
1 52 31.2 6.8 142 Yes
2 39 24.8 5.1 118 No
3 61 29.4 7.2 151 Yes

Here, diabetes status is the known outcome. Age, BMI, HbA1c, and blood pressure are predictors.

The model uses these examples to learn patterns. Once trained, the model can be given a new patient’s predictors and return a predicted probability of diabetes.

This is different from unsupervised learning, where there is no labelled outcome. In unsupervised learning, the aim may be to identify clusters, reduce dimensionality, or discover structure in the data. Examples include clustering patients into subgroups or using principal component analysis to summarise high-dimensional omics data.

In medical statistics, both supervised and unsupervised learning are useful, but clinical prediction modelling is usually a supervised learning task.

3. Supervised learning in biostatistics

Supervised learning is central to many medical and biomedical prediction problems.

Examples include:

Medical question Outcome Predictors
Can we predict diabetes within 10 years? Diabetes yes/no Age, BMI, glucose, HbA1c, family history
Can we predict 30-day readmission? Readmission yes/no Diagnosis, comorbidities, admission history, labs
Can we predict cancer recurrence? Recurrence yes/no or time to recurrence Tumour stage, grade, treatment, biomarkers
Can we predict treatment response? Responder/non-responder Clinical characteristics, molecular markers
Can we predict survival? Time to death or relapse Age, disease stage, biomarkers, treatment
Can we classify disease status from methylation data? Case/control CpG methylation markers

These examples show that supervised learning is not a single method. It is a framework. The specific model depends on the outcome type, sample size, predictors, missing data, and scientific question.

A logistic regression model, random forest, XGBoost model, penalised Cox model, and neural network can all be supervised learning methods. The key feature is that they are trained using known outcomes.

4. Outcome variable

The outcome is the variable we want to predict.

In medical statistics, defining the outcome carefully is one of the most important steps. A vague or poorly defined outcome can make even a technically sophisticated model clinically meaningless.

Examples of outcomes include:

Outcome Type
Systolic blood pressure Continuous
Diabetes status Binary
Hospital readmission within 30 days Binary
Cancer subtype Multiclass
Treatment response Binary or ordinal
Time to death Time-to-event
Time to cancer recurrence Time-to-event
DNA methylation level at a CpG site Continuous

A good outcome definition should specify:

  • what event or measurement is being predicted;
  • how it is measured;
  • over what time horizon it is observed;
  • whether censoring is present;
  • whether the outcome is clinically meaningful;
  • whether the outcome is available consistently across patients.

For example, “mortality” is not specific enough. A better outcome definition would be:

All-cause mortality within 1 year of hospital admission.

Similarly, “readmission” should specify whether it means any readmission, emergency readmission, disease-specific readmission, and the time window such as 30 days or 90 days.

5. Predictors

Predictors are variables used to make predictions.

In medical datasets, predictors may include:

  • demographic characteristics;
  • symptoms;
  • clinical measurements;
  • laboratory values;
  • imaging features;
  • comorbidities;
  • medication history;
  • tumour characteristics;
  • genetic or molecular markers;
  • DNA methylation sites;
  • gene expression values;
  • healthcare utilisation history.

The most important rule is:

Predictors must be available at the intended prediction time.

For example, if we want to predict ICU mortality at ICU admission, we can use variables measured at or before ICU admission. We should not use ICU length of stay, discharge destination, or lab values measured after clinical deterioration because those variables would not be available at the time the prediction is made.

This rule is essential for avoiding data leakage.

6. Prediction time point

The prediction time point is the moment at which the model is intended to make a prediction.

This concept is often ignored in poor medical machine learning studies, but it is central to good biostatistical modelling.

Examples:

Prediction problem Prediction time point
Predict ICU mortality At ICU admission
Predict 30-day readmission At hospital discharge
Predict 5-year cancer survival At diagnosis or treatment start
Predict treatment response Before treatment begins
Predict relapse after surgery At surgery or shortly after surgery
Predict disease using methylation At sample collection

The prediction time point determines which predictors are legitimate. If a variable is measured after the prediction time point, it should usually not be used.

For example, suppose we want to predict 30-day hospital readmission at discharge. It may be reasonable to use length of stay because it is known at discharge. But if we were predicting deterioration at admission, length of stay would not be available and would cause leakage.

This is why clinical prediction models must clearly state when prediction is being made (Steyerberg, 2019; Collins et al., 2024).

7. Regression, classification, and survival prediction

Supervised learning problems are often grouped according to the outcome type.

Regression

Regression is used when the outcome is continuous.

Examples:

  • predicting blood pressure;
  • predicting cholesterol level;
  • predicting biomarker concentration;
  • predicting length of stay;
  • predicting methylation beta values.

A regression model produces a numerical prediction.

Example:

Predicted systolic blood pressure = 132 mmHg

Common regression methods include linear regression, ridge regression, lasso regression, regression trees, random forests, and gradient boosting.

Classification

Classification is used when the outcome is categorical.

Binary classification has two categories.

Examples:

  • disease yes/no;
  • death yes/no within a fixed period;
  • readmission yes/no;
  • responder/non-responder.

Multiclass classification has more than two categories.

Examples:

  • cancer subtype;
  • disease stage;
  • pathogen type;
  • low/intermediate/high risk group.

A classification model often produces a predicted probability for each class.

Example:

Predicted probability of disease = 0.72

The probability can then be converted into a class label using a threshold.

Survival prediction

Survival prediction is used when the outcome is time-to-event and censoring is present.

Examples:

  • time to death;
  • time to relapse;
  • time to recurrence;
  • time to disease progression;
  • time to hospital readmission.

Survival prediction differs from ordinary regression or classification because not every patient has complete follow-up. Some patients may be censored, meaning the event has not occurred by the end of follow-up or the patient is lost to follow-up.

Methods such as Cox proportional hazards models, penalised Cox models, random survival forests, and survival boosting are used for survival prediction.

Survival machine learning will be covered later in the course.

8. Binary classification in medical prediction

Binary classification is one of the most common supervised learning tasks in medical statistics.

Examples include:

Disease: yes/no
Death within 1 year: yes/no
Readmission within 30 days: yes/no
Treatment response: yes/no
Cancer recurrence within 5 years: yes/no

In binary classification, the model usually estimates a probability.

Example:

Predicted probability of 30-day readmission = 0.35

This means that, based on the model and available predictors, the patient is estimated to have a 35% probability of readmission within 30 days.

A threshold can be used to convert the predicted probability into a classification.

Example:

If predicted probability >= 0.50, classify as high risk.
If predicted probability < 0.50, classify as low risk.

However, the threshold of 0.50 is not automatically appropriate in medicine. The choice of threshold should depend on the clinical context, outcome prevalence, harms of false positives, harms of false negatives, and intended use of the model.

9. Train/test split

To estimate how well a model performs on new patients, we usually need to evaluate it on data that were not used to train it.

A simple approach is the train/test split.

Training set: used to fit the model
Test set: used to evaluate model performance

A common split is:

80% training
20% testing

The training data are used for model development. The test data are held back and should only be used for final evaluation.

This gives a more realistic estimate of how the model may perform on unseen patients.

However, the test set must be handled carefully. If we use the test set repeatedly to choose variables, tune hyperparameters, or decide between many models, it is no longer an honest test set.

10. Stratified splitting

In medical classification problems, outcomes are often imbalanced.

For example:

Disease prevalence = 10%
No disease = 90%

If we randomly split the data, it is possible that the training and test sets will have different outcome proportions, especially when the sample size is small.

Stratified splitting preserves the proportion of outcome classes in both the training and test sets.

For example, if 10% of patients have the outcome in the full dataset, stratified splitting aims to keep approximately 10% outcome cases in both the training and test datasets.

This is useful because model training and evaluation can be unstable when too few outcome cases appear in either set.

11. Logistic regression as a baseline model

Logistic regression is one of the most important models for binary clinical prediction.

Although it is a classical statistical model, it can also be understood as a supervised learning method because it learns a relationship between predictors and a binary outcome.

Logistic regression estimates:

P(Y = 1 | X)

This is the probability of the outcome given the predictors.

The model uses the logit link:

logit(p) = log(p / (1 - p))

where p is the probability of the outcome.

Logistic regression is often a strong baseline model in medical prediction because it is:

  • interpretable;
  • relatively stable;
  • familiar to clinicians and statisticians;
  • able to produce predicted probabilities;
  • suitable for tabular clinical data;
  • easy to validate and calibrate;
  • extendable through penalisation such as ridge, lasso, and elastic net.

Before using more complex models such as random forests or gradient boosting, it is usually sensible to compare them with logistic regression.

A complex model is not automatically better. If a simpler model performs similarly and is easier to interpret, validate, and communicate, it may be preferable in a medical setting.

12. From probabilities to classes

Many binary classifiers produce probabilities.

Example:

Patient Predicted probability of disease
A 0.82
B 0.61
C 0.44
D 0.12

To create class labels, we choose a threshold.

If the threshold is 0.50:

Patient Probability Predicted class
A 0.82 Disease
B 0.61 Disease
C 0.44 No disease
D 0.12 No disease

But in medicine, the threshold should be chosen carefully.

For screening, we may use a lower threshold to identify more possible cases. This increases sensitivity but may also increase false positives.

For treatment decisions with serious side effects, we may use a higher threshold to avoid overtreatment. This increases specificity but may miss some true cases.

Therefore, classification is not only a statistical problem. It is also a clinical decision problem.

13. Confusion matrix

A confusion matrix compares predicted classes with observed outcomes.

  Predicted positive Predicted negative
Actual positive True positive False negative
Actual negative False positive True negative

Definitions:

  • True positive: the model predicts positive and the outcome is positive.
  • False positive: the model predicts positive but the outcome is negative.
  • True negative: the model predicts negative and the outcome is negative.
  • False negative: the model predicts negative but the outcome is positive.

In medicine, the clinical consequences of false positives and false negatives are often very different.

For example, in cancer screening, a false negative may delay diagnosis. A false positive may cause anxiety and additional testing. Both matter, but they matter differently.

14. Accuracy

Accuracy is the proportion of all patients correctly classified.

Accuracy = (True positives + True negatives) / Total

Accuracy is simple, but it can be misleading.

Suppose a disease has prevalence 2%. A model that predicts “no disease” for everyone will be correct for 98% of patients.

Accuracy = 98%

This looks excellent, but the model detects no disease cases. It has no value as a screening tool.

This is why accuracy should rarely be the only metric reported in medical classification problems.

15. Sensitivity

Sensitivity is the proportion of actual positive cases correctly identified.

Sensitivity = True positives / (True positives + False negatives)

Sensitivity answers:

Among patients who truly have the condition, how many did the model detect?

High sensitivity is important when missing a case is harmful.

Examples include:

  • cancer screening;
  • sepsis alerts;
  • identifying high-risk ICU patients;
  • infectious disease screening;
  • detecting adverse drug reactions.

A model with low sensitivity may miss patients who need care.

16. Specificity

Specificity is the proportion of actual negative cases correctly identified.

Specificity = True negatives / (True negatives + False positives)

Specificity answers:

Among patients who truly do not have the condition, how many did the model correctly classify as negative?

High specificity is important when false positives have serious consequences.

Examples include:

  • avoiding unnecessary invasive tests;
  • avoiding unnecessary treatment;
  • preventing unnecessary anxiety;
  • avoiding inappropriate allocation of limited resources.

A model with low specificity may over-identify patients as high risk.

17. Positive predictive value

Positive predictive value, or PPV, is the proportion of predicted positives who truly have the outcome.

PPV = True positives / (True positives + False positives)

PPV answers:

Among patients predicted to be positive, how many truly have the outcome?

PPV is strongly affected by prevalence.

If the disease is rare, even a model with good sensitivity and specificity may have a low PPV. This is important in screening, where many positive predictions may be false positives if the condition is rare.

18. Negative predictive value

Negative predictive value, or NPV, is the proportion of predicted negatives who truly do not have the outcome.

NPV = True negatives / (True negatives + False negatives)

NPV answers:

Among patients predicted to be negative, how many truly do not have the outcome?

Like PPV, NPV depends on prevalence.

When a disease is rare, NPV may be high even for a weak model. Therefore, NPV should be interpreted alongside sensitivity, specificity, prevalence, and clinical context.

19. ROC curve and AUC

The ROC curve shows how sensitivity and specificity change across different classification thresholds.

It plots:

Sensitivity vs 1 - Specificity

The area under the ROC curve is called AUC.

AUC measures discrimination: the ability of the model to assign higher risk scores to patients who experience the outcome than to those who do not.

Interpretation:

AUC = 0.5: no discrimination
AUC = 1.0: perfect discrimination

AUC is useful, but it is not enough.

A model can have a good AUC but still be poorly calibrated. It may rank patients well but give risk probabilities that are too high or too low.

For clinical risk prediction, calibration must also be assessed.

20. Clinical interpretation of metrics

No single metric is best for all medical problems.

The choice of metric depends on the clinical purpose.

Clinical task Important considerations
Screening High sensitivity may be important
Confirmatory diagnosis High specificity may be important
Risk prediction Discrimination and calibration are both important
Rare disease prediction PPV and sensitivity need careful interpretation
Treatment decision-making Clinical utility and net benefit matter
Patient reassurance NPV may be important, but prevalence matters

The key point is that statistical metrics must be interpreted in relation to clinical consequences.

For example, a false negative in sepsis prediction may be more harmful than a false positive. But a false positive in a model recommending toxic treatment may also be harmful.

Medical prediction modelling requires both statistical evaluation and clinical judgement.

21. Common mistakes in supervised medical learning

Common mistakes include:

  1. Defining the outcome vaguely.
  2. Including predictors that are not available at prediction time.
  3. Splitting rows instead of patients when there are repeated records.
  4. Evaluating only training performance.
  5. Using accuracy alone in imbalanced data.
  6. Ignoring calibration.
  7. Choosing thresholds without clinical justification.
  8. Trying complex models without a simple baseline.
  9. Performing preprocessing before train/test splitting.
  10. Reporting results without enough detail for reproducibility.

These mistakes are common in medical machine learning studies and can lead to overly optimistic or clinically misleading conclusions.

22. Practical workflow for a first supervised learning analysis

A basic supervised learning workflow for a binary medical outcome should include:

  1. Define the clinical prediction question.
  2. Define the prediction time point.
  3. Define the outcome clearly.
  4. Identify candidate predictors available at prediction time.
  5. Explore missingness and data quality.
  6. Split data into training and test sets.
  7. Fit a baseline logistic regression model.
  8. Generate predicted probabilities.
  9. Choose or examine classification thresholds.
  10. Evaluate confusion matrix-based metrics.
  11. Evaluate discrimination using ROC AUC.
  12. Assess calibration.
  13. Interpret results clinically.
  14. Report limitations clearly.

This workflow will be used in the first applied R examples.

23. Summary

Supervised learning is the foundation of many medical machine learning applications.

In supervised learning, the model learns from examples where both predictors and outcomes are known. In biostatistics, this framework is commonly used for clinical prediction tasks such as disease classification, readmission prediction, treatment response prediction, cancer recurrence prediction, and survival prediction.

The most important point is that supervised learning in medicine is not just about fitting a model. It requires careful definition of the clinical question, outcome, prediction time point, and candidate predictors.

For binary classification, logistic regression is often a useful baseline model. Model performance should be interpreted using clinically meaningful metrics such as sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, ROC AUC, and calibration.

The goal is not simply to maximise accuracy. The goal is to develop models that are valid, interpretable, clinically useful, and generalisable to new patients.

Classification Metrics in Medicine

Learning objectives

By the end of this note, you should be able to:

  1. Explain why classification metrics are essential in medical machine learning.
  2. Interpret accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, and negative predictive value.
  3. Understand why accuracy can be misleading in imbalanced medical datasets.
  4. Explain how disease prevalence affects positive and negative predictive values.
  5. Interpret ROC curves and AUC in clinical prediction.
  6. Understand the difference between discrimination and calibration.
  7. Explain why threshold choice should depend on clinical context.
  8. Understand why one metric is never enough in medical prediction.
  9. Apply classification metrics to examples from clinical prediction, breast cancer research, and methylation-based biomedical prediction.

1. Introduction

After building a classification model, the next question is not simply:

Did the model run successfully?

The more important question is:

Is the model useful, reliable, and clinically meaningful?

In medical machine learning, classification models are often used to predict binary outcomes such as disease status, hospital readmission, treatment response, recurrence, or mortality within a fixed time period. These models usually produce either a predicted class or a predicted probability.

For example, a model may predict:

Disease: yes/no
Readmission within 30 days: yes/no
Treatment response: responder/non-responder
Cancer recurrence within 5 years: yes/no
Malignant tumour: yes/no
Exposure status: exposed/unexposed

To evaluate such models, we need performance metrics. However, in medicine, performance metrics must be interpreted carefully. A model with high accuracy may still be clinically useless. A model with high AUC may still give poorly calibrated probabilities. A model with high sensitivity may produce many false positives. A model with high specificity may miss many true cases.

This is why classification metrics in medicine are not just mathematical calculations. They are tools for understanding clinical trade-offs.

A good medical prediction model should be assessed using a combination of metrics, including discrimination, calibration, threshold-based performance, and clinical usefulness (Steyerberg, 2019; James et al., 2023).

2. Binary classification in medical prediction

In binary classification, the outcome has two categories.

Examples include:

Medical problem Positive class Negative class
Diabetes prediction Diabetes No diabetes
Cancer diagnosis Malignant Benign
Hospital readmission Readmitted Not readmitted
Treatment response Responder Non-responder
5-year recurrence Recurrence No recurrence
Exposure prediction from methylation Exposed Unexposed

The “positive” class does not always mean something good. In medical prediction, the positive class usually means the event or condition of interest.

For example:

Positive = disease present
Negative = disease absent

or:

Positive = cancer recurrence
Negative = no recurrence

Before calculating metrics, it is essential to define the positive class clearly. If the positive class is coded incorrectly, sensitivity, specificity, PPV, and NPV may be interpreted incorrectly.

3. Predicted probabilities and predicted classes

Many classification models produce predicted probabilities.

For example:

Patient Predicted probability of disease
A 0.82
B 0.61
C 0.44
D 0.12

These probabilities can be converted into predicted classes using a threshold.

If the threshold is 0.50:

Predicted probability ≥ 0.50 → positive
Predicted probability < 0.50 → negative

Then:

Patient Predicted probability Predicted class
A 0.82 Positive
B 0.61 Positive
C 0.44 Negative
D 0.12 Negative

However, a threshold of 0.50 is not automatically appropriate in medicine.

For some clinical problems, a lower threshold may be preferred because missing a true case is dangerous. For other problems, a higher threshold may be preferred because false positives may lead to unnecessary invasive testing or harmful treatment.

This means classification is not only a statistical problem. It is also a clinical decision problem.

4. The confusion matrix

Most threshold-based classification metrics are derived from the confusion matrix.

A confusion matrix compares the model’s predicted class with the observed outcome.

  Predicted positive Predicted negative
Actual positive True positive False negative
Actual negative False positive True negative

The four cells are:

  • True positive (TP): the model predicts positive and the patient truly has the outcome.
  • False positive (FP): the model predicts positive but the patient does not have the outcome.
  • True negative (TN): the model predicts negative and the patient truly does not have the outcome.
  • False negative (FN): the model predicts negative but the patient truly has the outcome.

In medicine, false positives and false negatives often have very different consequences.

For example, in cancer screening:

  • a false negative may delay diagnosis;
  • a false positive may cause anxiety, unnecessary imaging, biopsy, or follow-up.

Both errors matter, but they matter differently.

5. Accuracy

Accuracy is the proportion of all patients correctly classified.

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + FP + TN + FN)

Accuracy answers:

Out of all patients, how many did the model classify correctly?

For example:

TP = 30
FP = 10
TN = 50
FN = 10

Then:

Accuracy = (30 + 50) / (30 + 10 + 50 + 10)
         = 80 / 100
         = 0.80

So the model has 80% accuracy.

Accuracy is simple and easy to understand, but it can be misleading in medical datasets, especially when the outcome is rare.

6. Why accuracy can be misleading

Suppose a disease has prevalence 2%.

This means:

2% have disease
98% do not have disease

Now imagine a model that predicts “no disease” for every patient.

This model will be correct for 98% of patients.

Accuracy = 98%

At first glance, this sounds excellent. But the model detects no disease cases.

Its sensitivity is:

Sensitivity = 0%

This model is clinically useless for screening because it fails to identify every patient with disease.

This example shows why accuracy alone should not be used to evaluate medical classification models. In imbalanced datasets, high accuracy can simply reflect the majority class.

This is especially important in medicine because many clinically important outcomes are uncommon:

  • rare diseases;
  • hospital mortality;
  • severe adverse events;
  • treatment toxicity;
  • cancer recurrence in low-risk groups;
  • specific molecular subtypes.

In these settings, accuracy can give a false sense of model performance.

7. Sensitivity

Sensitivity is the proportion of actual positive cases correctly identified by the model.

Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN)

Sensitivity answers:

Among patients who truly have the outcome, how many did the model detect?

Sensitivity is also called:

True positive rate
Recall

For example:

TP = 30
FN = 10

Then:

Sensitivity = 30 / (30 + 10)
            = 30 / 40
            = 0.75

The model detects 75% of true positive cases.

When is sensitivity important?

Sensitivity is especially important when missing a case is harmful.

Examples:

  • cancer screening;
  • sepsis alerts;
  • identifying high-risk ICU patients;
  • infectious disease screening;
  • detecting serious adverse drug reactions;
  • identifying patients at high risk of suicide or self-harm;
  • screening for disease in pregnancy.

In these settings, a false negative may delay diagnosis or treatment. Therefore, a model with low sensitivity may be unacceptable even if its accuracy is high.

8. Specificity

Specificity is the proportion of actual negative cases correctly identified by the model.

Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)

Specificity answers:

Among patients who truly do not have the outcome, how many did the model correctly classify as negative?

Specificity is also called:

True negative rate

For example:

TN = 50
FP = 10

Then:

Specificity = 50 / (50 + 10)
            = 50 / 60
            = 0.833

The model correctly identifies 83.3% of true negative cases.

When is specificity important?

Specificity is especially important when false positives are harmful.

Examples:

  • avoiding unnecessary invasive diagnostic tests;
  • avoiding unnecessary chemotherapy or surgery;
  • avoiding unnecessary isolation or treatment;
  • avoiding patient anxiety;
  • avoiding overuse of limited healthcare resources.

For example, if a prediction model recommends a treatment with serious side effects, low specificity may lead to overtreatment.

9. Sensitivity-specificity trade-off

Sensitivity and specificity often move in opposite directions.

If we lower the classification threshold, more patients are classified as positive.

This usually:

increases sensitivity
decreases specificity

If we raise the classification threshold, fewer patients are classified as positive.

This usually:

decreases sensitivity
increases specificity

For example, suppose a model predicts probability of disease.

At threshold 0.30:

Sensitivity = 0.90
Specificity = 0.60

At threshold 0.70:

Sensitivity = 0.55
Specificity = 0.92

Neither threshold is automatically correct. The better threshold depends on the clinical purpose.

In screening, we may prefer the first threshold because we want to detect most true cases. In confirmatory decision-making, we may prefer the second threshold because we want fewer false positives.

10. Positive predictive value

Positive predictive value, or PPV, is the proportion of predicted positive cases who truly have the outcome.

PPV = TP / (TP + FP)

PPV answers:

Among patients predicted to be positive, how many truly have the outcome?

For example:

TP = 30
FP = 10

Then:

PPV = 30 / (30 + 10)
    = 30 / 40
    = 0.75

This means 75% of patients predicted positive truly have the outcome.

PPV is especially important when a positive prediction leads to action.

For example:

  • referral for further testing;
  • additional imaging;
  • biopsy;
  • treatment;
  • monitoring;
  • clinical review;
  • genetic counselling.

If PPV is low, many patients flagged by the model may not actually have the outcome.

11. Negative predictive value

Negative predictive value, or NPV, is the proportion of predicted negative cases who truly do not have the outcome.

NPV = TN / (TN + FN)

NPV answers:

Among patients predicted to be negative, how many truly do not have the outcome?

For example:

TN = 50
FN = 10

Then:

NPV = 50 / (50 + 10)
    = 50 / 60
    = 0.833

This means 83.3% of patients predicted negative truly do not have the outcome.

NPV is important when a negative prediction is used for reassurance or de-escalation.

Examples:

  • ruling out disease;
  • deciding not to refer;
  • reducing monitoring intensity;
  • avoiding unnecessary treatment.

However, NPV can be high simply because the disease is rare. Therefore, NPV should be interpreted alongside prevalence, sensitivity, and specificity.

12. Prevalence affects PPV and NPV

Sensitivity and specificity are often described as test characteristics. They are not completely fixed in all settings, but they are less directly dependent on prevalence than PPV and NPV.

PPV and NPV depend strongly on disease prevalence.

If disease prevalence is high, PPV tends to be higher.

If disease prevalence is low, PPV tends to be lower.

This is important in screening.

Suppose a disease is rare. Even if the model has good sensitivity and specificity, many positive predictions may be false positives because true cases are uncommon.

This is why a model may perform differently in different populations.

For example:

Population A: disease prevalence = 20%
Population B: disease prevalence = 2%

Using the same model, PPV will usually be higher in Population A than in Population B.

This matters when applying a prediction model to a new hospital, country, age group, or screening population. A model developed in a high-risk population may not have the same PPV in a lower-risk population.

13. Worked example: confusion matrix metrics

Suppose a model predicts disease status in 200 patients.

True positives  = 40
False positives = 20
True negatives  = 120
False negatives = 20

The confusion matrix is:

  Predicted positive Predicted negative
Actual positive 40 20
Actual negative 20 120

Accuracy

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / Total
         = (40 + 120) / 200
         = 160 / 200
         = 0.80

Sensitivity

Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN)
            = 40 / (40 + 20)
            = 40 / 60
            = 0.667

Specificity

Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)
            = 120 / (120 + 20)
            = 120 / 140
            = 0.857

PPV

PPV = TP / (TP + FP)
    = 40 / (40 + 20)
    = 40 / 60
    = 0.667

NPV

NPV = TN / (TN + FN)
    = 120 / (120 + 20)
    = 120 / 140
    = 0.857

This model is better at identifying negative cases than positive cases. Whether this is acceptable depends on the clinical task.

14. ROC curve

The ROC curve shows how sensitivity and specificity change across all possible classification thresholds.

It plots:

Sensitivity

against:

1 - Specificity

where 1 - Specificity is the false positive rate.

At each threshold, we get a different pair of sensitivity and specificity values.

The ROC curve summarises the model’s ability to rank patients by risk across thresholds.

A model with better discrimination will have a ROC curve closer to the top-left corner.

A model with no discrimination will lie near the diagonal line.

15. AUC

AUC means area under the ROC curve.

AUC is a measure of discrimination.

Discrimination asks:

Can the model assign higher predicted risks to patients who experience the outcome than to patients who do not?

Interpretation:

AUC = 0.5: no discrimination
AUC = 1.0: perfect discrimination

An AUC of 0.80 suggests that the model has reasonably good ability to rank patients by risk.

A common interpretation is:

If we randomly choose one patient who experienced the outcome and one patient who did not, the model will assign a higher predicted risk to the outcome patient 80% of the time.

AUC is useful, but it does not tell us everything.

16. Limitations of AUC

AUC has several limitations in medical prediction.

First, AUC does not measure calibration.

A model can have good AUC but produce predicted probabilities that are too high or too low.

For example:

Predicted risk = 40%
Observed risk = 15%

This model may rank patients well but overestimate absolute risk.

Second, AUC does not tell us which threshold should be used.

A model with AUC 0.85 may still perform poorly at the threshold that matters clinically.

Third, AUC can be insensitive to clinically important improvements. A new biomarker may improve decision-making in a high-risk subgroup but only slightly change the overall AUC.

Fourth, AUC may look acceptable even when the model is not clinically useful.

Therefore, AUC should be reported with other metrics such as calibration, sensitivity, specificity, predictive values, and clinical utility (Steyerberg, 2019).

17. Precision-recall curve

The precision-recall curve is another way to evaluate classification models, especially when the positive outcome is rare.

In this context:

Precision = PPV
Recall = Sensitivity

A precision-recall curve plots:

PPV against sensitivity

This can be more informative than the ROC curve when the outcome is rare.

For example, in rare disease prediction, the ROC AUC may look high even when the PPV is poor. The precision-recall curve focuses more directly on the performance among predicted positives.

This is useful when the main concern is:

Among patients flagged by the model, how many are truly positive?

Precision-recall curves are particularly relevant in:

  • rare disease screening;
  • adverse event prediction;
  • high-dimensional biomarker classification;
  • case-finding algorithms in electronic health records.

18. Threshold choice in medicine

A classification threshold converts predicted probabilities into predicted classes.

For example:

Predicted risk ≥ 0.20 → high risk
Predicted risk < 0.20 → low risk

The threshold should not be chosen only because it gives the highest accuracy.

Instead, threshold choice should reflect the clinical setting.

Questions to ask:

  1. What happens if we miss a true case?
  2. What happens if we falsely label someone high risk?
  3. Is the model used for screening, diagnosis, treatment, or monitoring?
  4. What is the cost of follow-up testing?
  5. Is the treatment harmful, expensive, or scarce?
  6. What risk level would justify clinical action?

For example, in a sepsis alert system, we may accept more false positives to avoid missing true cases. In a model recommending chemotherapy, false positives may be more harmful because treatment has serious side effects.

This is why threshold selection should involve clinical reasoning, not only statistical optimisation.

19. Discrimination versus calibration

Two central concepts in clinical prediction are:

Discrimination
Calibration

Discrimination

Discrimination asks:

Can the model separate patients who experience the outcome from those who do not?

Metrics include:

  • ROC AUC;
  • C-statistic;
  • sensitivity and specificity at selected thresholds.

Calibration

Calibration asks:

Do predicted probabilities agree with observed risks?

For example, if 100 patients are each predicted to have 20% risk, then approximately 20 of them should experience the outcome if the model is well calibrated.

A model can have good discrimination but poor calibration.

Example:

AUC = 0.85
Predicted average risk = 40%
Observed average risk = 15%

This model ranks patients well but overestimates risk.

In medicine, calibration is essential because clinical decisions often depend on absolute risk, not just ranking.

20. Brier score

The Brier score is a measure of prediction error for probabilistic predictions.

For binary outcomes:

Brier score = mean((predicted probability - observed outcome)^2)

The observed outcome is coded as:

1 = event occurred
0 = event did not occur

A lower Brier score is better.

A Brier score of 0 means perfect probability prediction.

Example:

Patient Predicted probability Observed outcome
A 0.80 1
B 0.60 1
C 0.30 0
D 0.10 0

These predictions are reasonably close to the observed outcomes.

The Brier score combines aspects of discrimination and calibration, but it should still be interpreted alongside other metrics.

21. Example from breast cancer survival work

Your breast cancer survival dashboard focused mainly on time-to-event outcomes using methods such as Kaplan-Meier curves, log-rank tests, Cox models, penalised Cox models, random survival forests, and calibration.

In survival analysis, the outcome is usually:

time to event

such as time to death or time to recurrence.

However, sometimes a survival question is converted into a binary classification question.

For example:

Did the patient experience recurrence within 5 years?
Yes/No

Then classification metrics may be used.

Suppose a model predicts 5-year recurrence in breast cancer patients.

Important metrics may include:

  • sensitivity: among patients who truly recur within 5 years, how many are identified as high risk?
  • specificity: among patients who do not recur, how many are correctly classified as low risk?
  • PPV: among patients predicted high risk, how many actually recur?
  • NPV: among patients predicted low risk, how many remain recurrence-free?
  • AUC: how well does the model rank patients by recurrence risk?
  • calibration: do predicted 5-year recurrence probabilities match observed recurrence risks?

But there is an important caution.

If follow-up times differ and censoring is present, ordinary binary classification metrics may be inappropriate or incomplete. A patient who has not recurred by 3 years may still recur by 5 years, but if follow-up ends at 3 years, their 5-year status is unknown.

In that case, survival-specific metrics such as the C-index, time-dependent AUC, and time-dependent Brier score may be more appropriate.

This connects with a key biostatistical principle:

The evaluation metric should match the outcome structure.

22. Example from methylation and EWAS-style work

Your current research interests include DNA methylation, epidemiology, high-dimensional biomedical data, and EWAS-style analysis.

In a traditional EWAS, the question may be:

Which CpG sites are associated with an exposure or outcome?

That is mainly an association or inference question.

A classification version of the problem might be:

Can methylation profiles classify disease status?

or:

Can methylation profiles predict exposure status?

For example:

Outcome: exposed vs unexposed
Predictors: selected CpG methylation markers

In this setting, classification metrics are useful, but there are several risks.

First, the number of predictors may be very large.

n = 800 participants
p = 450,000 CpG sites

This creates a serious risk of overfitting.

Second, feature selection can cause leakage if it is done before the train/test split or before cross-validation.

Third, the outcome may be imbalanced. For example, if only a small proportion of participants are exposed, accuracy may be misleading.

Fourth, PPV may be low when the positive class is rare.

Therefore, methylation-based classification should report more than accuracy. It should include sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, ROC AUC, calibration where probabilities are used, and ideally external validation in an independent cohort.

In high-dimensional biomedical prediction, careful validation is just as important as model choice.

23. Example: screening versus treatment decision

The same model may require different thresholds depending on the intended use.

Suppose a model predicts risk of a disease.

Screening use

If the model is used for screening, the goal may be to identify as many possible cases as possible.

In that case, high sensitivity may be prioritised.

A lower threshold may be chosen:

Predicted risk ≥ 0.10 → screen positive

This may increase false positives, but that may be acceptable if follow-up testing is safe and inexpensive.

Treatment decision use

If the model is used to decide whether to give a treatment with serious side effects, false positives may be more harmful.

A higher threshold may be chosen:

Predicted risk ≥ 0.40 → treat

This may reduce overtreatment but may miss some true cases.

The correct threshold depends on clinical consequences, not only statistical performance.

24. Balanced accuracy

Balanced accuracy is useful when the outcome classes are imbalanced.

It is the average of sensitivity and specificity:

Balanced accuracy = (Sensitivity + Specificity) / 2

For example, if:

Sensitivity = 0.70
Specificity = 0.90

then:

Balanced accuracy = (0.70 + 0.90) / 2
                  = 0.80

Balanced accuracy gives equal weight to positive and negative classes.

It can be more informative than ordinary accuracy when one class is much more common than the other.

However, balanced accuracy still does not replace clinical interpretation. A model with balanced accuracy of 0.80 may still be unacceptable if sensitivity is too low for the intended use.

25. F1 score

The F1 score combines precision and recall.

Precision = PPV
Recall = Sensitivity

The F1 score is:

F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

F1 score is commonly used in machine learning, especially when the positive class is important and imbalanced.

However, F1 score has limitations in medicine.

It ignores true negatives. This means it does not directly account for specificity. In some clinical settings, false positives and true negatives are very important.

Therefore, F1 score may be useful in some biomedical classification tasks, but it should not be the only metric reported.

26. Choosing metrics according to clinical purpose

The best metric depends on the clinical question.

Clinical purpose Metrics that may matter
Screening Sensitivity, NPV, PPV, burden of false positives
Diagnosis Sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV
Risk prediction AUC, calibration, Brier score, decision curve analysis
Treatment decision Calibration, net benefit, threshold-specific performance
Rare disease prediction PPV, sensitivity, precision-recall curve
Patient reassurance NPV, calibration, safety of false negatives
Resource allocation PPV, specificity, clinical utility

A model should not be judged only by the metric that looks best.

Instead, the evaluation should match the intended use.

27. Common mistakes when reporting classification metrics

Common mistakes include:

  1. Reporting accuracy alone.
  2. Ignoring class imbalance.
  3. Reporting AUC without calibration.
  4. Not defining the positive class.
  5. Choosing a threshold without explanation.
  6. Reporting sensitivity but not specificity.
  7. Reporting PPV and NPV without discussing prevalence.
  8. Using test data repeatedly to choose the best threshold.
  9. Ignoring confidence intervals around performance estimates.
  10. Evaluating row-level data when patient-level splitting is needed.
  11. Reporting only internal validation without external validation.
  12. Applying ordinary binary metrics to censored survival outcomes.

These mistakes can make a model appear more useful than it really is.

28. How to report classification performance

A clear report should include:

  • outcome definition;
  • positive class definition;
  • sample size;
  • number of events;
  • prevalence;
  • prediction time point;
  • validation approach;
  • classification threshold;
  • confusion matrix;
  • sensitivity;
  • specificity;
  • PPV;
  • NPV;
  • ROC AUC;
  • calibration assessment;
  • uncertainty intervals where possible;
  • clinical interpretation.

For clinical prediction models, transparent reporting is important because readers need to judge whether the model is valid and applicable to their setting (Collins et al., 2024).

29. Practical interpretation framework

When interpreting a classification model, ask:

  1. What is the clinical question?
  2. What is the positive class?
  3. What is the prediction time point?
  4. What is the outcome prevalence?
  5. Is the dataset imbalanced?
  6. What threshold was used?
  7. Is sensitivity high enough for the intended use?
  8. Is specificity high enough for the intended use?
  9. Is PPV acceptable?
  10. Is NPV acceptable?
  11. Does the model discriminate well?
  12. Is the model calibrated?
  13. Would the model change clinical decisions?
  14. Was the model validated properly?
  15. Could performance be inflated by leakage?

This framework is more useful than asking only whether accuracy or AUC is high.

30. Summary

Classification metrics are central to evaluating medical machine learning models, but they must be interpreted carefully.

Accuracy is easy to understand but can be misleading when outcomes are imbalanced. Sensitivity and specificity describe how well the model identifies true positives and true negatives. PPV and NPV describe how trustworthy positive and negative predictions are, but they depend strongly on prevalence.

ROC AUC measures discrimination, but it does not measure calibration or clinical usefulness. Precision-recall curves may be helpful for rare outcomes. Threshold choice should depend on clinical context and the consequences of false positives and false negatives.

For your own areas of interest, these ideas are directly relevant. In breast cancer prediction, classification metrics may be useful for fixed-time recurrence prediction, but survival-specific metrics are needed when censoring is present. In methylation and EWAS-style prediction, class imbalance, high dimensionality, feature-selection leakage, and external validation are major concerns.

The main message is:

In medical machine learning, model performance should be judged by a combination of metrics that reflect the clinical purpose, outcome structure, prevalence, validation design, calibration, and consequences of decision-making.

Decision Curve Analysis and Clinical Usefulness

Learning objectives

By the end of this note, you should be able to:

  1. Explain why good model performance does not automatically imply clinical usefulness.
  2. Define threshold probability.
  3. Explain the purpose of decision curve analysis.
  4. Describe treat-all, treat-none, and model-based strategies.
  5. Explain the idea of net benefit.
  6. Understand how false positives and false negatives are weighted in decision-making.
  7. Recognise why decision curve analysis requires a clear clinical decision.
  8. Interpret decision curve analysis in medical prediction studies.

1. Why this note matters

A prediction model can have good statistical performance but still be clinically unhelpful.

For example, a model may have:

AUC = 0.82
Good calibration
Acceptable sensitivity and specificity

This sounds promising. But it does not automatically mean that using the model will improve patient care.

A clinical prediction model should support a decision. Decision curve analysis helps evaluate whether using a model improves decisions compared with simpler strategies.

The practical message of this note is:

A prediction model is clinically useful only if it improves decisions at relevant risk thresholds.

2. From prediction to decision

Many prediction models estimate risk.

For example:

Predicted diabetes risk = 25%
Predicted cancer recurrence risk = 18%
Predicted readmission risk = 30%

But risk prediction alone is not the final goal.

The question is what should be done with the prediction.

Possible actions include:

  • further testing;
  • referral;
  • treatment;
  • closer monitoring;
  • reassurance;
  • follow-up planning.

Decision curve analysis links predicted risk to clinical action.

3. The clinical decision

Before using decision curve analysis, define the decision clearly.

Examples:

Should this patient receive further diabetes testing?

Should this patient receive additional cancer follow-up?

Should this patient be referred to a specialist?

Without a clear decision, decision curve analysis becomes difficult to interpret.

A decision curve is not just a statistical plot. It is a way of asking whether model-based decisions are better than simple alternatives.

4. Threshold probability

A threshold probability is the risk level at which action is taken.

For example:

Refer for further testing if predicted risk ≥ 20%

Here, 20% is the threshold probability.

The threshold reflects the point at which the expected benefit of action is judged to outweigh the potential harm, cost, or burden.

5. Why threshold probability matters

Different clinical actions have different thresholds.

A low threshold may be reasonable when:

  • the action is safe;
  • the action is cheap;
  • the test is non-invasive;
  • missing disease is harmful.

A high threshold may be reasonable when:

  • treatment is risky;
  • treatment is expensive;
  • false positives cause harm;
  • the intervention is invasive.

Therefore, the same model may be useful at some thresholds but not others.

6. Treat-all strategy

The treat-all strategy means every patient receives the action.

For example:

Test every patient.

This may be reasonable when the test is cheap and safe, or when the outcome is common.

However, it may lead to unnecessary testing, anxiety, cost, or overtreatment.

7. Treat-none strategy

The treat-none strategy means no patient receives the action.

For example:

Test no patients.

This may be reasonable when the outcome is rare, the action is harmful, or the cost is high.

However, it may miss patients who could benefit from intervention.

8. Model-based strategy

The model-based strategy uses predicted risk.

For example:

Test patients if predicted risk ≥ 20%.

This strategy is useful only if it improves the balance between detecting true cases and avoiding unnecessary action.

Decision curve analysis compares model-based decisions with treat-all and treat-none strategies.

9. Net benefit

Decision curve analysis uses a measure called net benefit.

Net benefit combines:

  • true positives;
  • false positives;
  • threshold probability.

It rewards correct identification of patients who should receive action and penalises unnecessary action in patients who do not need it.

The exact formula is less important at this stage than the interpretation:

Higher net benefit = better decision strategy

10. Intuition behind net benefit

A true positive is beneficial because the model correctly identifies a patient who may need action.

A false positive is harmful because the model recommends action for a patient who does not need it.

The threshold probability determines how much false positives are penalised.

At a low threshold, false positives are penalised less.

At a high threshold, false positives are penalised more.

This reflects clinical judgement about the relative harm of false positives and false negatives.

11. Medical example: diabetes testing

Suppose a diabetes model predicts risk.

The clinic considers further testing if risk is at least 20%.

Possible strategies are:

Test everyone
Test nobody
Test only if predicted risk ≥ 20%

Decision curve analysis asks whether the model-based strategy gives higher net benefit than testing everyone or testing nobody.

If it does, the model may be clinically useful at that threshold.

12. Medical example: cancer follow-up

Suppose a model predicts cancer recurrence risk.

A clinician may consider intensive follow-up if predicted recurrence risk is high.

However, intensive follow-up may involve cost, anxiety, imaging, and clinic visits.

Decision curve analysis can help assess whether the model improves decisions across plausible thresholds for additional follow-up.

13. Why AUC is not enough

AUC measures discrimination.

It asks:

Does the model rank patients with the outcome above patients without the outcome?

But AUC does not tell us whether using the model improves decisions.

A model can have good AUC and still be clinically unhelpful if it does not improve net benefit at relevant thresholds.

14. Why calibration is still important

Decision curve analysis uses predicted probabilities.

If the model is poorly calibrated, threshold-based decisions may be misleading.

For example, if a model overestimates risk, too many patients may be placed above the treatment threshold.

If it underestimates risk, high-risk patients may be missed.

Therefore, calibration should be assessed before interpreting decision curves.

15. Relevant threshold range

Decision curve analysis should focus on clinically relevant thresholds.

For example, if clinicians would only act between 10% and 30% predicted risk, then model usefulness outside this range may be less relevant.

A decision curve should not be interpreted without asking:

Are these thresholds realistic for the clinical decision?

16. Interpreting a decision curve

A model is potentially useful when its net benefit is higher than both:

  • treat-all;
  • treat-none.

This should be true across thresholds that make clinical sense.

If a model has higher net benefit only at unrealistic thresholds, it may not be useful in practice.

17. Clinical usefulness depends on context

The same model may be useful in one setting but not another.

For example, a diabetes risk model may be useful for deciding who should receive a low-cost blood test.

The same model may not be sufficient for deciding who should start a medication with side effects.

Clinical usefulness depends on:

  • the decision;
  • the intervention;
  • harms and benefits;
  • disease prevalence;
  • patient preferences;
  • healthcare resources.

18. Common mistakes

18.1 Assuming high AUC means clinical usefulness

High AUC does not guarantee improved decisions.

18.2 Using decision curve analysis without a clear decision

The method requires a defined action.

18.3 Ignoring threshold relevance

The threshold range must be clinically realistic.

18.4 Ignoring calibration

Poor calibration can make threshold-based decisions unreliable.

18.5 Overclaiming clinical value

Decision curve analysis can support usefulness, but it does not prove real-world implementation benefit by itself.

19. Reporting decision curve analysis

A clear report should describe:

  • target population;
  • prediction model;
  • clinical decision;
  • action triggered by the model;
  • threshold probability range;
  • reason the threshold range is clinically relevant;
  • comparison strategies;
  • validation dataset;
  • interpretation of net benefit.

This makes the decision curve clinically interpretable.

20. Summary

Decision curve analysis evaluates clinical usefulness. It asks whether using a model improves decisions compared with treating everyone or treating no one.

It is useful because good statistical performance does not automatically mean clinical value.

A decision curve should always be interpreted in context: what decision is being made, what threshold range is realistic, what harms are involved, and whether the model has been validated.

Key terms

Term Meaning
Decision curve analysis Method for evaluating clinical usefulness of prediction models
Threshold probability Risk level at which clinical action is taken
Treat all Strategy where every patient receives the action
Treat none Strategy where no patient receives the action
Model-based strategy Strategy where action depends on predicted risk

False positive Patient incorrectly classified as needing action

Key takeaway

Decision curve analysis connects prediction modelling to clinical action. A model is not useful simply because it has good AUC or calibration; it is useful when it improves decisions at clinically meaningful risk thresholds.

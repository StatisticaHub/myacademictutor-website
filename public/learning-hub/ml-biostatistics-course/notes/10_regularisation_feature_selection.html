Regularisation and Feature Selection in Biomedical Prediction

Learning objectives

By the end of this note, you should be able to:

  1. Explain why many predictors increase the risk of overfitting.
  2. Describe why regularisation is useful in medical and biomedical prediction.
  3. Explain the basic idea of ridge regression.
  4. Explain the basic idea of lasso regression.
  5. Explain why elastic net can be useful for correlated predictors.
  6. Distinguish prediction-based feature selection from causal interpretation.
  7. Recognise how feature selection can cause data leakage.
  8. Understand why selected features should be validated and interpreted cautiously.

1. Why this note matters

Medical and biomedical datasets often contain many possible predictors.

Examples include:

  • age, sex, and clinical history;
  • blood test results;
  • imaging features;
  • genetic variants;
  • gene expression measurements;
  • DNA methylation sites;
  • proteomics and metabolomics markers;
  • electronic health record variables.

When the number of predictors is large compared with the number of patients or outcome events, ordinary modelling can become unstable.

The model may fit random noise instead of real signal.

Regularisation helps control this problem.

The practical message of this note is:

Regularisation can reduce overfitting in high-dimensional prediction, but feature selection must be validated and interpreted cautiously.

2. The problem of too many predictors

Suppose we have:

150 patients
500 candidate predictors

A model may find apparent patterns that look strong in the development data.

However, many of these patterns may be accidental.

This is especially important when the outcome is rare.

For example:

150 patients
30 outcome events
500 predictors

There are many more possible predictor patterns than outcome events.

Without careful modelling, overfitting is likely.

3. High-dimensional biomedical data

Biomedical datasets can be extremely high-dimensional.

Examples include:

Gene expression: thousands of genes
DNA methylation: hundreds of thousands of CpG sites
Genetics: thousands or millions of variants
Imaging: many extracted features
Electronic health records: many coded variables

In these settings, standard regression models may not work well without constraints.

Regularisation provides one way to handle many predictors.

4. What is regularisation?

Regularisation adds a penalty to the model-fitting process.

Instead of only fitting the data as closely as possible, the model is encouraged to be simpler or more stable.

The penalty discourages overly large coefficients.

This can improve performance on new data.

Regularisation is useful when:

  • there are many predictors;
  • predictors are correlated;
  • sample size is limited;
  • overfitting risk is high;
  • prediction stability is important.

5. Ridge regression

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients towards zero.

It usually keeps all predictors in the model, but reduces their influence.

Ridge is useful when many predictors may each contain a small amount of information.

It is also useful when predictors are correlated.

For example, many biomarkers may be related to the same biological pathway. Ridge can stabilise coefficient estimates in this situation.

6. Intuition for ridge regression

Imagine a model with many predictors.

Without regularisation, some predictors may receive large coefficients because of random noise.

Ridge regression reduces this by shrinking coefficients.

This makes the model less sensitive to small changes in the training data.

In medical prediction, this stability can be valuable.

7. Lasso regression

Lasso regression also shrinks coefficients.

However, lasso can shrink some coefficients exactly to zero.

This means lasso can perform feature selection.

Predictors with zero coefficients are effectively removed from the model.

This can make the model simpler and easier to describe.

8. Intuition for lasso regression

Lasso tries to find a smaller subset of predictors that helps prediction.

For example, from 100 candidate predictors, lasso may select 12.

This can be attractive when we want a more compact model.

However, selected predictors should not automatically be treated as clinically important or causal.

Selection can be unstable, especially when predictors are correlated.

9. Elastic net

Elastic net combines ridge and lasso penalties.

It can shrink coefficients and perform variable selection.

Elastic net is often useful when predictors are correlated.

This is common in:

  • gene expression data;
  • methylation data;
  • imaging features;
  • related clinical measurements;
  • electronic health record variables.

Elastic net can be more stable than lasso alone when groups of predictors are strongly correlated.

10. Comparing ridge, lasso, and elastic net

Method Main behaviour Feature selection?
Ridge Shrinks coefficients towards zero Usually no
Lasso Shrinks some coefficients to zero Yes
Elastic net Combines ridge and lasso behaviour Yes, with more stability for correlated predictors

There is no universally best method.

The choice depends on the data, the prediction task, the sample size, and validation performance.

11. Feature selection

Feature selection means choosing a subset of predictors.

Feature selection may be based on:

  • clinical reasoning;
  • previous literature;
  • statistical association;
  • prediction performance;
  • regularisation;
  • measurement quality;
  • availability in practice.

In medical prediction, feature selection should not be purely mechanical.

A predictor should also make sense in the intended clinical workflow.

12. Clinical versus data-driven feature selection

A purely data-driven approach may select predictors that do not generalise.

A purely clinical approach may miss useful predictive information.

A sensible approach often combines:

  • clinical knowledge;
  • biological plausibility;
  • data quality;
  • statistical modelling;
  • internal validation;
  • external validation.

This balance is especially important in biomedical prediction, where many features may be noisy or correlated.

13. Feature selection and data leakage

Feature selection can easily create data leakage.

An incorrect workflow is:

1. Use the full dataset to select predictors.
2. Split data into training and test sets.
3. Fit the model using selected predictors.
4. Evaluate performance on the test set.

This is wrong because the test set influenced predictor selection.

The test set is no longer independent.

Performance may be too optimistic.

14. Correct feature selection workflow

A safer workflow is:

1. Split data into training and test sets.
2. Select features using only the training set.
3. Fit the model using selected features.
4. Evaluate once on the untouched test set.

For cross-validation:

Feature selection must happen inside each training fold.

The validation fold should not influence which features are selected.

15. Prediction versus causation

A selected feature is not automatically causal.

A lasso model may select a predictor because it helps prediction.

This does not mean the predictor causes the outcome.

A selected feature may be:

  • biologically relevant;
  • correlated with a causal factor;
  • a proxy for another variable;
  • selected due to noise;
  • affected by measurement artefacts;
  • specific to one dataset.

Prediction and causal explanation are different goals.

16. Medical example: methylation prediction

Suppose we use DNA methylation data to predict exposure status.

The dataset contains:

300 participants
450,000 CpG sites

This is a high-dimensional problem.

A standard logistic regression model cannot handle all predictors directly.

Regularisation methods such as ridge, lasso, or elastic net may help build a prediction model.

However, selected CpG sites should be interpreted cautiously.

They may be predictive markers, not causal effects.

17. Medical example: cancer recurrence

Suppose we predict breast cancer recurrence using:

  • age;
  • tumour stage;
  • tumour grade;
  • receptor status;
  • treatment information;
  • biomarkers;
  • gene expression features.

If the number of predictors is large and the number of recurrence events is small, overfitting is likely.

Regularisation may improve stability.

However, the final model still needs:

  • internal validation;
  • calibration assessment;
  • external validation;
  • clinical usefulness evaluation.

18. Choosing the penalty strength

Regularised models require a tuning parameter that controls the amount of shrinkage.

If the penalty is too weak, the model may overfit.

If the penalty is too strong, the model may underfit.

Cross-validation is commonly used to choose the penalty strength.

However, this tuning process must be included properly within the validation workflow.

19. Stability of selected features

Selected features may change across samples.

For example, one bootstrap sample may select one biomarker, while another sample selects a different but correlated biomarker.

This does not necessarily mean the method is wrong.

It means that feature selection can be unstable when predictors are correlated or sample size is limited.

Feature stability should be assessed before making strong biological claims.

20. Interpreting selected features

Before claiming that a selected feature is important, ask:

  • Is it biologically plausible?
  • Is it selected consistently across resamples?
  • Does it improve validated prediction performance?
  • Is it measured reliably?
  • Is it available in clinical practice?
  • Does it validate externally?
  • Could it be a proxy for another variable?

This is especially important in omics and biomarker research.

21. Model simplicity versus performance

A simpler model is often easier to implement and explain.

However, a simpler model is not automatically better.

A complex model is not automatically worse.

The goal is to balance:

  • predictive performance;
  • calibration;
  • interpretability;
  • stability;
  • feasibility;
  • reproducibility;
  • clinical usefulness.

22. Regularisation and calibration

Regularisation can reduce overfitting, which may improve calibration.

However, regularised models can still be miscalibrated.

Therefore, after fitting a regularised model, we should still assess:

  • AUC;
  • calibration plot;
  • calibration slope;
  • calibration intercept;
  • Brier score;
  • clinical usefulness.

Regularisation is not a substitute for validation.

23. Common mistakes

23.1 Too many predictors for too few events

This increases overfitting risk.

23.2 Feature selection before validation

This causes leakage.

23.3 Treating selected predictors as causal

Prediction does not prove causation.

23.4 Ignoring correlated predictors

Correlation can make lasso selection unstable.

23.5 Reporting selected features without validation

Selected features may not generalise.

23.6 Assuming regularisation solves everything

Regularisation helps, but validation and calibration are still essential.

24. Reporting regularised models

A clear report should describe:

  • candidate predictors;
  • sample size;
  • number of outcome events;
  • missing data handling;
  • regularisation method;
  • tuning process;
  • validation method;
  • selected predictors, if applicable;
  • discrimination;
  • calibration;
  • limitations;
  • clinical interpretation.

Transparency is important because regularised models can be misunderstood, especially when feature selection is involved.

25. Summary

Regularisation is useful when there are many predictors and limited data. It reduces overfitting by shrinking coefficients and controlling model complexity.

Ridge regression shrinks coefficients but usually keeps all predictors.

Lasso can shrink some coefficients to zero and perform feature selection.

Elastic net combines ridge and lasso and is often useful when predictors are correlated.

Feature selection should be interpreted carefully. A selected feature may improve prediction without being causal or clinically important.

Key takeaway

Regularisation can make prediction models more stable when there are many predictors, but it does not remove the need for careful validation. Selected features should be interpreted as prediction features first, not automatically as causal or clinically proven factors.
